# What Is This Thing Called Knowledge?, Duncan Pritchard

## Thesis
Knowledge is a special kind of cognitive achievement, and clarifying it means asking precisely what separates it from lucky guessing, mere true opinion, and well-supported error. Pritchard argues that careful epistemology works by building candidate definitions, stress-testing them against sharp counterexamples, and revising until the account survives. The method is itself a thinking tool: progress comes from holding clear cases up against clear principles and letting each correct the other.

## Thinking tools

1. **Pull apart belief, truth, and justification.** Treat any knowledge claim as having three separable parts: that someone holds the belief, that the belief is true, and that they have good grounds for it. When you assess a claim, check each part on its own. A person can be right by accident (true belief, no grounds), or sincerely justified yet wrong (good grounds, false belief). Isolating the three components shows you exactly where a claim is weak rather than letting a vague sense of "they know it" paper over the gap.

2. **Demand more than true belief.** A correct guess is not knowledge, so true belief alone cannot be the standard. Apply this by asking whether the person would still have gotten it right if circumstances had shifted slightly. If their being correct traces to luck rather than to their handling of evidence, downgrade it from knowledge to opinion that happened to land. This is the working razor that forces every account of knowledge to add something beyond truth.

3. **Run the Gettier test.** Construct a case where a belief is true and well justified yet the truth is reached by a lucky detour the reasoner never saw. Apply it by imagining: could the justification have pointed at the right answer for the wrong reason, with reality quietly cooperating? If the answer is yes, justified true belief is present but knowledge is absent. Use these cases as the standard probe for any proposed definition: a definition that still counts the lucky case as knowledge has failed.

4. **Separate the two faces of luck.** Distinguish luck in how you formed the belief from luck in whether the world happened to match it. Apply this by tracing where the chance entered: did the person reason sloppily and get bailed out, or did they reason well while an unseen accident aligned the facts? Naming which kind of luck is operating tells you whether to blame the thinker or the situation, and it explains why some lucky-but-true beliefs feel closer to knowledge than others.

5. **Ask whether the belief tracks the truth.** Test a belief counterfactually: if the claim were false, would the person have stopped believing it? And in nearby situations where it stays true, would they still believe it? Apply this by mentally varying the facts and watching whether the belief moves with them. A belief that would persist even when false is not sensitive to reality and earns little credit. Tracking gives you a concrete mechanical check in place of a vague feeling of reliability.

6. **Locate the source of credit: the knower or the process.** When someone gets something right, ask whether the success is properly creditable to their own competence or merely to a dependable process running through them. Apply this by asking: did they exercise a skill, or did they get carried by a reliable mechanism they neither understood nor controlled? This separates genuine cognitive achievement from passive good fortune and clarifies why we praise some true beliefs as accomplishments and treat others as mere outputs.

7. **Choose your starting point honestly: cases or criteria.** Before deciding what counts as knowledge, notice the circular trap: to sort good cases you need a criterion, but to pick a criterion you seem to need known cases. Apply this by deciding deliberately which end you trust more. Start from clear examples and generalize (particularism), or start from a rule and let it judge examples (methodism). Making the choice explicit stops you from smuggling in an unexamined standard while pretending to discover one.

8. **Decide where justification bottoms out.** When you ask "how do you know that?" the answers form a chain, and you must choose how the chain ends. Apply this by testing each option: does support rest on basic beliefs that need no further backing (foundations), or on beliefs that mutually support one another in a web (coherence)? Pushing any justification toward its base reveals whether it stands on something solid or floats on a loop, and shows you the real structure holding a claim up.

9. **Match the test to the source.** Knowledge arrives through different channels, and each has its own failure mode: perception can misrepresent, memory can fade or rewrite, testimony can pass along error, and inference can overreach. Apply this by first identifying the channel a claim came through, then applying the check that channel actually needs. You do not interrogate a memory the way you interrogate a stranger's testimony. Tailoring scrutiny to the source catches the errors that a one-size check would miss.

10. **Confront the problem of induction directly.** Notice that every inference from past patterns to future cases assumes the future will resemble the past, and that assumption cannot itself be proved without using induction again. Apply this by separating two questions: is the inference logically guaranteed (no), and is it nonetheless reasonable to rely on (a separate matter)? Holding these apart keeps you from either overclaiming certainty about predictions or sliding into the paralysis of treating all expectation as groundless.

11. **Take the sceptic's argument apart valve by valve.** A sceptical argument is usually a tidy chain: you cannot rule out a deceiving scenario, so you cannot know the ordinary claim. Apply this by laying the chain out as explicit premises and asking which single link you reject and what it costs to reject it. The point is not to feel defeated by doubt but to find exactly which step does the damaging work, because that is where any answer must intervene.

12. **Watch how standards shift with context.** Whether a claim counts as known can depend on how demanding the situation is: casual settings accept loose grounds, courtrooms and philosophy seminars raise the bar. Apply this by asking what standard is in force here and whether a claim that passes everyday scrutiny would survive a stricter setting. Tracking the moving standard explains why people seem to both know and not know the same thing, and it stops a debate from stalling on a hidden disagreement about how strict to be.

13. **Ask why knowledge is worth more than true belief.** Press on the value question: if a true belief already gets you the right answer, what extra good does knowing add? Apply this by comparing two people who both believe the truth, one by luck and one by competence, and asking what the second has that the first lacks. The exercise forces you to say what knowledge is for, and a definition that cannot explain why knowledge is more valuable than a lucky hit is probably missing something real.

14. **Use clear examples as instruments rather than decoration.** A sharp, simple case (a stopped clock, a barn that may be a facade, a thermometer reader who trusts a gauge) is a measuring device for a theory. Apply this by building the smallest vivid scenario that isolates exactly one variable, then running every competing account against it and noting which verdicts feel forced. Concrete cases convert abstract disagreement into a question you can actually answer.

15. **Revise the definition, do not defend it to the death.** Treat each proposed account of knowledge as a draft. Apply this loop: state the definition, find the case that breaks it, diagnose what the case exposes, amend the definition, then hunt for the next breaking case. The goal is steady improvement under pressure, and the willingness to abandon a cherished account when a clean counterexample defeats it is what separates inquiry from advocacy.

## Named concepts, biases, fallacies, and techniques
- **Justified true belief (JTB):** The classical proposal that knowing something means believing it, being right, and having good grounds.
- **Gettier case:** A scenario where justified true belief is present yet the truth is reached by luck, showing JTB is not enough.
- **Epistemic luck:** Good fortune that makes a belief true in a way that undermines its status as knowledge.
- **Veritic luck:** Luck specifically in whether the world matches the belief, the kind most corrosive to knowledge.
- **Truth-tracking (sensitivity):** The condition that one would not hold the belief if it were false.
- **Safety:** The condition that the belief could not easily have been false while still held.
- **Reliabilism:** The view that what turns true belief into knowledge is its coming from a dependable belief-forming process.
- **Virtue epistemology:** The view that knowledge is true belief creditable to the knower's intellectual character or competence.
- **The problem of the criterion:** The circular bind between needing known cases to find a standard and needing a standard to identify known cases.
- **Methodism:** Starting inquiry from a criterion and judging cases by it.
- **Particularism:** Starting inquiry from clear cases and extracting the criterion from them.
- **Foundationalism:** The structure in which justification rests on basic beliefs needing no further support.
- **Coherentism:** The structure in which beliefs justify one another as a mutually supporting web.
- **The regress of justification:** The threat that every reason needs a further reason, with no natural stopping point.
- **The problem of induction:** The point that projecting past patterns into the future cannot be justified without assuming what it sets out to prove.
- **Radical scepticism:** The argument that we cannot rule out deceiving scenarios and therefore cannot have ordinary knowledge.
- **Closure principle:** The idea that knowing one thing and knowing it implies another lets you know the second; sceptics exploit it.
- **Contextualism:** The view that the standards for "knows" rise and fall with conversational context.
- **Internalism vs. externalism:** The dispute over whether the grounds for a belief must be accessible to the believer or can lie outside their awareness.
- **Closure-based sceptical argument:** The chain that runs from inability to rule out deception to inability to know everyday claims.

## What this book uniquely contributes
The book teaches epistemology as a craft of pressure-testing rather than as a list of doctrines. Its lasting gift is a reusable method: define, find the breaking case, diagnose, revise, repeat, with vivid examples used as instruments to settle disputes that would otherwise stay verbal. By keeping the value-of-knowledge question and the luck question in front of the reader the whole way through, it gives a steady criterion for judging any theory: a good account must explain both why luck destroys knowledge and why knowledge is worth more than being accidentally right. That combination of an explicit working method and two anchoring questions is what makes it a thinking toolkit and more than just a survey.

## Memorable principles and examples (paraphrased)
1. **The stopped clock.** You glance at a clock that happens to read the correct time, though it stopped exactly twelve hours ago. Your belief about the time is true and seems reasonable, yet you clearly do not know it, because being right here is pure luck. This is the cleanest demonstration that justified true belief can still fall short.
2. **Right answer, wrong road.** Imagine reasoning carefully from solid evidence to a conclusion that turns out true, but true for a reason entirely different from the one your evidence pointed at. The world quietly cooperated behind your back. Your justification did real work, your belief is correct, and still you did not know, because the link between your reasons and the truth was an accident.
3. **The barn facades.** You see what looks exactly like a barn and believe there is a barn there, and there is. Unknown to you, the area is full of convincing barn facades and you happened to look at the one real barn. The same perception that delivered truth would have delivered falsehood almost anywhere nearby, which is why the belief feels too lucky to count as knowledge.
4. **The doubt you cannot disprove.** You cannot strictly rule out that all your experience is being fed to you by some grand deception. The sceptic uses this to argue you know nothing about the outside world. The instructive move is not despair but dissection: lay the argument out, find the one premise you can reasonably deny, and pay the price for denying it, which is where every serious reply lives.
5. **Tomorrow will resemble today.** Every prediction leans on the assumption that nature keeps behaving as it has, yet that assumption can only be supported by appeal to how reliably it has held so far, which already uses the rule in question. The lesson is to separate "logically guaranteed" from "reasonable to rely on" and to stop demanding that good inference also be airtight proof.
