# Against Method, Paul Feyerabend

## Thesis
Feyerabend argues that there is no fixed set of rules, no single "scientific method," that has held across the actual history of inquiry and that would have helped rather than hindered progress. Every methodological rule worth having has been broken, deliberately and fruitfully, by the very scientists we most admire. The healthiest stance toward method is therefore opportunistic and pluralistic: keep many incompatible ideas alive, switch tactics as the situation demands, and treat "anything goes" as the honest summary of what history shows once you stop forcing it through a rationalist filter.

## Thinking tools

1. **Test methods against real history rather than idealized reconstructions.** Philosophers of science usually defend a rule by inventing a clean cartoon of how a discovery "must have" happened. Feyerabend's move is to go to the messy archival record of episodes like Galileo, Brownian motion, or the Copernican turn and ask whether the rule actually operated there. Apply it by treating any proposed standard ("always test against evidence," "never hold a refuted theory") as an empirical hypothesis about practice, then hunting for the famous case that violates it. If the giants broke the rule, the rule cannot be a precondition of good work.

2. **Counterinduction: build theories that clash with well-confirmed facts.** Standard advice says invent theories consistent with the established observations. Feyerabend recommends the reverse as a deliberate tactic: propose hypotheses that contradict accepted results and entrenched theories. The reason is that some evidence against a view only becomes visible once you have an alternative that predicts different things. Apply it by intentionally developing the rival no one is funding, using it as an instrument to expose assumptions baked into the reigning view that would otherwise stay invisible.

3. **Proliferate competing theories rather than converge early.** A single dominant theory makes its own supporting facts look inevitable and starves you of the contrasts that reveal its limits. Maintain a plurality of mutually inconsistent accounts on purpose. In practice this means resisting consensus pressure during the productive phase of a problem, keeping minority and "discredited" positions in active play, and judging the field's health by how many live alternatives it can sustain rather than by how fast it agrees.

4. **Treat all facts as theory-laden.** There are no bare observations sitting outside interpretation; what counts as a fact is already shaped by the framework that names it. This means an "observation that refutes a theory" may itself be an artifact of the theory you are trying to escape. Apply it by asking, for any datum used against a new idea, which background assumptions had to be true for that datum to register as a fact at all, then checking whether the new idea quietly denies those assumptions.

5. **Use ad hoc hypotheses and patches without shame, at least temporarily.** Methodologists scold scientists for propping up a young theory with convenient auxiliary assumptions. Feyerabend points out that every important theory was born underdeveloped, riddled with gaps, and survived only because people shielded it with provisional fixes until it grew strong enough to stand. Apply it by giving a promising-but-incomplete idea breathing room: let it carry temporary scaffolding instead of killing it the moment it fails a test, since premature rigor strangles the theories that later win.

6. **Reach outside reason: rhetoric, propaganda, and psychological tricks are part of how ideas win.** Persuasion in science is rarely pure logic; it involves style, repetition, authority, vivid examples, and emotional appeal. Rather than pretend these are corruptions, recognize them as ordinary tools that even Galileo used to make a new worldview feel natural. Apply it by being honest that introducing a radically new framework requires changing what people are willing to perceive and accept, which takes more than a clean syllogism, and by reading scientific persuasion for its non-rational machinery.

7. **Suspect the standard you are about to obey, especially when it feels obvious.** The rules that seem most self-evidently rational are usually the most historically contingent. Whenever a methodological principle strikes you as too obvious to question, treat that feeling as a flag rather than a license. Apply it by deliberately constructing the case where following the "obviously correct" standard would have blocked a real advance, which both tests the rule and loosens its grip on your imagination.

8. **Reconstruct a past thinker in their own terms before judging them.** Much philosophy of science condemns earlier figures for failing tests that did not exist yet. Instead, rebuild the problem situation as the historical actor faced it, with their available concepts, instruments, and rival options. Apply it by suspending modern verdicts about who was "right" and asking what moves were reasonable given what that person could actually see, which often reveals that the "irrational" choice was the productive one.

9. **Make the incommensurability check before comparing two big frameworks.** Some rival worldviews are not merely different answers to the same question; they reorganize the basic categories so thoroughly that there is no neutral measuring stick to rank them point by point. Before claiming one theory simply beats another, test whether their key terms even refer to the same things. If they do not, recognize that the choice between them is partly a choice of which world to inhabit, and stop pretending a shared yardstick settles it.

10. **Separate the question "is it true" from "who gets to decide."** Feyerabend insists that the authority of science is a political fact rather than a logical one, and should be treated as such in a free society. Apply this as a thinking move by always splitting two questions that get fused: whether a claim is well supported, and whether experts should have the power to impose it on everyone. Recognizing that the second is a civic decision, open to lay scrutiny, keeps you from mistaking institutional prestige for proof.

11. **Look for the productive role of error, myth, and "regression."** Apparent steps backward, older "primitive" ideas, and outright mistakes have repeatedly seeded later breakthroughs. Instead of filtering history into a clean march of progress, keep the discarded and the wrong in view as resources. Apply it by mining abandoned theories and non-scientific traditions for mechanisms, metaphors, and observations that the dominant approach has no room for.

12. **Refuse to let one tradition rule out the results of others by fiat.** A method that automatically disqualifies anything produced outside its own procedures has stopped being a search for truth and become a guild rule. Apply it by demanding that competing traditions, including non-scientific ones, be compared on what they actually deliver for the people who use them, rather than excluded in advance for using the "wrong" method. This is the pluralism that makes "anything goes" a description of open inquiry rather than a slogan for chaos.

13. **Adopt the opportunist stance: master many methods and switch fast.** Because the world keeps surprising us, no procedure fixed in advance can be trusted to be the best one for the next problem. The competent inquirer behaves like a versatile tactician, fluent in induction, deduction, analogy, intuition, and improvisation, ready to drop any of them the moment conditions change. Apply it by collecting methods as a toolkit rather than a creed, and by measuring yourself on how nimbly you abandon a tactic that has stopped paying rather than on loyalty to a school.

14. **Read your own certainty as a product of training.** Specialized education works by narrowing perception, freezing certain "facts," and trimming away the imagination, humor, and outside intuitions that might blur disciplinary boundaries. Apply it by periodically asking which of your firm convictions are conclusions you reached and which are conditioned reflexes installed by a curriculum. Deliberately importing an outsider's framing, a different field, a different culture, a naive question, is the antidote to manufactured obviousness.

15. **Defend reason only as a temporary, situational choice.** Feyerabend allows that there are moments when tightening the rules and giving strict method the floor is the wise move. The point is that this should be a conscious, revisable decision keyed to the situation, never a permanent oath. Apply it by treating any commitment to rigor as a tool you picked up for a reason and can put down, and by checking whether the current moment actually calls for discipline or merely for the comfort of having a rule to follow.

## Named concepts, biases, fallacies, and techniques
- **Epistemological anarchism**: the position that no universal, exception-free methodological rules govern good inquiry.
- **"Anything goes"**: not a positive principle Feyerabend endorses, but his summary of what history forces on anyone who demands one rule for all of science.
- **Counterinduction**: deliberately advancing theories that conflict with established facts and accepted theories to expose hidden assumptions.
- **Proliferation principle**: the methodological preference for many competing theories over premature convergence on one.
- **Theory-ladenness of observation**: the claim that no fact is neutral; what registers as data already depends on a framework.
- **Incommensurability**: the situation where two frameworks lack a shared standard or shared meanings, so they cannot be ranked by a neutral comparison.
- **Ad hoc hypothesis (rehabilitated)**: a patch added to save a theory, treated here as a legitimate and often necessary aid during a theory's infancy.
- **Natural interpretations**: deep, usually unnoticed assumptions fused to our observations, which must be made explicit and sometimes overturned to see a problem freshly.
- **The Galileo case**: Feyerabend's central worked example of a scientist winning through rhetoric, selective evidence, and rule-breaking rather than tidy method.
- **Consistency condition (criticized)**: the demand that new theories agree with confirmed predecessors, which he argues protects the old theory rather than truth.
- **Reason as ideology**: the diagnosis that "rationality" often functions as a quasi-religious authority rather than a transparent procedure.
- **Separation of science and state**: the civic proposal that science be one voice among many, subject to democratic oversight, like a separated church.
- **The tyranny of the well-trained mind**: how specialized education narrows perception and freezes facts, manufacturing false obviousness.

## What this book uniquely contributes
Most philosophy of science tries to discover the formula that distinguishes real science from imposters. Against Method attacks that whole project from inside the history of science itself, showing case by case that the celebrated advances depended on breaking the very rules later codified as "method," and that imposing those rules consistently would have aborted the discoveries. Its lasting gift is a permission and a discipline at once: permission to keep heretical ideas, broken rules, and outside traditions in play, paired with the historical and political alertness to see method as a contingent, sometimes coercive human institution rather than a neutral path to truth.

## Memorable principles and examples (paraphrased)
- Galileo did not win the argument for a moving Earth by calm appeal to the data. The plain evidence of the senses, a tower-dropped stone landing at its base, pointed the other way. He persuaded people by reframing what the senses were even reporting, leaning on a new physics that was itself shaky, and using vivid argument and salesmanship. The lesson is that overturning a worldview means changing what counts as obvious, and that takes more than clean logic.
- A fact that seems to refute your idea may be an artifact of the idea you are trying to leave behind. Because observation is soaked in theory, the only way to expose such a smuggled-in assumption is sometimes to build a rival theory that sees the same situation differently, then notice what each one lets you perceive.
- Every great theory was an ugly infant. It arrived incomplete, contradicted by some of the known facts, and survivable only because supporters refused to kill it on schedule and propped it up with temporary fixes until it could carry its own weight. Demanding instant rigor would have buried them all.
- Treat "anything goes" as a terrified gasp from the rationalist rather than as a rule of your own. It is what is left over once you insist on a single method and then look at how inquiry has actually proceeded. The honest reading is pluralism: many methods, many traditions, none crowned in advance.
- A free society should keep science on the same footing it keeps religion: useful, respected, and barred from imposing itself by decree. Whether a claim is well supported and whether experts may force it on everyone are separate questions, and the second belongs to citizens rather than to the prestige of the lab.
