# Superforecasting, Distilled

**Author:** Philip E. Tetlock and Dan Gardner (2015)

## Thesis
Forecasting accuracy is a trainable skill built from specific habits of mind: breaking questions into pieces, anchoring on base rates, thinking in fine-grained probabilities, and updating in small steps as evidence arrives, all kept honest by measuring your track record against the actual outcomes.

---

## Thinking Tools

### 1. Keep score with calibration and resolution (Brier scoring)
A forecast you never grade teaches you nothing. The book's foundational move is to express every prediction as a numerical probability with a defined claim and a deadline, then tally outcomes over many forecasts. Two qualities matter together. **Calibration** asks whether your probabilities match reality: when you say 70%, does it happen about 70% of the time? **Resolution** asks whether you are decisive: do you push confidently toward 0% and 100% when warranted rather than hiding at 50%? The Brier score measures the squared distance between your probability and what happened (0 is perfect, 0.5 is a coin flip, 2.0 is confidently wrong every time). Apply it by logging forecasts with timestamps and scoring them in batches so you can see your real accuracy instead of your remembered accuracy.

### 2. Translate vague words into numbers
Words like "serious possibility," "fair chance," and "may" feel precise but carry wildly different meanings to different readers. Tetlock recounts how intelligence analysts who all wrote "serious possibility" privately meant anything from 80/20 in favor to 80/20 against. Vague language also lets a forecaster claim victory no matter what happens, since "a fair chance" can be stretched after the fact. Force every probabilistic judgment into a number. The act of converting "likely" into "75%" is metacognition: it makes you examine how confident you actually are, and it makes the forecast gradeable.

### 3. Foxes beat hedgehogs
The single biggest predictor of accuracy was thinking style. **Hedgehogs** organize everything around one Big Idea (supply-side economics, a grand theory of history) and force every problem through that lens, which makes them confident, quotable, and frequently wrong. **Foxes** gather information from many sources, hold several models at once, talk in "howevers" and "on the other hands," and stay comfortable with uncertainty. Foxes won on both calibration and resolution. Become a fox by deliberately collecting clashing viewpoints, distrusting any single explanatory framework, and treating your favorite theory as one tool among many rather than a master key.

### 4. Start with the outside view (base rates first)
When facing a question, the instinct is to dive into its vivid specifics. Reverse that order. First find the **outside view**: treat the case as one instance of a broader class and ask how often things like this happen in general. If a family has a pet, what fraction of households like theirs own a dog before you study this particular family? Anchoring on the base rate first prevents the unique details of a case from inflating your estimate. Only after you have the reference-class frequency do you adjust.

### 5. Then layer in the inside view
After anchoring on the base rate, bring in the **inside view**: the specific facts that make this case different from the average member of its class. The discipline is in the sequence. Base rate first as the anchor, then move that anchor up or down for case-specific evidence. Most people skip straight to the inside view and never recover a sensible starting point.

### 6. Fermi-ize: decompose the impossible question
Many questions look unanswerable until you break them apart. Borrowing from physicist Enrico Fermi (famous for estimating quantities like piano tuners in Chicago), superforecasters split a hard question into smaller sub-questions, separating what is knowable from what is guesswork. Estimating each piece and recombining produces a far better answer than staring at the whole and guessing. The act of decomposition exposes hidden assumptions and shows you which sub-parts actually drive the answer.

### 7. Update in small, frequent steps
A forecast is a living judgment rather than a lottery ticket filed away until the draw. Superforecasters revisited their numbers constantly as news arrived, nudging by a few points rather than lurching. Good updating means weighting genuinely diagnostic information and ignoring noise. Two failure modes bracket this skill. **Underreaction** clings to a prior estimate when real evidence should move it (often from inattention or ego). **Overreaction** spins the wheel hard on flashy but weak evidence, destroying a good forecast. Aim for many small Bayesian-style adjustments and resist both freezing and overcorrecting.

### 8. Counter the wrong-side-of-maybe fallacy
People judge a probabilistic forecast by which side of 50% it landed on. If a forecaster said 70% rain and it stays dry, observers call the forecast "wrong," forgetting that the same forecast assigned 30% to dry. A single probabilistic call cannot be judged right or wrong; only a stack of them can be scored. Internalizing this protects you from punishing well-reasoned forecasts that happened to land on the less likely side, and it removes the perverse incentive to retreat into vague language to dodge blame.

### 9. Think in fine-grained probabilities
Most people run a mental dial with two or three settings: yes, no, maybe. Superforecasters use a dial with many gradations and distinguish 63% from 65%, even 4% from 5%. This granularity is not false precision; the tournament data showed that rounding superforecasters' estimates to coarser buckets measurably degraded their accuracy, while it barely touched ordinary forecasters. The lesson: if you can reliably tell that an 11% event happens slightly less often than a 12% event, that resolution is real skill worth cultivating.

### 10. Separate epistemic from aleatory uncertainty
Some uncertainty is **epistemic**: you do not know the answer, but it is knowable in principle (you could pry open the machine and study its gears). Some is **aleatory**: it is irreducibly random and no amount of study removes it (next year's weather on a given day). Recognizing which kind you face tells you how hard to push. For cloud-like, aleatory questions, superforecasters keep initial estimates in the cautious 35% to 65% band and move out only tentatively, because no cleverness beats the underlying randomness.

### 11. Probabilistic thinking over fate thinking
Asking "why did this happen to me" presumes events are meant to be. Probabilistic thinkers ask "how" and treat outcomes as quasi-random draws from a distribution of once-possible worlds. The data showed a real correlation: the more a forecaster leaned toward "everything happens for a reason," the less accurate they were. Finding cosmic meaning in events may aid wellbeing, but it corrodes foresight by replacing chance with destiny. To forecast well, accept that improbable things genuinely happen for no deeper reason than that some path had to be taken.

### 12. Take the dragonfly-eye view (synthesize many perspectives)
A dragonfly's compound eye fuses thousands of lenses into one image. Good forecasters actively seek out views that conflict with their own, weight prediction markets and crowd wisdom, and synthesize the lot into a single sharper estimate. Aggregating diverse, partly independent perspectives cancels individual errors. Apply it by genuinely arguing the other side, consulting people who disagree, and treating a wide spread of informed opinion as signal rather than noise.

### 13. Cultivate perpetual beta (growth mindset)
The strongest correlate of becoming a superforecaster was not raw intelligence but commitment to self-improvement: treating every belief as a draft to be revised and every outcome as a chance to learn. Tetlock calls this "perpetual beta," borrowing the software term for software that is never declared finished. The trait that mattered most was the determination to keep getting better. Build it by running deliberate post-mortems on your hits and misses and by hunting for the reasoning error rather than the bad-luck excuse.

### 14. Hold scout mindset toward your own errors
Superforecasters owned their mistakes quickly and looked for the flawed assumption behind them. When Bill Flack mispredicted a leader's shrine visit, he realized he had answered "what would I do" rather than "what will this person do," importing his own preferences into someone else's decision. The corrective is to keep asking whether you are reasoning toward the truth or defending a position, and to interrogate the substitution your mind quietly makes when a hard question gets swapped for an easier one.

## Named Concepts, Biases, and Fallacies

- **Dart-throwing chimpanzee:** Tetlock's earlier finding that the average expert pundit forecasting years out performed near chance. The point is that confident media expertise is uncorrelated with accuracy unless it is measured.
- **Brier score:** Distance between forecast probability and outcome. Lower is better; perfection is 0.
- **Calibration vs. resolution:** Matching your stated probabilities to reality versus being decisive enough to assign extreme probabilities when warranted.
- **Wrong-side-of-maybe fallacy:** Grading a probabilistic forecast by whether the outcome landed above or below 50%.
- **Hedgehog vs. fox (from Isaiah Berlin and Archilochus):** One-big-idea thinkers versus many-tools thinkers.
- **Outside view / inside view:** Base-rate-class reasoning versus case-specific reasoning, used in that order.
- **Reference class:** The broader category a case belongs to, used to source the base rate.
- **Fermi estimation:** Decomposing an unanswerable question into estimable parts.
- **Epistemic vs. aleatory uncertainty:** Knowable-in-principle ignorance versus irreducible randomness.
- **Tip-of-your-nose perspective:** The fast, intuitive first impression that feels like reality but is just one limited vantage point.
- **Perpetual beta:** Treating one's own judgment as permanently under revision.
- **Bias substitution:** Answering an easier question than the one actually asked (a Kahneman idea the book applies to forecasting).
- **Granularity that is bafflegab:** Fake precision used to impress, distinct from real precision that improves accuracy.

## What This Book Uniquely Contributes

Tetlock supplies the missing measurement layer for the entire enterprise of judgment. Where most thinking books describe biases, this one ran a multi-year forecasting tournament with tens of thousands of dated, scored, numerical predictions, then identified the specific habits that distinguished the top performers. The contribution is empirical proof that good forecasting is a learnable skill rather than an innate gift, plus a concrete recipe: define the question, gather diverse views, anchor on base rates, decompose, assign a fine-grained probability, update in small steps, and grade yourself relentlessly. It turns "think clearly" from an exhortation into a measurable practice.

## Memorable Principles and Examples (paraphrased)

1. **Be a bookie rather than a poet.** The intelligence veteran Sherman Kent fought to replace elastic phrases like "serious possibility" with explicit odds, because a number can be checked and a phrase can hide. Precision feels tacky until you realize vagueness is what lets bad forecasters never be wrong.

2. **The arms-race surprise.** A panel of brilliant Cold War experts confidently expected the next Soviet leader to be another grim party man. Then Gorbachev arrived and liberalized everything, and within hours those same experts explained why it had been obvious all along. Watching smart people rewrite their own predictions taught Tetlock how hindsight disguises failure as foresight.

3. **A forecast is a thermostat rather than a tattoo.** The best forecasters touched their numbers constantly, nudging them as news came in, and one attributed his success mostly to frequent updating. Keynes captured the spirit: when the facts change, change your mind.

4. **The love-of-your-life trap.** The odds that you would ever meet your partner were once vanishingly small, yet you did, and the mind concludes it was meant to be. Run that logic out and it claims a near-zero-probability event actually had probability 100%, which is incoherent. Improbable things happen because something had to happen, and accepting that is what keeps your thinking probabilistic.
