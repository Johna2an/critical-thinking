# A Whack on the Side of the Head, Roger von Oech

## Thesis
Most adults stop generating ideas because their minds carry a set of learned attitudes that von Oech calls mental locks: habits like hunting for the single right answer, demanding logic too early, obeying inherited rules, and fearing error. These locks are useful for executing known tasks and disastrous for finding new ones. Creative thinking is recoverable for anyone, because it is a posture you adopt on purpose, and the way back is to deliberately whack your own assumptions loose with provocations, reversals, play, and questions that invite many answers instead of one.

## Thinking tools

1. **Hunt for the second right answer.** The first acceptable answer ends the search, and a single idea gives you nothing to compare against, so you cannot judge its strengths or weaknesses. Force the search to continue past the obvious solution. Ask explicitly for "answers," "meanings," and "results" in the plural, and treat the first good idea as the floor rather than the ceiling. The most valuable solution is often the off-beat one sitting just past where most people quit.

2. **Change the question to change the answer.** The words you phrase a problem in smuggle in assumptions that steer the whole search. An architect who asks "what door goes here" designs a door; one who asks "what passageway goes here" might design a courtyard or a tunnel. Before solving, rewrite the question with broader or different vocabulary, then notice which new options the rewording opens. The Lithuanian villagers who asked "is the buried person alive" added air holes and food; those who asked "is the person dead" drove a stake through the lid. Same problem, opposite designs, all from the framing word.

3. **Surface and break your assumptions.** Every problem comes wrapped in unstated rules about what is allowed, and many of those rules expired long ago or never applied. List the constraints you are silently obeying, then ask which are real and which are inherited habit. Picasso saw bull's horns in bicycle handlebars because he dropped the assumption that handlebars are only handlebars. Treat each "you can't" as a hypothesis to test rather than a wall.

4. **Switch between soft and hard thinking at the right phase.** Idea generation and idea evaluation need opposite mental modes. Soft thinking is loose, metaphorical, playful, and tolerant of ambiguity; hard thinking is precise, logical, and judgmental. Use soft thinking in the germinal phase when you are looking for possibilities, and save hard thinking for the practical phase when you are selecting and building. The common error is applying hard logic and criticism while ideas are still forming, which kills them before they can grow.

5. **Ask "what if?"** A contrary-to-fact question instantly suspends a chunk of reality and pushes your mind into the realm of the possible. State a "what if" with some impossible or unlikely condition, then seriously answer it. What if people had edible clothing, what if we elected officials by lottery, what if we had seven fingers per hand. The answers rarely ship as-is, and the point is that working them out loosens deeply ingrained assumptions and produces stepping stones you would never have reached by staying realistic.

6. **Use ideas as stepping stones rather than destinations.** An impractical or even wrong idea can still carry you to a usable one if you refuse to judge it on arrival. When a thought seems silly, instead of discarding it, ask where it points and what it suggests next. The value of a provocation is the movement it creates in your thinking, so let one idea trigger the next in a chain until the chain reaches solid ground.

7. **Borrow someone else's mind.** You are trapped in your own assumptions, so import a different set by asking how a specific other person would attack the problem. Run it through Walt Disney, Machiavelli, your grandmother, a fool, a child. Each persona brings constraints they would ignore, expertise they would add, and twists they would invent that you would not. The mismatch between their approach and yours is where fresh moves appear.

8. **Consult the fool and reverse your viewpoint.** The fool's licensed job is to mock the rules everyone else takes seriously, and that mockery exposes assumptions you cannot see while you respect them. Take your holiest principle and argue the opposite case in earnest. Reverse the problem entirely: ask how to cause the failure you want to prevent, or describe your situation from the perspective of the opposite role. The fool is the reverse gear of the mind, and you sometimes have to back up to get out of a rut and move forward on a better road.

9. **Cross-fertilize from outside your field.** The richest ideas often come from importing a concept from a domain that has nothing to do with your problem. Deliberately graze junkyards, old magazines, sports, history, other professions, and ask what their patterns might transfer. Treat "that's not my area" as a signal to look there, because expertise inside one field becomes a cage, and the outsider's analogy is frequently the breakthrough.

10. **Be willing to be led astray.** Discoveries arrive when you follow an anomaly off your planned path: Jansky chasing telephone static found radio waves from the galaxy, Perkin hunting synthetic quinine found the first synthetic dye. Adopt a readiness that loosens preconceptions, pays attention to the incongruous things you would normally filter out, and uses surprises as doorways. You cannot force serendipity by hunting it directly, and you can prepare for it by staying relaxed and alert to what does not fit.

11. **See the obvious you have been overlooking.** What you take for granted hides in plain sight, like the bicycle makers who used drive-train technology in their factory for years before anyone thought to put a chain on the bike itself. Step back and ask the plainest possible questions: what are the most obvious things about this, what resources are already in front of me, and what is obviously absent that should be present. Explaining the problem aloud to someone who knows nothing about it drags the obvious into view.

12. **Think ambiguously on purpose.** Precision serves contracts and directions, and ambiguity serves invention, because a thing open to several readings forces you to ask what else this could mean. When generating, deliberately read instructions and situations in more than one way. "Cross out six letters" can mean remove six, or it can mean strike out the literal letters s-i-x, which leaves a different answer entirely. Cultivate a personal source of ambiguity, a paradoxical text or oracle, that routinely makes you find a second meaning.

13. **Treat errors as feedback and play the odds of trying.** Fear of being wrong shuts down the experimentation that creative work requires. Errors tell you what does not work and point toward what might, so reframe a miss as information rather than a verdict on you. Generate many attempts knowing most will fail, the way a photographer shoots a hundred frames to keep a few; quantity of tries is the reliable path to a worthwhile result. Also distrust your successes, because a method that worked once can lock you into repeating it past its usefulness.

14. **Add a constraint or a deadline to force invention.** Total freedom can paralyze, while a well-placed limit channels energy and provokes ingenuity. When stuck, deliberately impose a restriction (do it in half the space, with no budget, in one sentence) or set a real deadline with something at stake. The pressure narrows the field enough that your mind starts producing the unconventional moves it would never reach in open-ended comfort.

15. **Give yourself a creative license and then sell the idea.** Believing "I'm not creative" is a self-fulfilling prophecy that prevents the very attempts that would prove it false, so grant yourself explicit permission to generate, fail, and play. Then remember that having an idea is only half the job: ideas die without persistence and persuasion, so develop a thick skin for rejection, line up support, and keep selling the idea until it lands.

## Named concepts, biases, fallacies, and techniques

- **Mental locks**, the ten learned attitudes that block idea generation (the right answer, that's not logical, follow the rules, be practical, play is frivolous, that's not my area, don't be foolish, avoid ambiguity, to err is wrong, I'm not creative).
- **The whack**, a jolt, question, or provocation that knocks your mind out of its habitual groove so you can see differently.
- **Soft thinking vs hard thinking**, loose metaphorical idea-finding versus precise logical idea-judging; each belongs to a different phase of work.
- **Germinal (imaginative) phase vs practical phase**, the early stage where you generate and the later stage where you evaluate and execute.
- **The second right answer**, the deliberate search for additional solutions past the first acceptable one.
- **Imaginative seedbed**, the realm of the possible, cultivated with "what if" questions and stepping stones.
- **Stepping stone**, an impractical or wrong idea used as a bridge to a workable one.
- **The fool / the fool's cap**, the licensed role that parodies rules and reverses viewpoints to expose assumptions.
- **Slay a sacred cow**, challenge a rule or belief that is treated as beyond question.
- **The Aslan phenomenon**, the way naming or labeling something shapes and limits how you then think about it.
- **Cross-fertilization**, importing ideas and patterns across unrelated fields.
- **Being led astray (serendipity readiness)**, staying open to anomalies that redirect you to something better than your goal.
- **Groupthink / benefits of conformity**, the pressure to go along that suppresses dissenting and original ideas.
- **Self-fulfilling prophecy**, the belief that you are not creative, which suppresses the attempts that would disprove it.
- **The "more is better" trap (moreness)**, assuming the answer is always to add, when subtracting or constraining may serve better.
- **Heraclitus as creativity teacher**, paradoxical epigrams ("you can't step into the same river twice," "expect the unexpected") used as a personal engine of ambiguity.

## What this book uniquely contributes

This book supplies the upstream half of critical thinking that analytical texts skip: the deliberate manufacture of options before any of them is evaluated. Where most of the library teaches you to test, weigh, and dismantle ideas, von Oech teaches you to break your own assumptions on purpose and flood the field with candidates, on the principle that you cannot reason your way to a good decision from a single choice. Its lasting move is the attitude diagnosis: it names the specific learned mindsets (right answer, be practical, avoid ambiguity, to err is wrong) that quietly amputate idea generation, and it gives a concrete provocation to unlock each one. It is the playful, divergent counterweight to the convergent rigor of the rest of the shelf.

## Memorable principles and examples (paraphrased)

- A teacher drew a single chalk dot and asked a class of high-schoolers what it was; the room could only manage "a dot on the blackboard," yet kindergartners had named fifty things, from an owl's eye to a squashed bug. Schooling trains you to find the one right answer and quietly costs you the ability to look for more.

- A bandleader complained his nightclub's piano was always out of tune. Told a month later it was fixed, he returned to find it as sour as ever, because the owner had solved the wrong problem and simply had it painted. Grabbing the first way out can leave the real problem untouched.

- A programmer tried to brute-force the "cross out six letters" puzzle and got ten thousand lines of garbage, because the greatest computer on earth still produces nonsense when fed the wrong question. Reading the instruction ambiguously, by literally striking the letters s-i-x, leaves the word "banana."

- Bicycle makers spent years building bikes with enormous front wheels for speed while the drive-train that would make a safe, geared bicycle sat in their own factory. Nothing escapes attention quite so stubbornly as the thing you take for granted.

- Scientists chasing one target kept finding a better one off to the side: telephone static led to galactic radio waves, a malaria drug search led to the first synthetic dye. Follow the anomaly and you often arrive somewhere more valuable than your plan.
