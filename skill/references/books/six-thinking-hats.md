# Six Thinking Hats, Edward de Bono

## Thesis
Ordinary thinking fails because we try to do everything at once: gathering facts, feeling emotions, judging risks, finding benefits, and inventing ideas all collide inside a single confused moment. De Bono proposes that thinkers separate these functions into six named modes, each represented by a colored hat, and have everyone adopt the same mode at the same time. This replaces adversarial argument, where people defend fixed positions, with parallel thinking, where a group explores every facet of a subject together before choosing a route. The hats are deliberate directions to look in, chosen on purpose, so a person can switch into a mode at will rather than waiting for it to arise naturally.

## Thinking tools

1. **Separate the modes before you think.** The mind performs worse when it tries to assess danger, generate ideas, log facts, and feel emotion simultaneously, because each function sensitizes the brain in an incompatible direction. The move is to consciously isolate one function at a time and run it cleanly. Before tackling any problem, name which mode you are in right now (facts, feelings, caution, benefit, ideas, or control) and do only that. When you catch yourself doing two at once, stop and pick one.

2. **Use parallel thinking instead of argument.** In an argument, two people stake out opposite positions and spend their energy proving each other wrong, which explores only the seam between two views. The fix is to have everyone look in the same direction at the same moment, then all rotate to the next direction together, so the whole subject gets mapped from every angle. Apply it by asking a group to all do caution together, then all do benefits together, rather than letting one person attack while another defends. Contradictory views both get written down in parallel and reconciled later, or the design simply covers both.

3. **White hat: report facts as facts, opinions as opinions.** Decisions go wrong when a confident opinion is smuggled in dressed as a fact, so the white hat strips a claim down to what is actually known, what is believed, and what is missing. Wear it by asking only for information: what do we have, what do we need, what questions should we ask, and how could we get the rest. Forbid interpretation and persuasion while the hat is on; if someone reports a third party's opinion, label it as a reported opinion rather than treating it as established truth.

4. **Grade your facts by confidence, do not flatten them.** Real information lives on a spectrum from proven fact to believed fact to unverified rumor, and treating all of it as equally solid corrupts the decision. The move is to attach a confidence level to each piece, something the author frames as Japanese-style input where facts are laid out neutrally for everyone to examine before any conclusion is drawn. In practice, qualify every input ("checked," "probably," "someone claimed") so the group can weight it correctly instead of arguing past each other over shaky data.

5. **Red hat: make feelings visible and legitimate.** Emotions and hunches steer decisions whether or not we admit them, so hiding them just lets them act in the dark. The red hat gives people formal permission to state a feeling without justifying it, which paradoxically reduces emotional contamination because feelings get a defined channel instead of leaking into everything. Apply it by asking directly ("give me your red hat on this") and by stating your own gut reaction in two sentences, then taking the hat off. Never demand reasons during red hat time; the whole point is unjustified feeling spoken plainly.

6. **Treat intuition as a track-record adviser rather than an oracle.** A practiced expert's hunch carries compressed experience and can be genuinely valuable inside their domain, yet the same person's intuition is worthless outside it, and even good intuition is wrong often enough that it cannot be trusted blindly. The move is to handle intuition like an adviser you weight by past accuracy: if it has been right more than wrong in this domain, listen and play the odds; if it is venturing outside its field, discount it. Log intuitions on the map as feelings, then decide separately whether to act on them.

7. **Black hat: run deliberate, logical caution.** Survival depends on spotting what will fail, what breaks the law, what does not fit your resources or values, and the black hat is the dedicated time to hunt only for those flaws. The move is to sensitize the brain entirely toward danger and let it find every reasonable objection, while requiring that each objection stand on its own logic rather than mere distaste (distaste belongs under the red hat). Wear it by asking specifically for what could go wrong, and demand reasons that hold up in cold print rather than merely when a persuasive person delivers them.

8. **Quarantine criticism so it does not run loose.** The black hat is the most useful and the most abused mode, because habitual critics tend to fire objections at every moment and kill ideas before they form. The fix is to fence criticism inside an allotted slot: during black hat time, be as ruthlessly cautious as you can; outside it, criticism is out of order. This lets reflexive skeptics deploy their full critical power on schedule, and it protects fragile new ideas from being frosted the instant they appear.

9. **Yellow hat: search for value with the same rigor as caution.** People find it harder to be systematically positive than systematically negative, so benefits get overlooked unless someone is assigned to look for them. The yellow hat is the deliberate hunt for value, feasibility, and reasons something could work, backed by logic wherever possible and labeled as speculation where not. Apply it by asking, for any proposal, what the genuine benefits are and under what conditions they would materialize, forcing yourself to build the positive case before judging it.

10. **Green hat: schedule creativity as a duty rather than a gift.** Treating creativity as the job of one "idea person" leaves everyone else passively waiting to pounce, so good ideas never get a chance. The green hat allocates explicit time in which everyone is expected to produce a fresh idea or stay silent, and because people rise to what is expected, even self-described non-creatives start generating. Apply it by declaring a creative window ("ten minutes of green hat on this"), where putting forward possibilities and half-formed options is the assignment and judgment is suspended.

11. **Replace judgment with movement when generating ideas.** Normal thinking checks each new idea against past experience and discards whatever does not fit, which is fatal during creation because it kills anything genuinely new. The green hat substitutes movement: instead of asking "is this right," you ask where the idea leads, what is interesting in it, what principle it suggests, and use it as a stepping stone to the next thought. Apply it by deliberately riding an idea forward ("use this for its movement value") rather than rating it, extracting a usable principle even from an absurd starting point.

12. **Provoke on purpose to break out of patterns.** The brain is a pattern-recognition machine that channels thinking down established grooves, so escaping a groove requires a jolt from outside current logic. Provocation is a deliberately unreasonable statement (de Bono marks it with the word "po") used purely to launch movement, like supposing hamburgers were square or everyone became a policeman, then following where the jolt leads. Apply it by stating a provocation explicitly, signaling it is a provocation so others treat it correctly, and using random inputs like an unrelated word to force a new connection.

13. **Blue hat: think about the thinking and run the sequence.** Discussions drift into a stew of facts, feelings, criticism, and half-ideas because no one is managing the process itself. The blue hat is the conductor: it defines the problem, sets the order in which the other hats are used, enforces the discipline of staying in the called mode, and produces summaries and conclusions. Apply it at the start to lay out the agenda of hats, during the session to call switches and break up arguments, and at the end to ask for the outcome and next steps.

14. **Hold a difference open instead of forcing a winner.** When two views collide and the data cannot yet decide between them, arguing wastes the room and entrenches both sides. The blue hat move is to record both positions on the thinking map in parallel and move on, returning later when better information or a needed choice arrives. Apply it by asking under what circumstances each side would be right, then checking which circumstances match reality, so both parties are validated and the disagreement becomes a question rather than a fight.

15. **Wear the hats as roles, never as labels for people.** The instant you call someone "a black hat person," you trap them in a box and destroy the system, because the whole value is that every person can think in every direction. Each thinker must be able to use all six modes like all the gears of a car. Apply it by assigning hats to moments rather than to individuals: the entire group wears the same hat together and rotates, and no one is permanently cast as the critic, the dreamer, or the facts person.

## Named concepts, biases, fallacies, and techniques
- **Parallel thinking**: everyone examines the same aspect of a problem at the same moment, then rotates together, instead of debating from opposing corners.
- **Argument (adversarial thinking)**: the traditional Western mode where two sides defend fixed positions and try to prove each other wrong.
- **Map-making thinking**: explore the whole terrain first, note all routes, and choose a path last, rather than picking a conclusion first and defending it.
- **White hat**: pure information mode, facts and figures stated neutrally, opinions excluded or labeled.
- **Red hat**: emotion mode, feelings and intuition stated openly with no obligation to justify them.
- **Black hat**: caution mode, logical hunting for risks, faults, and reasons something will not work.
- **Yellow hat**: benefit mode, logical search for value, feasibility, and reasons something will work.
- **Green hat**: creative mode, deliberate generation of new ideas, alternatives, and possibilities.
- **Blue hat**: control mode, thinking about the thinking, managing sequence, focus, and conclusions.
- **Movement**: an active idiom that replaces judgment in creative mode, using an idea for where it leads rather than whether it is correct.
- **Provocation**: a deliberately unreasonable statement made to jerk thinking out of its existing pattern.
- **Po**: de Bono's signal word marking a statement as a provocation rather than a serious proposal.
- **Random word method**: feeding an unrelated word into a problem to force fresh associations.
- **Mismatch mechanism**: the brain's built-in discomfort when something fails to fit an existing pattern, the natural root of black hat caution.
- **Directions rather than descriptions**: the hats name where you intend to think next rather than a verdict on what was already said.

## What this book uniquely contributes
Most critical-thinking books sharpen the quality of a single thinker's reasoning; this one re-engineers the social and temporal structure of thinking so a group can reason together without fighting. Its core gift is the time-sharing insight: the mind can be sensitized in only one direction at once, so high-quality thinking comes from running facts, feelings, caution, benefit, creativity, and control as separate scheduled passes rather than a simultaneous blur. It also supplies a practical vocabulary and a switching device that lets people change mental modes on command and, just as important, request a specific mode from others, which turns abstract advice like "be more open-minded" into a concrete, callable action.

## Memorable principles and examples (paraphrased)
- **The four observers and the country house.** Four people stand on different sides of a house, each insisting over the intercom that their view is the true one. Parallel thinking has them all walk to the front together, then the side, then the back, so the building is seen whole. The argument vanishes because at every moment everyone faces the same way.
- **The roulette wheel has no memory.** After red comes up eight times running, intuition screams that black is due, yet the odds are unchanged. The lesson is that intuition is real and worth hearing but carries no infallibility, so you weight it by its track record rather than obeying it.
- **New ideas are seedlings exposed to frost.** A fresh idea arrives delicate and unformed, and the reflexive criticism of black hat habits can kill it on contact. The green hat exists to shelter the seedling long enough to see whether it can grow into something sturdy.
- **The thin man, the fat man, and the bicycle.** In a footrace the thin man wins, which is "what is." Give the fat man a bicycle and he wins, which is "what can be." Labeling people by their current tendencies freezes them in place; the hats insist everyone can learn to operate in any mode.
- **The blue hat as orchestra conductor.** The conductor plays no instrument yet gets the best from the orchestra by ensuring each part enters at the right time. The blue hat thinks about the thinking, calling each mode into play in sequence so the whole performance coheres.
