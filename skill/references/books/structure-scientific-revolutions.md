# The Structure of Scientific Revolutions, Thomas S. Kuhn

## Thesis
Science does not advance by smoothly piling up confirmed facts; it moves through long stretches of settled, rule-bound work punctuated by upheavals that replace the entire framework of assumptions a community works within. Each community organizes its research around a shared model of exemplary problems and solutions, and that model determines which questions count, which methods are legitimate, and even what the practitioners perceive. To understand how knowledge actually changes, study the historical communities that produce it and the moments when one shared framework gives way to another, rather than measuring science against an idealized logic of proof.

## Thinking tools

1. **Locate the paradigm before judging the work.** A paradigm is the shared package of achievements, assumptions, instruments, and standard problems that a community treats as the model for "doing it right." Before you evaluate any claim or experiment, identify the paradigm the practitioner is operating inside, because it silently fixes what counts as a real question, a legitimate method, and an acceptable answer. Apply it by asking of any field, argument, or organization: what is the agreed-upon exemplar everyone here is implicitly imitating, and what does that exemplar quietly rule out of bounds?

2. **Distinguish normal work from foundational work.** Most activity in a mature field is "normal" puzzle-solving: cleaning up details, extending measurements, and matching the framework to nature more precisely, all while the framework itself goes unquestioned. A smaller, rarer activity challenges the framework itself. Apply this by sorting any debate into one of two layers, are people disputing a move inside the agreed rules, or disputing the rules themselves, because the two require completely different kinds of evidence and persuasion.

3. **Treat research as puzzle-solving with assured solutions.** Inside a settled framework, problems resemble puzzles: the community already assumes a solution exists, and the challenge is the practitioner's ingenuity in reaching it. A failure usually reflects on the researcher rather than the framework. Use this to read failure correctly: when a result disappoints, first ask whether the community would blame the worker rather than the theory, because that attribution is itself a sign you are in normal-science mode and the framework is being protected.

4. **Let shared examples rather than stated rules, carry the meaning.** Practitioners often agree on how to work while disagreeing about, or never articulating, the abstract rules behind it. What they actually share is a stock of concrete model solutions they learned to imitate. Apply this by studying the worked examples a field trains its newcomers on, rather than its official methodology statements, since the examples reveal how people really reason and the stated rules are often a later rationalization.

5. **Watch for anomaly as the trigger of change.** Discovery begins when something refuses to fit expectations and the community notices the misfit. An anomaly only becomes significant once the framework has made expectations precise enough for the violation to register as a violation. Apply this by tracking persistent, recognized misfits in any system: the surprises that practitioners cannot explain away are the leading edge of eventual restructuring, so log them rather than discarding them.

6. **Diagnose crisis by the loosening of the rules.** When anomalies accumulate and resist repair, confidence in the framework erodes and the field enters crisis: people start proposing rival versions, reopening settled questions, and arguing about fundamentals they previously took for granted. Apply this as a diagnostic. When you see proliferating ad hoc fixes, open disagreement about basics, and willingness to try wild alternatives, the community is in crisis and a shift is becoming possible.

7. **Expect a new framework rather than a patched old one.** Crises rarely resolve by repairing the existing framework; they resolve when a fresh framework arrives that handles the stubborn anomalies and reorganizes the field. The old framework is abandoned only when a viable competitor exists to replace it. Apply this by refusing to accept "the theory was simply disproven" as a full story: ask what replacement made abandonment thinkable, because frameworks are dropped for successors rather than for emptiness.

8. **Recognize incommensurability across frameworks.** Rival frameworks are hard to compare directly because they often define their core terms differently, prize different problems, and even disagree about what the relevant facts are. There is no fully neutral measuring stick standing above both. Apply this whenever two camps seem to talk past each other: check whether they are using shared words with shifted meanings and ranking importance by different scales, because much apparent stubbornness is genuine difficulty of translation.

9. **Notice that frameworks shape perception itself.** Adopting a new framework can change what practitioners literally see when they look at the same instrument reading, specimen, or sky. The framework supplies the categories through which raw experience becomes meaningful observation. Apply this by distrusting "just look at the data" as a tiebreaker: ask how each side's framework pre-sorts the very observations they point to, since the data does not arrive pre-labeled.

10. **Read textbooks as rewritten history.** Once a framework wins, the field's textbooks present its development as a straight, cumulative march toward present knowledge, erasing the dead ends, the displaced framework, and the messiness of the transition. This makes revolutions nearly invisible after the fact. Apply this by treating any tidy "and then we steadily progressed" narrative with suspicion, and by hunting for the original disputes the clean account has smoothed over.

11. **Account for conversion rather than merely proof.** Switching frameworks resembles a shift of allegiance more than a forced logical deduction; evidence matters, but so do judgments of promise, fruitfulness, and elegance, and older practitioners often never convert at all. Apply this when trying to move a group: marshal evidence, and also show that the new approach opens more productive work ahead, because adoption follows perceived future fertility as much as past results.

12. **Use values as the tiebreakers when proof runs out.** When competing frameworks cannot be settled by a knockdown experiment, practitioners lean on shared values: accuracy, scope, simplicity, consistency, and fruitfulness. These values guide choice without dictating a unique answer, so reasonable experts can weigh them differently. Apply this by making your decision values explicit in any underdetermined choice, then noticing that disagreement may be a legitimate difference in weighting rather than one side being irrational.

13. **Redefine progress as problem-solving power rather than approach to truth.** Kuhn measures advance by a framework's growing ability to solve concrete problems rather than by closing in on some final picture of reality. Later frameworks tend to solve more puzzles than their predecessors, which is the defensible sense of progress. Apply this by judging a method or theory on what it lets you actually do and resolve, rather than on grand claims about ultimate correctness.

14. **Mine the prehistory: before consensus there is competing chaos.** Young fields lack a single shared framework and instead host many schools, each gathering facts almost at random under its own assumptions. The field matures only when one achievement is compelling enough to organize everyone's work. Apply this to any emerging discipline or industry: expect scattered, incompatible approaches early, and watch for the first exemplar powerful enough to consolidate the community.

15. **Read your own field historically and from the inside.** Kuhn's whole method is to understand past scientists on their own terms, asking why intelligent people once held views now considered wrong, instead of grading them against current knowledge. Apply this charitable, historical lens to colleagues, rivals, and your earlier self: reconstruct the framework that made a position reasonable in its moment before you dismiss it, because that reconstruction is where the real learning lives.

## Named concepts, biases, fallacies, and techniques
- **Paradigm**: the shared achievements, assumptions, and exemplary problem-solutions that organize a scientific community's work.
- **Normal science**: routine, framework-preserving research aimed at refining and extending the accepted paradigm.
- **Puzzle-solving**: the core activity of normal science, where solutions are assumed to exist and the test is the researcher's skill.
- **Disciplinary matrix**: Kuhn's later, sharper term for the full set of commitments a community shares (symbolic generalizations, models, values, exemplars).
- **Exemplar**: a concrete worked problem-solution that trainees imitate and that carries practical meaning beyond any stated rule.
- **Anomaly**: a recognized failure of nature to match the expectations the paradigm produced.
- **Crisis**: the breakdown of confidence when anomalies pile up and practitioners begin questioning fundamentals.
- **Scientific revolution**: the replacement of one paradigm by an incompatible new one, reorganizing the field.
- **Paradigm shift**: the discontinuous change in worldview, perception, and problem-set that a revolution brings.
- **Incommensurability**: the absence of a fully neutral common standard for comparing rival paradigms, due to shifted meanings and priorities.
- **Theory-ladenness of observation**: the idea that what one perceives as data is shaped by one's framework.
- **Gestalt switch**: Kuhn's analogy for how the same evidence flips into a wholly new pattern after conversion.
- **Conversion**: the allegiance-like, partly non-logical character of accepting a new paradigm.
- **Cognitive values**: accuracy, scope, simplicity, consistency, and fruitfulness, used to choose between frameworks when proof is inconclusive.
- **The invisibility of revolutions**: how textbooks rewrite history into a smooth cumulative story that hides past upheavals.
- **Pre-paradigm period**: the early, school-ridden phase of a field before a unifying achievement appears.
- **The essential tension**: the productive pull between disciplined tradition and the openness needed for breakthroughs.

## What this book uniquely contributes
Kuhn reframed scientific knowledge as the product of human communities with histories, training, and shared habits of mind, rather than as a timeless logic that any rational person would derive from the facts. By insisting that perception, vocabulary, and the very list of "real" problems are framework-dependent, he gave us a portable vocabulary, paradigm, anomaly, crisis, revolution, incommensurability, for analyzing how any organized group of experts thinks, defends its assumptions, and eventually changes its mind. The lasting contribution is a model of intellectual change that takes resistance, persuasion, and worldview seriously instead of treating disagreement as mere error.

## Memorable principles and examples (paraphrased)
- **The duck-rabbit moment.** Two researchers can study the identical instrument trace and genuinely see different things, because each brings a different framework that tells them what the trace means. Convince someone of a new framework and the world they observe rearranges itself, much like a figure that suddenly reads as a different animal.
- **Oxygen and the air that was already "discovered."** Several investigators isolated the same gas around the same time, yet what they had found stayed unsettled because they described it through an older chemical framework. The discovery was complete only once a new framework let them recognize what the substance actually was, which shows that observing a thing and understanding it are separate steps.
- **The planet that was a star for centuries.** Sky-watchers had recorded a certain object for ages while reading it as just another fixed point. Only after attention and expectations sharpened did the same object reveal itself as a wandering planet, illustrating how a community must be primed before an anomaly can register as a discovery.
- **Textbooks hide the war that was fought.** A student learns the winning theory as if it descended in a calm, logical sequence, never glimpsing the displaced framework or the bitter contest that preceded victory. The cleanliness of the lesson is exactly what makes the prior revolution disappear from view.
- **Old guards rarely convert; the field turns over instead.** When a powerful new framework arrives, many senior practitioners hold their ground to the end, and the change completes mostly as a younger generation, trained inside the new approach, takes their place. Frameworks shift partly by succession rather than solely by argument.
