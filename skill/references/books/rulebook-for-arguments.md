# A Rulebook for Arguments, Anthony Weston (5th ed.)

## Thesis
Good thinking is a constructive craft: you build a claim by marshaling honest reasons and evidence in the right form, then stress-test it against objections, alternatives, and counterexamples until it earns belief.

## Orientation
Weston treats an argument as an offering of reasons in support of a conclusion, a tool for inquiry (finding which views hold up) and for explanation (showing others how you got there). The book is a checklist of forms and faults, organized so you can build arguments rather than merely shoot down fallacies. The tools below are the load-bearing ones.

## Thinking tools

### 1. Resolve premises and conclusion
**What it is.** Before anything else, separate the claim you want to establish (conclusion) from the reasons offered for it (premises), and confirm you actually endorse both.
**Why it works.** Most muddled thinking comes from never naming the conclusion or from smuggling the conclusion in among the reasons. Once you can write "Premises... Therefore, conclusion," you can see whether the support actually bears on the claim.
**How to apply.** State the conclusion in one sentence. List the reasons under it. Ask of each reason: does this give independent support, or does it just restate the conclusion? If you cannot commit to a premise, change it.

### 2. Build on substance rather than overtone
**What it is.** Persuade with concrete reasons rather than emotionally loaded words. Loaded language ("disgracefully allowed," "honor-bound," "going back to the caves") simulates an argument while supplying no evidence.
**Why it works.** Overtones can move an audience without giving them any actual ground to stand on, which leaves everyone exactly where they started. Emotional charge also tends to misfire when the reader notices the manipulation.
**How to apply.** Strip the charged adjectives and see what factual claim remains. If little remains, the argument was overtone. Also resist loading language against opponents: assume people hold their views for serious reasons, and state their position as they would.

### 3. Use representative examples (and use more than one)
**What it is.** A generalization needs several examples, and the examples must mirror the whole population you are generalizing about rather than a convenient or self-selected slice.
**Why it works.** One example may be an exception. Even many examples mislead when they share a bias: insects that bite come to mind first because they bug us, so "do insects bite?" gets the wrong answer. Yard signs, online polls, and tourists are self-selected and skew the picture.
**How to apply.** Ask "what would a fair cross-section look like, and does my sample resemble it?" Larger populations need more examples. Reject any sample people can opt into. For small finite sets (your siblings, the planets), examine all of them.

### 4. Background rates (base rates) are often crucial
**What it is.** A vivid success or failure means little until you know the ratio of hits to tries. One bull's-eye proves nothing unless you also count the misses.
**Why it works.** Dramatic single cases (a horoscope that came true, a shark attack, a lottery winner) are memorable and heavily featured, which makes us overestimate how common they are. The denominator, the number of attempts or the population at risk, carries the real information.
**How to apply.** For any "look, it happened" claim, ask: out of how many chances? Compare the event rate to the base rate. When you fear a rare event or chase a rare reward, look up the actual frequency before acting.

### 5. Read statistics with a critical eye
**What it is.** Numbers demand the same scrutiny as any other evidence. An impressive figure often omits the comparison or denominator that would make it meaningful.
**Why it works.** Statistics carry an aura of authority that can switch off scrutiny. "More than 50 percent of athletes graduate" hides the comparison to all students and to prior years; "412,067 cups wasted" fakes precision; doubled car thefts may mean two instead of one.
**How to apply.** For every statistic ask: compared to what? compared to when? how many cases underlie the percentage? Distrust suspicious precision, questions designed to push an answer, figures based on extrapolation, and numbers about activities people hide.

### 6. Reckon with counterexamples
**What it is.** Actively hunt for cases that would contradict your generalization, early and on purpose, then refine the claim to survive them.
**Why it works.** Exceptions threaten to disprove a rule, so finding them yourself lets you narrow or sharpen the claim before an opponent does. The search frequently produces a more interesting and defensible thesis ("many renewables are widely used" rather than "renewables are widely used").
**How to apply.** After drafting any generalization, deliberately ask what would violate it. When you find a counterexample, decide whether to qualify the scope, restate the claim, or rethink what you actually want to argue.

### 7. Analogies require relevantly similar cases
**What it is.** Argument by analogy reasons from one case to another on the strength of likeness. It is strong only when the two cases share features relevant to the conclusion rather than merely many incidental features.
**Why it works.** Two things differ in countless ways; the test is whether they match on the dimension the conclusion depends on. Nordwell's "discovery of Italy" lands because the relevant point (claiming an already-inhabited land is senseless) transfers cleanly, even though jets and the Santa Maria differ.
**How to apply.** Name the property doing the work in the conclusion. Ask whether both cases truly share that property. List the differences and judge whether any of them break the relevant similarity.

### 8. Weigh authority with a four-part test
**What it is.** When you must rely on sources, accept them only if they are cited, informed, impartial, and cross-checked.
**Why it works.** Experts are still human: overconfident, sometimes misled, sometimes biased. The four filters catch the common failures. Citation lets others verify; qualification ensures the source knows the field; impartiality flags conflicts of interest; cross-checking catches the lone outlier.
**How to apply.** Cite enough that a reader can find the source. Match the source to the actual question (climatologists for climate rather than politicians). Discount sources with a stake in the answer. Confirm the claim against independent sources, and apply extra Internet savvy to anonymous or unvetted material.

### 9. Move from correlation to the most likely cause
**What it is.** Causal claims start from a correlation, but a correlation between E1 and E2 has several possible explanations, and good reasoning works toward the most plausible one.
**Why it works.** A correlation is consistent with E1 causing E2, E2 causing E1, a third factor causing both, mutual causation, or coincidence. Naming these alternatives stops you from leaping to the first story that fits, the error behind post hoc reasoning.
**How to apply.** Whenever you see "A is linked to B," list the alternative explanations explicitly. Look for evidence that discriminates among them. Prefer the explanation that accounts for the data with the fewest unsupported assumptions, and expect real-world causes to be multiple rather than single.

### 10. Master the valid deductive forms
**What it is.** A toolkit of argument shapes that guarantee a true conclusion when their premises are true: modus ponens, modus tollens, hypothetical syllogism, disjunctive syllogism, dilemma, and reductio ad absurdum.
**Why it works.** These forms are valid by structure, so the only way to attack a deductive argument is to challenge a premise. Knowing the forms lets you both build airtight chains and locate exactly where a chain can break.
**How to apply.** Translate prose into the skeleton (If p then q; p; therefore q). Use modus tollens to refute (if p then q, not-q, so not-p). Use disjunctive syllogism to eliminate options ("rule out all other factors"). Use reductio by assuming the opposite and deriving an absurdity. Chain simple forms into multi-step proofs, labeling intermediate conclusions so you can reuse them.

### 11. Explore the issue before taking a side
**What it is.** Begin extended thinking with an open question, survey the arguments on several sides, and stay willing to be moved before you commit.
**Why it works.** Starting from a fixed conclusion and shopping for support produces brittle, predictable arguments. Genuine inquiry surfaces unexpected facts and stronger positions, and you cannot know in advance where the evidence leads.
**How to apply.** Frame the topic as a question. Collect the best case for each answer, including answers you dislike. Even when handed a position, study the opposition so you can engage it, and look for fresh approaches rather than the worn arguments everyone has heard.

### 12. Your argument is your outline (spell out, then defend each premise)
**What it is.** Draft the core claim as a short premises-and-conclusion argument, then treat each premise as the conclusion of a further argument needing its own support.
**Why it works.** This turns a vague position into a structure where every load-bearing claim has visible backing. The outline doubles as the skeleton of an essay: state the basic argument, then advance each premise in turn with its own paragraph and defense.
**How to apply.** On scratch paper, write three to five premises and a conclusion. For each contestable premise, build a supporting mini-argument. Expect the whole structure to flex as evidence comes in, so premises and even the conclusion may change.

### 13. Reckon with objections and explore alternatives
**What it is.** Seek out the strongest case against your conclusion and answer it, and for any proposal show it beats the other plausible solutions to the same problem rather than merely that it works.
**Why it works.** Most actions have many effects, so ignoring the downside leaves you exposed. A proposal that solves a problem is still unjustified if a cheaper or better option does the same. Considering objections also signals that you have done the work with an open mind.
**How to apply.** Ask "what is the best argument against this?" and lay out the full counter-argument before replying. Then brainstorm serious alternatives (not strawmen), and argue that yours is superior. If an alternative wins, revise your conclusion.

### 14. Clarify contested terms by working from clear cases
**What it is.** When a key word is unclear or disputed, get specific. For a contested term, build a definition that includes the clear yes-cases, excludes the clear no-cases, and draws a defensible line through the unclear middle.
**Why it works.** Many disputes are really verbal: people argue past each other because "drug," "equal," or "meeting" means different things to each. A definition that respects the clear cases keeps the argument honest, and exposing a shift in meaning (equivocation) collapses a bad argument.
**How to apply.** List the obvious cases the term fits and the obvious cases it does not. Find the feature that separates them (feathers rather than flight, for birds) and justify the boundary. Keep one fixed sense throughout. Remember that defining a term clarifies the question but does not settle it; you still need the substantive argument.

## Named fallacies and faults worth recognizing

- **Equivocation.** A term shifts meaning between premise and conclusion (the sexes are not "equal" sliding from "identical" to "entitled to the same rights"). Pin each term to one sense.
- **False dilemma.** Reducing the options to two, usually extremes ("Love it or leave it"). Increase the number of options you consider.
- **Begging the question / petitio principii.** Assuming what you are trying to prove, often by hiding the conclusion inside a premise.
- **Mere redescription.** A premise that just restates the conclusion in new words ("a fine architect" because "a very capable designer of buildings"), or Molière's "dormitive principle" that explains sleep by renaming it. Demand independent evidence.
- **Non sequitur.** A conclusion that does not follow from or even relate to the evidence. Identify the missing link.
- **Overgeneralizing.** Generalizing from too few or unrepresentative examples (Rules 7 and 8).
- **Overlooking alternatives.** Forgetting that an effect can have several causes, or a situation several options.
- **Post hoc, ergo propter hoc.** Inferring causation from mere sequence in time. Test rival causal explanations.
- **Loaded language and persuasive definition.** Wording (or a definition) built to push emotions rather than to inform ("abortion means murdering babies," "evolution means the atheistic view that...").
- **Poisoning the well.** Disparaging a position with loaded language before it is even stated ("no sensitive person thinks that...").
- **Red herring.** Diverting attention to an irrelevant, usually heated, side issue.
- **Straw person.** Refuting a caricature of the opposing view instead of the real one. If you cannot imagine why anyone holds the view, you probably do not understand it yet.

## Craft rules for presenting arguments

- **Unfold ideas in a natural order** with signpost words ("one reason is..."); each sentence should set up the next.
- **Start from reliable premises**; a perfect inference from a weak premise still yields a weak conclusion.
- **Be concrete and concise**; "we hiked for hours in the sun" beats an inflated paraphrase, and airy elaboration loses the reader.
- **Use consistent terms**; carry one idea through with the same words rather than restyling it each time, which keeps long arguments coherent (parallel construction, as in the Beatitudes).
- **Jump right in** without windy preambles; state a definite claim or proposal; detail objections and meet them; seek feedback; close with modesty about how far the argument reaches.
- **In public debate**, listen and learn, offer something positive, work from common ground, stay civil, and leave the audience thinking. The aim is shared inquiry, and treating debate well is itself part of the case.

## What this book uniquely contributes
- A **constructive stance on critical thinking**: it equips you to assemble good arguments, where most fallacy-focused treatments only teach demolition.
- A **rule-numbered, consultable reference**: you can point yourself or a writer to "Rule 16" rather than re-explain a principle each time.
- A **single ladder from short arguments to essays to public debate**, showing that long arguments are short ones combined, defended, and arranged.
- An explicit ethics of arguing: charity toward opponents, common ground, and civility are presented as part of reasoning well rather than as mere manners.

## Memorable principles, paraphrased
- A strong view with nothing behind it is the real mistake. Holding convictions is fine; having only convictions, with no reasons to offer, leaves you unable to learn or to persuade.
- Count the misses rather than merely the hits. One bull's-eye looks impressive until you ask how many shots were fired; the denominator decides whether a result is skill or luck.
- Counterexamples are a generalizer's allies. Searching for what would break your claim is the surest way to sharpen it into something true and defensible.
- A definition organizes thought but rarely ends an argument. Once "drug" is defined to include coffee and alcohol, you still owe a separate argument about what to do with each.
