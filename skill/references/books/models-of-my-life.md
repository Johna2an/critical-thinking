# Models of My Life, Herbert A. Simon

## Thesis
Simon argues that a single mind can think across many disciplines because the deep structure of problem solving is the same everywhere: search through a space of possibilities under limits of attention, memory, and time. His own life, told as a sequence of "models," is offered as evidence that intelligence is a matter of how you represent a problem and how you search rather than of raw genius. The book teaches that bounded rationality, satisficing, and disciplined search are general-purpose tools for living and working well in a world too complex to be optimized.

## Thinking tools

1. **Bounded rationality.** Treat every decision as made by a mind with finite attention, memory, and computing power facing a world far richer than it can model. Before solving, ask what the agent can actually perceive and calculate, then design your method to fit those limits rather than assuming perfect information. Apply it by listing what you genuinely know and can compute, and stop pretending you have access to the full set of options or consequences.

2. **Satisficing instead of optimizing.** Set an aspiration level (a "good enough" threshold) and accept the first option that meets it, because finding the true maximum usually costs more than the gain is worth. Apply it by writing down your criteria in advance, then taking the first candidate that clears the bar and adjusting the bar up or down based on how easily candidates appear. This converts paralyzing global searches into finite, livable ones.

3. **Problem space and search.** Frame any hard problem as a starting state, a goal state, a set of legal moves, and the tree of states those moves generate; then solving becomes navigating that tree. Apply it by explicitly naming the moves available and the test that recognizes the goal, which often reveals that the difficulty is a poor representation rather than a hard problem. Most insight comes from redrawing the space so the path becomes short.

4. **Heuristic search over exhaustive search.** Since the tree of possibilities is usually astronomically large, use rules of thumb that prune branches and steer toward promising regions even though they offer no guarantee. Apply it by asking "which partial moves look most like progress toward the goal?" and following those, accepting that a heuristic that works most of the time beats a complete method that never finishes. Cleverness lives in the pruning rule rather than in brute force.

5. **Means-ends analysis.** Compare your current state to the goal, identify the largest difference, and pick an action known to reduce that kind of difference; if the action's preconditions are not met, make reducing that gap the new subgoal. Apply it recursively until each remaining gap has a ready operator. This single habit organizes everything from proving a theorem to fixing a stalled project.

6. **Decomposition into near-decomposable systems.** Complex systems are usually built from subsystems whose internal links are strong and cross-links are weak, so you can analyze one part at a time and reassemble later with small error. Apply it by carving a problem at its weak joints, treating each module as roughly independent for the first pass, and only then accounting for the slow, weak interactions between modules. This is why hierarchy is the natural architecture of complexity.

7. **The watchmaker parable (stable subassemblies).** Build anything complex out of intermediate stable parts, because a process that must complete the whole thing in one uninterrupted run will almost never finish in a noisy world. Apply it to careers, software, and arguments alike: create checkpoints that hold their value even if you are interrupted, so progress accumulates instead of resetting. Robust construction beats heroic construction.

8. **Procedural vs. substantive rationality.** Judge a decision by the quality of the process that produced it rather than merely by whether the outcome turned out well, because in an uncertain world good processes sometimes yield bad outcomes and luck sometimes rescues bad ones. Apply it by auditing how a choice was searched and tested rather than scoring it purely on results. This protects you from learning the wrong lesson from a single noisy outcome.

9. **Symbol systems as the substrate of thought.** Treat thinking as the manipulation of symbols that stand for things, which means a mind and a suitably arranged machine can in principle perform the same operations. Apply it by trying to write down, step by step, the symbolic operations you actually perform when you solve a problem; if you can specify them, you understand the problem, and if you cannot, you have only an intuition. Externalizing the procedure is the test of real comprehension.

10. **Chunking and the limits of working memory.** Recognize that you can hold only a handful of items in mind at once, so expertise is largely the slow accumulation of larger meaningful "chunks" that each count as one item. Apply it by deliberately grouping raw details into named patterns, which frees attention for higher-level moves; this is why a master sees a position where a novice sees scattered pieces. Build your chunks on purpose rather than waiting for them to form.

11. **Attention as the true scarce resource.** In a world flooded with information, the binding constraint is not data but the attention available to process it, so designing what to ignore matters as much as designing what to do. Apply it by treating every information source as a tax on attention and asking whether it earns that tax; filter aggressively and route only the highest-value signals to your limited focus. Information consumes attention, so abundance of one creates scarcity of the other.

12. **Identification with subgoals (organizational loyalty).** People in organizations adopt the goals of their unit as their own, which channels their bounded rationality toward those local objectives and shapes every decision they make. Apply it by asking "which subgoal has this person internalized?" to predict and understand behavior, and by deliberately designing the subgoals you want people (including yourself) to absorb. Loyalty is a decision premise installed by environment rather than a fixed trait.

13. **Cross-disciplinary transfer through shared structure.** Because problem solving, decision making, and design share the same underlying mechanics, a tool learned in one field often transfers directly to another. Apply it by abstracting any technique to its structural core (what is the state, the operators, the test?) and then scanning unrelated fields for the same shape. Simon used this to move between economics, psychology, computer science, and administration without starting over each time.

14. **The sciences of the artificial.** Distinguish the natural sciences, which study how things are, from a science of design, which studies how to make things that achieve goals within constraints. Apply it by treating any plan, organization, or program as an artifact to be engineered against an environment, asking how its inner structure must be shaped to meet outer demands. Design is a teachable, rigorous discipline rather than a mystery reserved for artists.

15. **Aspiration adjustment and learning.** Let your aspiration level rise when good options are easy to find and fall when they are scarce, so your standards stay calibrated to the real difficulty of the environment. Apply it by tracking how hard your last few searches were and resetting the "good enough" bar accordingly, which keeps you from quitting too early or holding out for an unreachable ideal. Learning is largely the tuning of these thresholds over time.

## Named concepts, biases, fallacies, and techniques
- **Bounded rationality**, real minds decide with limited information, attention, and computing power rather than as perfect optimizers.
- **Satisficing**, accept the first option that meets a "good enough" threshold rather than hunting for the maximum.
- **Aspiration level**, the moving bar that defines what counts as "good enough" in a given search.
- **Problem space**, the set of all states reachable from a start by legal moves, through which solving is a search.
- **Heuristic**, a rule of thumb that prunes the search and often works, with no guarantee of success.
- **Means-ends analysis**, close the gap to a goal by applying actions that reduce the biggest current difference.
- **Near-decomposability**, complex systems split into modules with strong internal and weak external links.
- **Stable subassemblies**, intermediate parts that hold their value, letting complex things be built reliably.
- **Procedural rationality**, judging the quality of the decision process rather than merely the outcome.
- **Physical symbol system**, the claim that symbol manipulation is sufficient for general intelligent action.
- **Chunking**, grouping details into larger meaningful units to overcome working-memory limits.
- **Attention scarcity**, the principle that information abundance makes attention the binding constraint.
- **Identification with subgoals**, members of an organization internalize their unit's goals as personal aims.
- **Sciences of the artificial**, a rigorous design discipline for shaping artifacts to meet goals under constraints.
- **Maze metaphor of a life**, a career seen as branching paths where each choice forecloses others.

## What this book uniquely contributes
The book's distinctive move is to treat an entire intellectual life as a working demonstration of a single theory of mind. Rather than separating memoir from method, Simon shows that the same toolkit (bounded rationality, search, satisficing, decomposition) explains how he chose careers, ran committees, built early AI programs, and crossed half a dozen disciplines. The unique payoff for a reader is seeing abstract concepts about thinking applied at full scale to real decisions, which makes the tools concrete and teaches that disciplinary boundaries are mostly artifacts of habit rather than features of how problems actually behave.

## Memorable principles and examples (paraphrased)
- The two watchmakers: one assembles watches from stable subassemblies and finishes many; the other builds each watch in one fragile sequence and, because interruptions reset his work, almost never finishes. Build with checkpoints.
- A pair of scissors cuts because of two blades. Behavior is the joint product of the mind's limits and the shape of the environment, so explaining a choice by intelligence alone misses half the cause.
- Faced with too many possible moves, a strong player does not examine them all. They notice a few patterns built from years of stored chunks and search only the promising branches, which is why expertise looks like intuition but is really compiled search.
- Choosing what to ignore is itself a design decision. In an information-rich world, the scarce resource is the attention that information consumes, so a good system is mostly a good filter.
- A career is a maze: each accepted path quietly closes the others. Simon's lesson is to satisfice the choice, commit, and keep moving rather than freeze in front of an unsolvable optimization.
