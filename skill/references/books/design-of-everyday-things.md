# The Design of Everyday Things, Don Norman

## Thesis
When a person struggles to use a thing, the fault usually sits in the design rather than in the person. Good design closes the gap between what a system actually does and what its user can perceive, understand, and predict, by putting the right cues in the world so people barely have to reason at all. Norman's deeper claim about thinking is that human action runs on cheap subconscious pattern-matching and on stories we invent after the fact, so reliable performance comes from arranging the environment to carry the load that memory and reasoning cannot. Treat confusion and error as diagnostic signals that point at a structural flaw, then redesign the structure rather than scolding the human.

## Thinking tools

1. **Blame the system rather than the person.** When someone fails at a task, the reflex is to label them careless or stupid, which ends inquiry and leaves the cause untouched. Norman's move is to invert the default: assume the structure made the failure likely, and that any competent person in the same setup would trip the same way. Apply it by asking "what about the design invited this mistake?" before you ask "who messed up?" The test is whether others repeat the error; if they do, you have a system problem masquerading as a personal one.

2. **Separate affordances from signifiers.** An affordance is what an object actually permits (a chair affords sitting, a slot affords inserting), while a signifier is the visible cue that tells a person where and how to act. Confusing the two lets you ship a thing that is technically usable but unreadable, because the possible action exists with no sign pointing to it. Apply it by auditing any tool in two passes: first list what it genuinely allows, then check whether each allowed action is signaled clearly enough that a stranger would find it without instructions. Missing signifiers rather than missing capability, are usually what frustrate people.

3. **Diagnose with the seven stages of action.** Norman breaks any action into a loop: form a goal, plan, specify the steps, execute, perceive the result, interpret it, and compare it against the goal. Most usability and reasoning failures localize cleanly to one stage, so naming the stage tells you what to fix. Apply it by walking a confused user (or yourself) through the loop and finding where it breaks: a person who cannot figure out what to do has an execution-side gap, while a person who acts but cannot tell whether it worked has an evaluation-side gap.

4. **Close the gulfs of execution and evaluation.** The gulf of execution is the distance between your intention and the actions the system makes available; the gulf of evaluation is the distance between the system's state and your ability to read that state. Both gulfs are bridgeable by design, and naming which gulf is open tells you which kind of cue is missing. Apply it by asking two questions of any interface or process: "Can the person tell what they can do here?" and "Can the person tell what just happened?" The first failure needs better signifiers and constraints; the second needs better feedback.

5. **Demand feedback, fast and informative.** People keep a running internal prediction of what their action will cause, and without timely confirmation they cannot tell success from failure, so they repeat, escalate, or give up. Feedback that is too slow gets ignored, and feedback that is too loud trains people to silence it. Apply it by ensuring every consequential action produces an immediate, legible signal of its result, calibrated to the stakes so it informs without nagging. When you analyze a breakdown, check first whether the person ever received clear evidence of what their action did.

6. **Match the conceptual model to a good mental model.** A person operates anything by running a mental model, a simplified internal story of how it works, and that story comes from whatever the system shows them, which Norman calls the system image. When the visible cues imply one model but the machine behaves by another, the user predicts wrong and feels stupid. Apply it by designing the visible behavior so the story a reasonable person infers is the true story, and when diagnosing confusion, surface the user's actual model and find where it diverges from reality.

7. **Move knowledge into the world when memory is unreliable.** Knowledge in the head is fast but fragile and unavailable when you need it; knowledge in the world is slower to read but always present and needs no rehearsal. The practical lever is to externalize the cues so precise behavior emerges from imprecise memory. Apply it by writing things down, putting the next step where the eye will land, and shaping objects so their form encodes their use, the way pilots enter instructions into equipment as they hear them instead of trusting recall.

8. **Engineer constraints so the wrong action becomes hard or impossible.** Rather than relying on people to remember rules, build physical, cultural, semantic, and logical limits that channel behavior toward the only sensible option. A part that fits one way only, a connector that cannot be inserted backward, a step that cannot be skipped: these prevent error without vigilance. Apply it by asking, for any error-prone step, "what constraint would make the mistake physically awkward or logically dead-ended?" and then build that constraint in rather than adding a warning label.

9. **Use natural mapping to make controls self-explaining.** When the spatial layout of controls mirrors the layout of the things they affect (a stove where each knob sits in the pattern of the burner it controls), people operate correctly on the first try with no learning. Arbitrary mappings force memorization and breed slips. Apply it by arranging controls, options, or menu items so their physical or visual relationship matches the structure of what they govern, and when a control keeps getting confused, suspect a broken mapping before you suspect the user.

10. **Distinguish slips from mistakes before fixing anything.** A slip is a correct intention executed wrong (you meant to do the right thing and your hands did something else); a mistake is the wrong intention formed from a bad goal or wrong model. They have different causes and different cures, so collapsing them wastes the fix. Apply it by asking whether the person had the right plan: if yes and the action went astray, redesign for slips with constraints and feedback; if the plan itself was wrong, redesign for mistakes with clearer information and better conceptual models.

11. **Solve the correct problem before solving any problem.** Problems in the real world arrive as surface symptoms, and a brilliant solution to the stated problem can be worse than nothing if the stated problem is the wrong one. The disciplined move is to treat the given problem as a hypothesis and deliberately widen before narrowing. Apply it by resisting the pull to start solving, instead studying what people are actually trying to accomplish, generating many framings of the underlying issue, and only then converging. Ask repeatedly "is this the real problem, or a symptom of one underneath?"

12. **Run the Five Whys to reach the root cause.** Asking "why" once gets you a proximate cause that still sits near the surface; chaining the question roughly five times drives past symptoms to the structural origin worth fixing. The mechanism works because each answer exposes the assumption behind it, which you can then interrogate. Apply it to any failure by writing the immediate cause, asking why that happened, and repeating until you hit a cause that is a design or process decision you can actually change. Stop when fixing the current answer would genuinely prevent recurrence.

13. **Treat failure as information and iterate.** Norman frames early failure as the fastest teacher, which is why good design proceeds by repeated cycles of prototype, observe, and revise rather than one careful leap. The mechanism is that real use reveals gaps no amount of armchair reasoning predicts, so you want those gaps to surface cheaply and early. Apply it by building rough versions, putting them in front of real people, watching where they stumble, and using each stumble as a precise pointer to the next change instead of a verdict on whether the idea was good.

14. **Trust observation over what people say they do.** People narrate their behavior with after-the-fact stories that rationalize choices the subconscious already made, so self-report is an unreliable guide to actual use. The corrective is to watch behavior directly, especially the moments of hesitation, workaround, and error that users themselves rarely mention because they blame themselves. Apply it by observing real tasks in real settings, paying special attention to the silent struggles and improvised fixes, and weighting what you see above what you are told.

15. **Forgive error in advance by designing for recovery.** Since humans err continually as an intrinsic trait, a robust system assumes mistakes will happen and limits their cost: it makes actions reversible, confirms before irreversible steps, and treats user difficulty as a signal of where to improve rather than a reason for an error message. Apply it by replacing scolding messages with guidance that lets the person continue from where they are, never forcing a restart, and by asking of any design "when this fails, how bad is it and how easily can the person undo it?"

## Named concepts, biases, fallacies, and techniques
- **Affordance**: the relationship between an object and a user that determines what actions are possible.
- **Signifier**: any perceivable cue, mark, or sound that tells a person where and how to act.
- **Mapping**: the relationship between controls and their effects; natural mapping uses spatial or analogical correspondence.
- **Feedback**: immediate, legible confirmation of the result of an action.
- **Conceptual model**: a person's simplified internal story of how something works, used to predict its behavior.
- **System image**: everything the product visibly presents, from which the user builds their conceptual model.
- **Gulf of execution**: the gap between a user's intention and the actions the system makes available.
- **Gulf of evaluation**: the gap between the system's state and the user's ability to perceive and interpret it.
- **Seven stages of action**: goal, plan, specify, execute, perceive, interpret, compare, used to localize where action breaks down.
- **Three levels of processing**: visceral (automatic), behavioral (learned skill), reflective (conscious meaning).
- **Knowledge in the head vs. in the world**: the tradeoff between fragile internal memory and reliable external cues.
- **Constraints**: physical, cultural, semantic, and logical limits that channel behavior and block error.
- **Slip**: a right intention executed wrongly (action, memory-lapse, mode error, capture, description-similarity).
- **Mistake**: a wrong intention formed from a faulty goal, plan, or model.
- **Human error as system error**: most "human error" traces to design that made the error likely.
- **Root-cause analysis and the Five Whys**: chaining "why" to pass symptoms and reach the fixable origin.
- **Double-diamond model**: diverge then converge, twice, first to find the right problem and then the right solution.
- **Human-centered design (HCD)**: an iterative process grounded in real human needs and capabilities.
- **Poka-yoke**: error-proofing fixtures that make incorrect operation physically impossible.
- **Swiss cheese model**: accidents happen when holes in successive layers of defense line up.
- **Forcing function**: a constraint that halts progress until a required step is completed (lockout, lock-in, interlock).
- **Post-hoc rationalization**: the mind inventing conscious reasons after subconscious decisions are already made.
- **Paradox of technology**: added features increase capability and complexity at the same time, raising the chance of confusion.

## What this book uniquely contributes
This book grounds critical thinking in the physical and cognitive realities of how people actually act, which most reasoning books skip. Its central reframe, that failure is usually a property of structure rather than character, gives a reader a concrete protocol for redirecting blame into diagnosis: name the stage of action that broke, find the open gulf, supply the missing cue. Where other works teach you to scrutinize arguments and beliefs, Norman teaches you to scrutinize the environment that shapes behavior, and to externalize cognition into the world so good outcomes survive bad memory. The vocabulary of affordances, signifiers, mappings, feedback, constraints, slips, and mistakes equips a thinker to debug any human-system breakdown with precision instead of vague frustration.

## Memorable principles and examples (paraphrased)
- **The Norman door.** A glass door that needs a sign reading "push" is a confession of failed design, because the hardware should already tell your hand what to do. A flat plate affords pushing and signals it; a handle invites pulling. When a designer adds a label to compensate for misleading shape, the object has failed at its one communicative job.
- **The stove with scrambled knobs.** Four burners in a square but four knobs in a row force people to memorize an arbitrary code and guess wrong forever. Lay the knobs out in the same pattern as the burners and the mapping becomes self-evident, so nobody ever turns on the wrong ring again. Structure carries the knowledge that memory should not have to.
- **Return versus Enter.** Norman found users of a system constantly destroying their work by hitting one near-identical key instead of another, yet every user blamed their own clumsiness and never reported it, while the designer insisted no one had the problem. The fix was never more training; it was a design that did not let a single reflexive keystroke wipe out minutes of work.
- **How pilots remember rapid-fire instructions.** Faced with a torrent of headings, altitudes, and codes, pilots do not rely on superhuman recall; they write the numbers down or dial them straight into the equipment as they hear them. They convert fleeting knowledge in the head into stable knowledge in the world, which is why a good cockpit is designed to absorb information the instant it arrives.
- **Fail often, fail fast.** A design firm treats early breakdowns as the price of discovery, because each failure points exactly at what to change next. A person or team that never fails is playing it too safe to learn anything worth knowing, so the cycle of build, watch people stumble, and revise is the engine of every good outcome.
