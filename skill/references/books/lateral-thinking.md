# Lateral Thinking, Edward de Bono

## Thesis
The mind is a self-organizing pattern system that locks information into fixed grooves, so deliberate provocation and pattern-breaking techniques are needed to restructure old ideas and generate genuinely new ones.

## Why the book exists (the mechanism behind every tool)
De Bono models the mind as a self-maximizing memory surface that builds patterns, then routes new input down those grooves automatically. This makes recognition fast and reaction efficient. The same property makes the patterns hard to rewire: a pattern controls where attention goes, so you keep seeing what the pattern primes you to see. Vertical thinking (logic, sequential proof) digs the existing hole deeper. Lateral thinking digs a hole somewhere else. Both are required. The tools below are deliberate procedures to escape the dominant pattern long enough for the information to settle into a better one. A recurring rule across all of them: judge less while generating, and accept being temporarily wrong or irrelevant if it opens a path to a better structure.

## Thinking tools

1. **Vertical vs. lateral thinking (know which mode you are in).** Vertical thinking is selective, sequential, and every step must be justified; it develops and proves a chosen idea. Lateral thinking is generative, can move sideways, can be provisionally wrong, and welcomes the irrelevant; it restructures and provokes. The mechanism: a logically sound chain still rests on a starting concept that perceptual choice handed you, and logic cannot inspect that choice. Apply it by naming your current mode before you work. When stuck after digging deeper, stop refining and switch to generation. Use vertical thinking afterward to test and build out whatever lateral thinking produced.

2. **Generation of alternatives with a quota.** Deliberately produce several different ways to see a situation, even after a good one appears. The mechanism: the first acceptable interpretation suppresses all others, so general intent to "stay open" fails under difficulty. Apply it by setting a fixed number (find five readings of this picture, five restatements of this problem) and meeting the quota whether or not you already like an answer. The goal is loosening the grip of the obvious, so generate for its own sake rather than stopping at "best."

3. **Challenging assumptions.** Treat every taken-for-granted boundary or concept as a pattern that can be rebuilt, including the ones everyone agrees on. The mechanism: most assumptions survive on historical continuity rather than on repeated checks of whether they still hold; self-imposed limits (drawn only for convenience) can make a solvable problem look impossible. Apply it by asking which limits you assumed, whether they are real, and what happens if you step outside them. The nine-dot puzzle (only four lines stay inside the square of dots) is the canonical case: the boundary was never in the instructions.

4. **The "why" technique.** Ask why about things you already think you understand, then question the explanation itself, and the explanation of that. The mechanism: ordinary "why" seeks a comforting familiar answer and stops; this version refuses comfort to keep the pattern unsettled. Apply it by treating no answer as final and directing each "why" at a specific part of the prior answer rather than firing a blanket "why." Accept that a historical reason explains how something started rather than why it should continue.

5. **Suspended judgement.** Hold off on evaluating an idea so it can survive long enough to lead somewhere, and let an idea be useful for its effect even when it is wrong as a conclusion. The mechanism: premature judgement kills provocative ideas before they can trigger restructuring; a wrong idea can be a stepping stone to a right one. Apply it by separating the generation phase from the judgement phase. During generation, defer "is this correct?" and ask "where does this lead?"

6. **Design (build solutions, do more than just analyze).** Produce arrangements for a chosen purpose (organize a service, design a device) instead of only describing or critiquing what exists. The mechanism: open-ended design forces fresh assembly of parts and exposes how many workable structures exist, which analysis alone hides. Apply it by restating problems as "design X to achieve Y," generating multiple complete designs, and comparing the structures rather than defending the first.

7. **Spotting and escaping the dominant idea (and crucial factors).** In any situation one idea quietly organizes everything else; alongside it sit factors you assume are fixed and mandatory. The mechanism: the dominant idea funnels all thinking down one channel, so you never reach alternatives. Apply it by explicitly naming the ruling idea in a problem so you can set it aside, and separately listing the "crucial" factors you have been treating as untouchable to test whether they truly bind.

8. **Fractionation (break the whole into parts you can recombine).** Split a problem into smaller pieces, since the division is yours to choose and any split can be redrawn. The mechanism: a problem held as one solid block resists restructuring, while pieces can be regrouped into a new pattern. Apply it by carving the situation into two or more fractions, then recombining them differently. The split does not need to be "correct"; its value is the new arrangements it enables.

9. **The reversal method.** Take the situation as it is and turn it inside out (reverse the direction, the order, the roles, the cause and effect), then see where the absurd arrangement leads. The mechanism: reversal forcibly ejects you from the natural line of development so the pattern has to reassemble. Apply it to provoke movement rather than to find truth; the reversed version is a launch point, and most reversals lead somewhere only after you follow them.

10. **Brainstorming as a structured setting.** Use a group setting whose rules are deferred judgement, free cross-stimulation, and welcomed off-line ideas. The mechanism: other people's contributions act as inputs that do not follow your own train of thought, and the no-criticism rule keeps provocative ideas alive. Apply it by enforcing the no-evaluation rule during the session, treating every contribution (including ones you disagree with) as raw stimulation, and saving judgement for a separate later pass.

11. **Analogies.** Map your problem onto an unrelated story or system that has its own line of development, then carry that development back. The mechanism: the analogy supplies movement and structure that your stuck pattern lacks, generating moves you would not have produced directly. Apply it by picking a concrete process (a snowball rolling, a queue forming), letting it unfold on its own terms, and importing each turn back to your problem.

12. **Choice of entry point and attention area.** Where you start and what you choose to look at largely determine the answer you reach. The mechanism: attention is limited and settles on the most obvious area, and whatever you leave out gives no signal that it is missing. Apply it by deliberately starting from a different point (work a problem backward from the goal, or from a side detail) and by rotating attention over every part, especially the parts that seem to deserve none.

13. **Random stimulation.** Inject a deliberately unconnected input (a dictionary word, a random object, an unrelated exhibit) and force a link between it and your problem. The mechanism: in a self-organizing memory system two inputs held in attention together will eventually connect, so a random word becomes a new entry point or an analogy that disturbs the stable pattern. Apply it by generating the input with no selection at all (true randomness; selecting a "suitable" word destroys the effect), giving yourself about three minutes with quiet confidence that something will emerge, and following the chain of associations into the problem.

14. **The word "po" (provocation and pattern-escape).** De Bono's deliberately meaningless signal that marks a statement as a provocation to think past, rather than a claim to be judged true or false. The mechanism: language is built for yes/no judgement, which immediately routes any new arrangement into an existing box; "po" suspends that judgement so an arrangement can exist long enough to be useful. Apply it by floating an idea as a provocation ("po, cars have square wheels") and exploring its consequences instead of asking whether it is correct.

## Named biases, fallacies, and traps to counter

- **Pattern lock-in (rigidity of the self-organizing mind).** The very efficiency that lets you recognize and react fast is what makes established patterns resist change; combining or extending patterns is easy, restructuring them is hard.
- **The dominance of the obvious interpretation.** Once a situation is read one way, the alternative readings are ignored and start to feel silly. Countered by the quota method and altered/partial views that strip away the obvious.
- **Self-imposed boundaries and the cry of "cheating."** Whenever a solution that steps outside an assumed limit draws an accusation of cheating, it reveals a boundary that was never actually required.
- **Polarization (the grey-ball trap).** New information gets shoved into one of a few existing categories and then looks identical to everything else in that box; nothing ever forces a new category to be created, and the fewer the categories, the larger the distortion. Countered by challenging labels, doing without a label to see what lies under it, and creating new categories on purpose.
- **The tyranny of the fixed label/name.** A name freezes a unit so it can be communicated, which is useful, and the same permanence makes the unit hard to redraw when it stops fitting. The rice-and-maize famine is the example: the label "maize is animal food" can starve people who refuse the only food available.
- **Myth words.** Once you have names you can manufacture new words (add "un-", fuse two words) that then dictate how reality is seen, even though nothing in the environment produced them. Treat such words as suspects rather than as facts.
- **Blocked by openness.** Having an adequate answer in hand blocks the search for a better one; the open, "good enough" path is itself a trap because it removes the pressure to restructure.

## What this book uniquely contributes
De Bono reframes creativity from a mystique you can only admire in the result to a process you can practise like arithmetic. The contribution is mechanistic: he grounds the need for creative thinking in how a pattern-forming mind actually behaves, then supplies deliberate, repeatable procedures (quota, challenge, reversal, random word, po) for escaping those patterns on demand. He legitimizes being deliberately wrong, irrelevant, and provocative as a working method, and he separates idea generation from idea judgement so the two stop sabotaging each other. He also coins shared vocabulary (lateral thinking, po) that makes the move teachable and discussable.

## Memorable principles worth keeping (paraphrased)

- **Digging the same hole deeper never relocates it.** Refining one approach with more rigor cannot reach an answer that lives in a different place; you have to start digging elsewhere.
- **A name freezes a thing for good and for ill.** The label that lets you carry an idea around also welds the idea into a shape that may have stopped fitting the world; question the label even while you keep using it.
- **In a connected mind nothing is truly irrelevant.** Because any two things held in attention together will eventually form a link, a random dictionary word can crack open a housing-shortage problem; the trick is to take the input unselected, since the moment you pick a "relevant" one you have lost the provocation.
- **An idea can be valuable for where it sends you, even when it is false as an answer.** Suspend the reflex to judge, follow the wrong-looking move, and let it become a stepping stone to the structure you could not see from the start.
