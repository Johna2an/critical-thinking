# How to Lie with Statistics, Darrell Huff

## Thesis
A number wearing the costume of precision can still be empty or deceptive, so every statistic deserves a structured second look that traces it back to its sample, its definitions, its base, and the unstated incentives of whoever produced it.

## Thinking tools

1. **Trace the statistic to its sample.** Every average, percentage, or trend rests on a sample, and the sample is the ceiling on how good the conclusion can be. A polished decimal cannot rise above the quality of the raw selection beneath it. Apply it by asking who actually got counted and who fell out before you weigh any figure. The Yale "average income of $25,111" collapses once you notice the sample was only graduates whose addresses were known and who chose to reply, which silently dropped the men who fell on hard times.

2. **Hunt for self-selection bias.** When respondents choose whether to participate, the people who opt in differ systematically from the people who opt out, so the sample tilts toward whoever cared enough to answer. The mechanism is that non-response is rarely random. To apply it, treat any mail-in coupon, volunteer survey, or "readers who wrote in" result as suspect, and ask what kind of person would have thrown the questionnaire away. The newspaper poll claiming 98 percent metric-system awareness was inflated because the uninformed simply never clipped the coupon.

3. **Demand the response rate.** A projection built from the minority who replied can be eleven times too large when the silent majority systematically differs. The mechanism: non-responders often share the very trait being measured (no conversions to report, low income to admit). Apply it by always asking "out of how many sent?" before trusting any projection from a survey. The claim that millions of Catholics turned Protestant rested on roughly 9 percent of ministers replying, and the missing 91 percent most plausibly had nothing to report.

4. **Ask which average it is: mean, median, or mode.** "Average" is a loose word covering three different numbers that can sit far apart on skewed data. The mean is the arithmetic total divided by count and gets dragged upward by a few large values; the median is the middle value where half lie above and half below; the mode is the most common value. Apply it by refusing any unqualified "average" for income, wealth, or anything lopsided. On a street with three millionaire weekenders, the mean income flatters and the median tells the honest middle, which is why nearly everybody there can sit below the "average."

5. **Insist on the range and the spread.** A single central figure hides how widely the data scatter, and the spread often carries the decision-relevant information. The mechanism: two groups with the same average can differ enormously in variability. Apply it by asking for the range, the deviation, or the distribution shape, especially before acting on a figure. A child whose development sits below the stated average may be perfectly normal once you see the range of normal, so a lone midpoint can needlessly alarm.

6. **Check the margin of error and sample size.** Every sampled estimate carries a probable error, and figures quoted without that band pretend to a certainty they lack. The mechanism: small samples produce wide, unstable estimates, and percentages drawn from tiny counts swing wildly. Apply it by asking how large the sample was and what the plus-or-minus is. A reported "4.9 percent" of job offers turned out to rest on two cases out of forty-one, where the raw count would have been more honest than the decimal.

7. **Strip away false precision.** Extra decimal places manufacture an aura of exactness that the underlying measurement never supported. The mechanism: carrying a rough input to three decimals dresses up a guess as a measurement. Apply it by treating suspiciously precise figures as a warning sign rather than a credential. Asking a hundred people how long they slept and reporting 7.831 hours sounds authoritative, yet the inputs were rough recollections, so "about 8 hours" was the only honest version.

8. **Read the graph's axis before its shape.** A chart can amplify or flatten the same trend by stretching, compressing, or truncating the vertical scale. The mechanism: chopping the bottom off the y-axis turns a 10 percent rise into a cliff (the "gee-whiz" graph). Apply it by checking where the axis starts, whether it is labeled, and whether zero appears, before you absorb the visual impression. A modest climb can be made to look explosive purely by where the artist begins the scale.

9. **Watch one-dimensional pictures inflate two ways.** When a pictograph scales a drawing by both height and width to represent a single quantity, doubling the figure roughly quadruples the visible area, overstating the change. The mechanism: the eye reads area, while the data only justify a change in one dimension. Apply it by distrusting moneybag or barrel icons that grow in every direction. A money symbol drawn twice as tall is also twice as wide, so it looks four times larger for a value that only doubled.

10. **Catch the semi-attached figure.** A speaker proves one thing and then asserts a related but different thing the number does not actually support. The mechanism: a true statistic about A gets quietly swapped for a louder claim about B. Apply it by checking whether the figure cited measures the exact claim being made. A medicine "proven" effective in a lab dish, then advertised as curing colds in people, swaps a real result for an unearned one.

11. **Break the post hoc / correlation-causation reflex.** Two things moving together do not establish that one causes the other. The mechanism splits into cases: pure chance, a hidden third factor driving both, reversed direction, or genuine but mutual causation. Apply it by listing rival explanations before accepting the offered one, and by asking whether a lurking variable explains both series. Smokers earning lower grades does not mean smoking dulls minds, since an underlying trait like sociability could drive both, and the rum-price-versus-minister-salary link rises only because general prices rose.

12. **Refuse to add or chain percentages naively.** Percentages computed on different bases cannot be summed or layered as if they shared one base. The mechanism: a 20 percent cut and a later 5 percent raise apply to different starting figures, so they do not net cleanly. Apply it by always asking "percentage of what base?" before combining figures. A "50 percent and 20 percent off" discount is 60 percent off rather than 70, because the second cut applies to the already-reduced price, and a 50 percent pay cut needs a 100 percent raise to undo it.

13. **Separate percentage from percentage points.** A change measured in points and the same change measured as a percentage of the starting value tell two very different stories. The mechanism: a move from 3 percent to 6 percent is three percentage points and also a 100 percent increase, and a manipulator picks whichever sounds better. Apply it by clarifying which unit is being used, especially with polls, returns, and rates. The smaller-sounding "three points" and the dramatic "doubled" describe the identical move.

14. **Run the five questions on any statistic.** Before trusting a figure, interrogate it: Who says so (look for conscious bias and the borrowed "O.K. name" of a respected institution)? How do they know (sample, method, response rate)? What is missing (the base, the range, the comparison year, the raw counts)? Did somebody swap the subject (semi-attached figure, shifted units)? Does it make sense (sanity-check against common experience and known totals)? Apply this as a standing checklist on every reported number. A conclusion attributed to "Cornell" often came from a writer who only borrowed Cornell's data, so confirm the authority stands behind the claim and not merely beside it.

## Named concepts, biases, fallacies, and techniques
- **Built-in / selection bias**, a sample skewed by how members were chosen, so it cannot represent the whole. Detect it by asking who was systematically left out.
- **Self-selection (volunteer) bias**, participants opt in, so the group over-represents whoever cared to respond. Classic in mail-ins, online polls, and volunteer studies.
- **Non-response bias**, the people who do not answer differ from those who do; a low response rate quietly distorts projections.
- **The "O.K. name" maneuver**, borrowing the authority of a respected lab or university to lend weight to a conclusion that institution never endorsed.
- **Skew and the mean/median gap**, on lopsided distributions the mean drifts toward the long tail while the median stays central; the unqualified "average" exploits this gap.
- **Gee-whiz graph**, a truncated or stretched y-axis that exaggerates a trend.
- **One-dimensional pictograph**, scaling an icon in two dimensions to show a one-dimensional change, multiplying the visual impression.
- **Semi-attached figure**, proving one claim and asserting a different, unsupported one beside it.
- **Post hoc ergo propter hoc**, assuming sequence or correlation implies causation; covers chance correlation, lurking third variables, and reversed causation.
- **Shifting base / confusion of base**, computing related percentages on different starting values so they cannot be combined honestly.
- **Percentage vs. percentage points**, conflating an absolute point change with a relative percentage change.
- **False precision (the decimal bluff)**, extra decimals implying measurement accuracy that the data never had.
- **Statisticulation**, Huff's coined word for misinforming through statistical manipulation, whether by deceit or incompetence.
- **Map distortion**, shading large-area, low-population regions to overstate a quantity that has nothing to do with land area.
- **Geometric vs. arithmetic average**, switching average types (or the index base year) to make prices appear up, down, or unchanged from the same data.

## What this book uniquely contributes
It gives a non-mathematical reader a portable defense kit. Rather than teaching statistical computation, it teaches statistical skepticism as a literacy skill, the way reading and writing are skills, by cataloguing the recurring tricks in plain language and pairing each with a question that defuses it. Its lasting contribution is the move from passively receiving numbers to actively cross-examining them, condensed into the five-question routine that anyone can run on any figure in a newspaper, an ad, or a chart. It also reframes the producer's incentive structure: one-sided errors that always flatter the presenter are evidence of motive rather than innocent noise.

## Memorable principles or examples worth keeping
- A river cannot rise above its source, and a conclusion cannot be more trustworthy than the sample feeding it. Layers of arithmetic and a tidy decimal point cannot purify a biased or tiny sample beneath them. (Paraphrased.)
- On a road where three millionaires summer among modest farmers, an honest estate agent can quote a high "average" to a buyer and a low "average" to a tax committee using the very same incomes, simply by switching between the mean and the median. The word "average" alone tells you almost nothing. (Paraphrased.)
- Islanders once concluded that body lice cause good health because healthy people had lice and sick people often did not. The causation runs backward: when fever raised body temperature, the lice left, so the sickness drove the lice away rather than the lice driving the health. Correlation read backward becomes superstition. (Paraphrased.)
- When the errors in a set of figures all happen to favor the same side, blundering becomes an unconvincing excuse, because honest mistakes would scatter in both directions. Consistent one-sidedness points to motive. (Paraphrased.)
