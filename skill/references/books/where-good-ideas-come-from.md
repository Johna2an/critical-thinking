# Where Good Ideas Come From, Steven Johnson

## Thesis
Good ideas rarely arrive as sudden flashes from lone geniuses. They assemble slowly out of available parts, by recombining existing pieces in new ways, and they thrive in environments that connect, store, and remix fragments rather than wall them off. The way to think better, then, is to build the conditions that favor recombination: stay near the frontier of what is currently possible, keep half-finished thoughts alive, and let your ideas collide with other people's. Innovation is an environmental and ecological problem at least as much as a cognitive one.

## Thinking tools

1. **Work at the adjacent possible.** Every idea is built from the parts available right now, and those parts define a finite menu of next moves: the "adjacent possible." You cannot leap straight to a destination that requires components no one has invented yet; you can only reach the doors that the current configuration opens. To apply this, stop asking "what is the ultimate solution?" and start asking "what is one recombination of things I already have access to?" Each move you make unlocks new doors, so deliberately take the most generative next step, the one that expands future options rather than closing them.

2. **Cultivate the slow hunch.** Most important ideas do not snap into focus; they incubate for years as vague, incomplete intuitions. The danger is that a hunch is too faint to act on, so you discard it before it has time to mature and meet its missing half. To apply this, give your unresolved questions somewhere to live: keep a running notebook, a "stuck ideas" file, a folder of problems you cannot yet solve. Revisit them. Protect them from your own impatience for tidy conclusions, because the payoff often comes only after a long latency.

3. **Keep a commonplace book (write everything down).** Johnson revives the old practice of the commonplace book: a single place where you copy quotations, observations, and fragments you find interesting, then reread and cross-link them over time. The mechanism is that rereading old notes places present thinking next to past fragments, and proximity breeds collision. Apply it by maintaining one searchable, long-lived store of everything that catches your attention, and by browsing it nonlinearly so old and new entries can spark against each other.

4. **Let hunches collide (the liquid network).** A private hunch usually completes itself only when it meets a complementary hunch held by someone else. Networks that allow ideas to flow and connect, what he calls "liquid" environments, produce more breakthroughs than rigid or isolated ones. To apply this, get your half-formed thoughts out of your head and into conversation, shared documents, and mixed rooms. Talk about the problem before you have the answer. The point of sharing early is to find the other piece you are missing.

5. **Design for serendipity.** Serendipity is a useful collision between an idea you are carrying and information you were not looking for. You can engineer the odds. Apply it by deliberately introducing noise and breadth: read outside your field, take walks, sleep on problems (dreams loosen associations), keep diverse inputs flowing. The walk, the shower, the long reading binge are not breaks from thinking; they are the mechanism that lets distant fragments meet.

6. **Reframe error as a feature.** Being wrong can shake you out of the assumptions that block the right answer; noise and mistakes introduce variation that pure correctness never would. Many discoveries began as contaminated experiments, misread results, or accidents that the researcher chose to investigate instead of dismiss. To apply this, treat anomalies as data rather than embarrassments. When something fails in an interesting way, pause on it before you clean it up. Tune your tolerance for error toward the level that maximizes useful surprise.

7. **Mine exaptation (borrow from far fields).** Exaptation is taking a tool, idea, or trait built for one purpose and repurposing it for something unrelated, the way feathers evolved for warmth before they were used for flight. Cross-domain borrowing is one of the richest sources of breakthroughs. Apply it by asking, "What works in a totally different domain, and what would it do here?" Keep many hobbies and side interests; people with the widest range of pursuits have the deepest reservoir of parts to repurpose.

8. **Use the platform stack.** A platform is a stable layer that others can build on without rebuilding it, and stacking platforms compounds what becomes possible: coral reefs enable ecosystems, GPS enabled a generation of apps. Apply it by looking for stable foundations you can stand on instead of starting from zero, and by building your own work as a platform that others (or your future self) can extend. The most generative move is often to make the layer reusable rather than the product final.

9. **Build dense, diverse environments.** Innovation scales with the density and mixing of a space, which is why cities and the right kind of workplace out-create isolated rooms. The mechanism is more contacts per unit time, more accidental overlaps, more chances for hunches to meet. Apply it by raising the connection rate in your own surroundings: shared spaces over private silos, cross-team mixing over departmental walls, open floor plans of ideas where information leaks productively.

10. **Prefer generative openness to airtight protection.** Johnson's running argument is that environments which let ideas flow, copy, and recombine generate more good ideas than environments built mainly to fence them off. The trade is real: openness loses some short-term control to gain long-term fertility. Apply it by defaulting to sharing your work earlier and more widely than feels comfortable, and by judging a system by how easily ideas move through it rather than by how tightly each one is owned.

11. **Take the long zoom (multiple scales at once).** Look at the same problem across many timescales and levels at the same time: the chemical, the biological, the individual, the institutional, the civilizational. Patterns that are invisible at one scale jump out at another. Apply it by deliberately re-asking your question at a wider and a narrower frame. What does this look like over a decade rather than a quarter? At the level of one neuron rather than one company? The recurring shapes across scales are where the real insight hides.

12. **Connect more than you protect.** When you face a choice between guarding an idea and connecting it to others, lean toward connection. An idea that stays sealed cannot find its missing complement, recombine, or get exapted. Apply it concretely: publish the rough draft, demo the prototype, explain the half-solution to a colleague. Treat your ideas as ingredients in a shared system rather than possessions to be defended, and you will get more back than you give away.

13. **Read the 0-1 history.** Innovations have traceable origin stories, and studying how real breakthroughs actually happened (rather than the tidy myth) teaches you the conditions that produced them. The honest history almost always shows recombination, long incubation, borrowed parts, and dense networks rather than a solitary eureka. Apply it by reconstructing the genealogy of any idea you admire: what parts existed first, who connected them, how long it took. Then recreate those conditions instead of waiting for lightning.

14. **Stay near the edge rather than over it.** The most fertile state for a system, and a mind, sits between rigid order and pure chaos: structured enough to hold form, loose enough to recombine. Too much order freezes ideas in place; too much disorder prevents anything from sticking. Apply it by tuning your routines and inputs toward that border: enough discipline to keep returning to the problem, enough slack and novelty to keep generating variation.

## Named concepts, biases, fallacies, and techniques
- **Adjacent possible**: the set of next steps reachable from your current parts; you can only build doors that today's pieces open.
- **Slow hunch**: an idea that matures over years as a vague intuition before it becomes actionable.
- **Liquid network**: a connected, fluid environment where ideas flow and combine, contrasted with rigid or isolated ones.
- **Commonplace book**: a personal store of copied quotes and notes, reread and cross-linked to spark new connections.
- **Serendipity**: a productive collision between a problem you carry and information you were not seeking.
- **Exaptation**: repurposing something evolved or built for one use into an unrelated new use.
- **Platform / platform stacking**: stable reusable layers that others build on, compounding what becomes possible.
- **Generativity**: a system's capacity to produce unanticipated new ideas, favored by openness over enclosure.
- **The long zoom**: examining a phenomenon across many timescales and levels of organization simultaneously.
- **Stacked platforms in nature (coral reef, GPS)**: examples of foundational layers enabling rich downstream innovation.
- **Error as variation**: mistakes and noise introduce useful diversity that strict correctness suppresses.
- **The lone-genius myth**: the misleading folk belief that breakthroughs come from solitary flashes of insight.
- **Eureka myth / flash-of-insight bias**: overrating the sudden moment and underrating the long buildup behind it.
- **Density and connection rate**: the idea that more contacts per unit time (cities, mixed teams) raise innovation output.

## What this book uniquely contributes
The book's distinctive move is to treat creativity as an ecological and environmental question rather than a personality trait. Instead of asking what kind of person has good ideas, it asks what kind of space, network, and time horizon manufactures them, and it answers with one toolkit that holds across biology, cities, software, and individual minds. By naming patterns like the adjacent possible, the slow hunch, and exaptation, it gives you concrete levers you can pull on your own environment, turning "be more creative" from a wish into a set of engineering choices about connection, storage, error tolerance, and timescale.

## Memorable principles and examples (paraphrased)
- Charles Darwin's theory looks like a single revelation, but his own notebooks show the idea surfacing in fragments long before he could articulate it. The breakthrough was a slow hunch finally clicking into place, which is why he wrote everything down for years.
- Coral reefs hold an outsized share of ocean life because the reef itself is a platform: one organism builds a stable structure that countless others can then colonize and build upon. Stable layers breed abundance on top of them.
- A swirl of inputs and recombination, dense cafe culture and freely circulating fragments, tends to out-create the sealed laboratory, because more pieces are touching more often and finding their missing complements.
- GPS began as a Cold War tracking effort and was later opened up, after which a generation of unrelated products built on top of it. A platform's value showed up mostly in the uses its creators never imagined.
- Feathers were useful for insulation before any animal flew with them. The same part, kept around for one reason, became the key to an entirely different capability. That is exaptation, and it is how a lot of good ideas reach their second, larger life.
