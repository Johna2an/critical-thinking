# Thinking from A to Z, Nigel Warburton

## Thesis
Clear thinking is a trainable skill built from a vocabulary of named moves: once you can label the argument structures, fallacies, persuasion tricks, and mental habits in play, you can spot bad reasoning fast and make your own reasoning sound.

## How to read this distillation
Warburton organizes critical thinking as an alphabetized field guide of about 100 short entries. The power comes from naming things. When a flawed move has a name, you recognize it in the wild and resist it. The four families of entry are: common argument moves, seductive reasoning errors, techniques of persuasion and avoidance, and psychological obstacles to clear thought. The tools below regroup the most actionable of these.

## Thinking tools

**1. Map the argument: premises, conclusion, and the hidden assumption (enthymeme).**
Every argument reduces to premises offered in support of a conclusion. The conclusion is the main claim, and it often appears first rather than last, so do not assume the final sentence is the point. Most real arguments are enthymemes: they leave a premise unstated. To evaluate any argument, restate it as numbered premises plus conclusion, then surface the suppressed premise that the conclusion silently depends on. Apply it by asking "what would have to be true, but is not said, for this conclusion to follow?" The hidden premise is usually where the weakness hides.

**2. Separate validity from truth, and demand soundness.**
Validity is a property of structure: an argument is valid when true premises would force a true conclusion. Truth is a property of the individual premises. A sound argument is valid and has true premises, so its conclusion is guaranteed. The trap is that a valid argument can have a false conclusion (because a premise is false), and a true conclusion can come from a broken argument. Apply it by checking two things separately: does the conclusion follow from the premises (structure), and are the premises actually true (content)? Reject anything that fails either test even when you like the conclusion.

**3. Know the four conditional forms (two valid, two fallacies).**
Arguments built on "if p then q" come in four shapes, and confusing them is one of the most common reasoning errors. Affirming the antecedent (p, therefore q) is valid. Denying the consequent (not q, therefore not p) is valid. Affirming the consequent (q, therefore p) is a fallacy. Denying the antecedent (not p, therefore not q) is a fallacy. Both fallacies work by secretly reading "if" as "if and only if," which ignores that the consequent can have other causes. Apply it by spotting the two bad forms: whenever someone reasons backward from an effect to one specific cause, ask whether other causes could produce the same effect.

**4. Hunt for alternative explanations before accepting any causal claim.**
A single explanation that fits the facts feels conclusive, especially when it is the one you want to be true. That feeling is unreliable, because many rival explanations can fit the same observations. Apply it by listing at least two other accounts of the same evidence before you accept the first one. The film did not develop: bad film, poor processing, and a lens cap left on are all live options alongside accidental exposure. Belief in the improbable is justified only after the ordinary explanations have been eliminated.

**5. Resist correlation-equals-cause and post hoc reasoning.**
Two things appearing together, or one following the other in time, does not establish that one causes the other. The correlation can come from a common third cause, from coincidence (especially with few data points), or from reversed causation. Larger shoe size tracks larger vocabulary because both grow with age rather than because feet teach words. Apply it by demanding a plausible mechanism for how the cause produces the effect, and by checking whether a hidden common cause drives both. Treat correlation as the first clue in a causal investigation, never as the verdict.

**6. Refute with counterexamples and the absurd-consequences move.**
To break a sweeping generalization, produce one genuine case that violates it. "No great women scientists" falls to Marie Curie alone. To break a principle, follow it to a conclusion the proposer would reject: if "lock away anyone who uses a mind-altering drug" is taken seriously, it jails everyone who has had alcohol, which is absurd, so the principle needs narrowing. Apply both by stress-testing claims: search rash "all" statements for a single exception, and push proposed rules to their logical extreme to see if they explode. The honest response to a real counterexample is to revise or drop the claim, downgrading "all" to "some" or "most."

**7. Test analogies by hunting for disanalogies.**
Arguments from analogy claim that because two things are alike in some respects, they are alike in another. They are only as strong as the relevant similarities. Apply it by listing the ways the two cases differ and asking whether any difference is relevant to the conclusion being drawn. Comparing heroin to an occasional glass of wine collapses once you note the differences in health risk, addiction, and legality. Irrelevant differences do not weaken an analogy, so focus on disanalogies that bear directly on the point. The same scrutiny defeats the domino effect and slippery slope, which are analogies dressed as inevitability.

**8. Play devil's advocate and run thought experiments.**
Strengthen a view by attacking it with the strongest possible objection, even one you do not believe. This finds the loopholes before an opponent does, and a position that survives sustained assault is probably solid. A thought experiment is an imagined, often far-fetched scenario built to isolate one intuition: Nozick's experience machine tests whether you truly value pleasure above everything by asking if you would plug in for life. Apply both by deliberately arguing the other side of your own position, and by inventing a clean hypothetical that strips a question down to the single variable you care about. Dismissing a thought experiment for being unrealistic misses its purpose.

**9. Spot the diversion tactics: red herring, straw man, ad hominem, and the politician's answer.**
A cluster of moves wins arguments by avoiding them. A red herring drags attention to an irrelevant issue. A straw man attacks a caricature of the opponent's view rather than the real one. Getting personal (ad hominem) shifts focus from the claim to the claimant's character, which is irrelevant to whether the claim is true. The politician's answer dodges the question asked and answers an easier one. Apply it by repeatedly asking "is this actually responding to the point on the table?" and by restating your opponent's view in its strongest form before you reply, which is the opposite of building a straw man.

**10. Disarm loaded questions and false dichotomies.**
A complex (loaded) question smuggles in an assumption you have not granted, so any short answer concedes it: "will you keep behaving like a brat?" traps you whether you say yes or no. A false dichotomy presents two options as if they were the only ones when a third exists. Apply it by refusing the frame: reject the buried assumption explicitly and answer the parts separately, and when handed an either/or, ask whether the choices are genuinely exhaustive. Many manipulations work entirely by controlling the menu of options you think you have.

**11. Police authority, consensus, and expertise.**
Truth does not flow from who said it or how many agree. Truth by authority treats a claim as true because an expert asserts it, yet experts are fallible, sometimes disagree, and are reliable only inside their actual field. Universal expertise is the error of trusting a brilliant physicist on morality or society. Truth by consensus and the democratic fallacy treat agreement or a majority vote as a truth detector, which fails when the crowd is uninformed. Apply it by weighting an opinion by the speaker's relevant expertise and the quality of their reasons rather than their fame or their numbers, and by seeking a second opinion where evidence is contested.

**12. Detect language manipulation: emotive words, weasel words, and pseudo-profundity.**
Word choice does argumentative work before any reasoning happens. Emotive language ("murder" for capital punishment, "scroungers" for the homeless) loads a conclusion into a label and short-circuits debate. Weasel words and vagueness let a speaker seem to commit while staying uncommittable. Pseudo-profundity and gobbledygook dress empty claims as deep ones. Apply it by translating loaded terms into neutral ones and seeing if the argument still stands, and by pressing vague claims for the specific, checkable commitment hiding behind them. Watch especially for spurious "therefore" and "so," which assert a conclusion without supplying the argument that would earn the word.

**13. Catch the costly self-deceptions: wishful thinking and the sunk cost fallacy.**
Some errors come from inside. Wishful thinking is believing something because you want it true, and it underlies many of the fallacies above. The sunk cost fallacy is continuing to invest in a failing project because of what you already spent, when the only rational question is the future return from here. Apply it by separating "what I want to be true" from "what the evidence supports," and by asking, when tempted to keep going, "would I start this today knowing what I now know?" Past investment is gone and should not steer the next decision.

**14. Guard the definitions: dictionary limits, stipulation, and the Socratic fallacy.**
Disputes often stall on words. A dictionary records how a word is used and cannot settle a theoretical question like "what is art?" or "what is justice?" A stipulative definition declares how you will use a term for the current discussion, which prevents confusion when a word is vague or used unusually. The Socratic fallacy is insisting you cannot use a term at all until you have a perfect definition of it, which is too strong. Apply it by stipulating key terms up front in any careful argument, by not treating the dictionary as the final judge of substantive questions, and by allowing useful working concepts even without airtight definitions.

## Named concepts, biases, fallacies, and techniques (quick reference)

- **Affirming the consequent / denying the antecedent**: the two invalid conditional forms; both ignore that an effect can have multiple causes.
- **Affirming the antecedent (modus ponens) / denying the consequent (modus tollens)**: the two valid conditional forms.
- **Ad hoc clause**: a patch bolted onto a failing hypothesis to dodge a refuting fact; acceptable in moderation, dishonest when it neuters the claim's generality.
- **Enthymeme**: an argument with a suppressed premise; find it to test the argument.
- **Begging the question / circular argument**: assuming in the premises the very thing to be proved.
- **Equivocation and ambiguity**: trading on a word with two meanings (lexical, referential, or syntactical) mid-argument.
- **Companions in guilt / tu quoque**: "your position has the same flaw," sometimes fair, sometimes a deflection.
- **Bad reasons fallacy**: concluding a claim is false because the argument for it is weak; a weak argument leaves the conclusion's truth undecided.
- **Non sequitur**: a conclusion that does not follow from the premises.
- **Slippery slope / domino effect**: asserting an inevitable chain of bad consequences without showing the chain is real.
- **Drawing a line**: the fact that a boundary on a continuum is somewhat arbitrary does not mean no line should be drawn.
- **Sophistry**: clever-sounding argument that smuggles in unlikely conclusions under sham reasoning.
- **Economy with the truth / lawyer's answer**: deceiving by selective omission while technically not lying.
- **Poisoning the well**: discrediting a source in advance so nothing they say gets a fair hearing.
- **"You would say that, wouldn't you"**: dismissing a claim by attributing it to the speaker's interest rather than addressing it.
- **Ockham's razor**: prefer the explanation with fewer assumptions, other things equal.
- **Principle of charity**: interpret an opponent's argument in its strongest reasonable form before responding.

## What this book uniquely contributes
Most critical-thinking books march through formal logic. Warburton instead supplies a portable, alphabetized vocabulary for the everyday moves of real argument, including the persuasion and avoidance tactics that formal logic ignores, like the politician's answer, smokescreens, weasel words, and getting personal. The names are deliberately memorable and English rather than Latin, because the book's claim is that naming a move is what lets you catch it in conversation. The cross-referenced web of entries trains you to see how tricks relate, and the relentless use of plain, topical examples bridges the hardest gap in the whole subject: moving from a textbook case to the messy one in front of you.

## Memorable principles worth keeping (paraphrased)
- The hardest and most important step is carrying a tool from a clean classroom example to the tangled real case, so every technique demands sensitivity to the specific situation and its context.
- To expose an invalid argument form, build an obviously absurd argument with the identical structure; if the absurd version fails, the structure is broken regardless of how reasonable the original sounded.
- Believe the improbable only after the ordinary explanations have been ruled out, and then stay alert to how strongly you wanted the exotic answer to be true.
- A familiar saying is no substitute for thinking; anyone leaning on an adage should first show it actually applies to the case at hand, because the appearance of wisdom is not the same as wisdom.
