# Asking the Right Questions, Distilled

*M. Neil Browne and Stuart M. Keeley*

## Thesis

Critical thinking is a learnable, repeatable routine of asking the same set of probing questions about any argument so you can decide for yourself what to believe, instead of absorbing whatever the loudest persuader hands you.

## Orientation: how the book frames the work

The authors hand you a fixed checklist of questions and teach you to run every persuasive message through it. Two stances bracket the whole method. The **sponge** soaks up whatever it reads and remembers it; useful for acquiring raw knowledge, useless for judging which knowledge to keep. The **gold-panner** interacts with the text, shaking the pan to separate the worthwhile from the worthless. The book trains the panning stance. It also distinguishes **weak-sense** critical thinking (using these tools only to attack views you already dislike) from **strong-sense** critical thinking (turning the same scrutiny on your own beliefs first). Strong-sense is the goal, because it is the only version that can change your mind.

## Thinking tools

1. **Find the issue and the conclusion before reacting.** The *issue* is the question that prompted the conversation; the *conclusion* is the position the author wants you to accept. Locate both before you judge anything, because you cannot evaluate a claim you have not pinned down. Apply it by hunting for the conclusion first (check the opening and closing sentences, watch for signal words like "therefore," "thus," "the truth of the matter is"), then infer the issue from it. Sort the issue into **descriptive** (a question about what the world is, was, or will be) or **prescriptive** (a question about what ought to be, what is good or right), because each type demands different evidence.

2. **Find the reasons, then ask "Why?"** Reasons are the beliefs, evidence, and statements offered to support the conclusion. A conclusion with no reasons is a bare assertion that deserves no weight. The mechanism: an argument equals reasons plus conclusion, so reasons are the only thing that determines whether a conclusion is worth holding. Apply it by asking "Why does the author believe this?" and treating every supporting statement as a reason even when you disagree with it. Paraphrase each reason in your own words to confirm you understood it, and keep reasons visibly separate from the conclusion they support.

3. **Reasons first, then conclusions (guard against backward reasoning).** Healthy reasoning shapes a conclusion out of the evidence. Backward reasoning picks the conclusion first and scrambles for reasons afterward. Watch for the tell: someone states an opinion confidently but looks irritated or stumped when asked for support. The fix is to be your own censor, holding your initial beliefs as provisional and letting them stand or fall on the reasons you uncover.

4. **Hunt for ambiguity in key terms.** Words flex. A claim can sound persuasive only because a crucial word is vague enough to mean whatever the reader wants. Identify the key terms (the ones that carry the conclusion), then ask whether each could carry more than one meaning in context. If swapping in a different reasonable meaning changes whether you accept the argument, you found a load-bearing ambiguity and the author owes you a definition. Watch especially for **loaded language** that smuggles in an emotional verdict before any evidence arrives.

5. **Surface the value and descriptive assumptions.** Assumptions are the unstated ideas the argument needs to work. A **value assumption** is a hidden preference for one value over a competing one (security over freedom, individual responsibility over collective welfare). A **descriptive assumption** is an unstated belief about how the world actually is that bridges a reason to its conclusion. Find value assumptions by asking which value the author had to prioritize for the reasoning to hold, often readable from the author's background and from the consequences they treat as good or bad. Find descriptive assumptions by looking for the gap between a reason and the conclusion and asking what unstated fact would have to be true to close it.

6. **Scan for reasoning fallacies.** Fallacies are tricks of reasoning that fool you into accepting a conclusion the evidence does not earn. The book teaches you to catch them as a named set so you can flag them on sight. Keep the high-frequency ones loaded: **ad hominem** (attacking the person instead of the argument), **straw person** (distorting a position into a weaker version to knock it down), **false dilemma** (presenting two options when more exist), **slippery slope** (claiming one step inevitably triggers an extreme chain), **appeal to popularity**, **appeal to questionable authority**, **search for a perfect solution** (rejecting a fix because it does not solve everything), and **begging the question** (assuming in the premise the very thing you claim to prove). Apply it by checking whether each reason, once you strip the rhetoric, actually connects to the conclusion.

7. **Grade the evidence by its type.** Different kinds of evidence carry different weight, so first ask "What kind of evidence is this?" then ask the questions specific to that kind. **Intuition** is private and unverifiable, so trust it only when it likely encodes real buried experience. **Personal experience** and **case examples** are vivid but unrepresentative, and leaning on them invites the **hasty generalization** fallacy; they prove something is possible, never that it is typical. **Testimonials** are selected, often self-interested, and stripped of context. **Appeals to authority** are only as good as the authority's expertise, firsthand access, and freedom from distorting bias. Strongest of all is well-designed **research** that is publicly verifiable, follows scientific method, and survives peer review.

8. **Interrogate research studies instead of bowing to "studies show."** Research is the best evidence and still breaks in predictable ways. Quality varies, findings get contradicted, and the press distorts them. Ask whether the **sample** is large, random, and representative enough to generalize the people studied, and whether the **measures** (surveys, questionnaires, instruments) actually capture what they claim. Watch for **biased survey wording** and leading question order. A single study rarely settles anything; look for the weight of replicated evidence.

9. **Search for rival causes.** When a writer claims X caused Y, generate competing explanations before accepting theirs. Most observed links admit several plausible causes, and the author usually presents one. Apply it by deliberately brainstorming alternatives, and refuse to confuse **correlation with causation** or to commit the **post hoc** error (treating "after this" as "because of this"). For a difference between two groups, ask whether some third factor, rather than the proposed cause, explains the gap.

10. **Test whether the statistics prove what is claimed.** Numbers borrow authority they have not earned. Authors deploy unknowable or biased statistics, slide between different meanings of **average** (mean, median, mode) to flatter a point, and prove one thing while concluding another. Ask where the number came from, what was measured, which average is in play, and whether the statistic actually supports the specific conclusion drawn from it.

11. **Ask what significant information is omitted.** Every argument is incomplete because persuaders show their best side and hide the rest. Treat the page as a curated case, then ask deliberately for the missing pieces: the downsides and side effects, the counterarguments, the absolute numbers behind the percentages (and vice versa), the perspectives of people who would disagree, and the long-term as well as short-term consequences. Missing information that you cannot recover is itself a reason to lower your confidence.

12. **Generate the alternative conclusions the same reasons allow.** A given set of reasons usually supports several defensible conclusions rather than the single one offered. **Dichotomous thinking** (forcing every issue into two sides) blinds you to the rest. Free yourself by asking "two sides or many?" and by attaching **if-clauses** to the conclusion: under one set of conditions reason points one way, under another it points elsewhere. This converts a forced yes/no into a qualified, more honest judgment and reveals where you actually need more information.

13. **Slow down System 1 and recruit System 2.** Drawing on Kahneman, the book names fast, automatic, emotion-driven thinking (**System 1**) as the source of most reasoning errors, and deliberate, effortful thinking (**System 2**) as the corrective. The mechanism: System 1 reaches a verdict before you have asked a single critical question. The practical move is to pause on purpose, treat your first reaction as a hypothesis rather than an answer, and run the checklist before committing.

14. **Counter the predictable mental habits that sabotage you.** The book catalogs specific biases to watch for in yourself: the **halo effect** (one good trait makes you assume others), **belief perseverance** (clinging to a belief after its support collapses), the **availability heuristic** (overweighting whatever comes to mind easily), **answering the wrong question** (swapping a hard question for an easier one without noticing), **egocentrism**, and **wishful thinking** (believing something because you want it true), which the authors call the single biggest obstacle. Naming each one lets you catch it in the act.

## Named concepts, biases, fallacies, and techniques

- **Sponge vs. gold-panning**, two reading stances; absorb everything vs. actively sift for value.
- **Weak-sense vs. strong-sense critical thinking**, scrutiny aimed only at opponents vs. scrutiny aimed first at your own views.
- **Descriptive vs. prescriptive issues**, questions about what is vs. questions about what ought to be.
- **Value assumptions and value conflicts**, hidden rankings of competing values (freedom vs. security, competition vs. cooperation) that drive prescriptive arguments.
- **Descriptive assumptions**, unstated factual beliefs that connect a reason to its conclusion.
- **Principle of charity**, interpret an argument in its strongest reasonable form before you attack it, which is the discipline that makes strong-sense thinking possible.
- **Fallacy roster**, ad hominem, straw person, false dilemma, slippery slope, appeal to popularity, appeal to questionable authority, search for a perfect solution, begging the question, hasty generalization, post hoc.
- **Evidence hierarchy**, intuition, personal experience, case examples, testimonials, appeals to authority, personal observation, research studies, analogies, ranked roughly weakest to strongest with research on top.
- **Rival causes, correlation vs. causation, post hoc**, the causal-reasoning toolkit.
- **Deceptive statistics**, confusing averages, unknowable figures, proving one thing while concluding another, omitting the base numbers.
- **Dichotomous thinking and if-clauses**, the trap of two-sided framing and the technique that escapes it.
- **Cognitive biases**, halo effect, belief perseverance, availability heuristic, question substitution, egocentrism, wishful thinking, stereotyping.
- **System 1 and System 2**, fast automatic thinking vs. slow deliberate thinking.

## What this book uniquely contributes

The book turns critical thinking into a portable, ordered checklist that anyone can carry into any argument, in any field, without specialized training. Its signal move is making the question, rather than the answer, the unit of thinking; you advance by asking better questions rather than by accumulating verdicts. It also insists that critical thinking is social, so it teaches you to question in a way that keeps the conversation alive instead of shutting people down, and it presses for strong-sense practice where you interrogate your own side first. The "think-aloud" sample responses show the messy, bit-by-bit process of reaching a judgment, modeling the gardening rather than displaying only the finished bouquet.

## Memorable principles worth keeping

- **Experts give you raw material rather than your decision.** Authorities disagree, are often wrong, and frequently sound certain precisely because certainty is persuasive. Gather their assertions, then build your own conclusion from them like a craftsperson, rather than following any one of them like a puppet on a string.
- **Believing is seeing.** Once you expect a result, you notice the evidence that confirms it and miss the rest. This is why a glowing testimonial or a single vivid case feels like proof when it is only a selected fragment, and why you must ask what the people you never heard from would have said.
- **Weak reasons make weak reasoning, no matter how strong the conclusion feels.** A conclusion is only as trustworthy as the support beneath it, so the moment to do your hardest thinking is when you most want the claim to be true.
- **The same reasons usually permit more than one reasonable conclusion.** Before you settle on the author's yes-or-no, generate the other defensible answers and attach the conditions under which each holds; the honest position is often a qualified one, and the gaps reveal exactly what else you need to learn.
