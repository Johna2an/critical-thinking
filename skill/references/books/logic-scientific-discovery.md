# The Logic of Scientific Discovery, Karl Popper

## Thesis
Science does not advance by piling up confirming observations; it advances by proposing bold conjectures and then trying as hard as possible to refute them. A statement earns scientific status only if it forbids something, that is, only if some conceivable observation could prove it false. The mark of a good theory is its exposure to risk: the more it rules out, the more it says, and the more we learn when it survives serious attempts to break it.

## Thinking tools

1. **Demarcate by falsifiability rather than by verification.** To decide whether a claim is scientific, ask what observation would count against it. If you can specify a possible result that the claim forbids, it is testable and belongs to empirical science. If no conceivable observation could ever clash with it, the claim may be meaningful or interesting, but it sits outside empirical science. Apply this as a filter before you spend effort testing: write down, in advance, the outcome that would force you to abandon the claim. If you cannot, you are holding an unfalsifiable belief dressed as a hypothesis.

2. **Hunt for refutations, do not collect confirmations.** Confirmations are cheap; almost any theory can find supporting instances if you go looking for them. The honest test is one where you expected the theory to fail and it did not. To apply this, design the harshest experiment you can imagine, the one most likely to produce a clash with your prediction. Treat each surviving theory as merely "not yet refuted," never as proven. The goal of testing is to overthrow, and a theory you have tried hard to kill and could not is worth far more than one you only flattered.

3. **Reject induction as a method of proof.** No number of observed instances logically guarantees the next one, so you cannot validly reason from "all swans seen so far are white" to "all swans are white." Stop treating accumulated examples as proof. Instead, recognize that general laws are guesses that go beyond the evidence, and their value lies in being testable rather than in being derived from data. When someone says the evidence "proves" a universal claim, remember the logical gap: many confirming cases plus one counterexample still equals refutation.

4. **Prefer the bolder, more improbable theory.** Between two theories that both fit the current evidence, choose the one that says more, forbids more, and is therefore easier to refute. Higher content means lower prior probability and greater informativeness. To apply this, ask of each rival: how many possible states of the world does it exclude? The theory that sticks its neck out furthest is the better scientific bet, because if it survives testing it has told you the most, and if it fails you have learned the most.

5. **Make predictions risky and specific.** A prediction that could fit almost any outcome tells you nothing. Frame each prediction so that a narrow band of results confirms it and a wide range refutes it. Before running the test, state precisely what numbers, intervals, or events would be incompatible with your theory. The narrower the survivable target, the more impressive the survival and the more genuine the test.

6. **Treat every theory as provisional.** Knowledge is a structure of corroborated guesses, all of them open to overthrow by future evidence. Never grant a theory the status of final truth. In practice this means holding your best current theory firmly enough to act on it, while keeping a standing willingness to drop it the moment a well-tested observation contradicts it. Certainty is not on offer; durable usefulness under repeated attack is.

7. **Use corroboration as a report card rather than a probability.** When a theory has passed severe tests, say it is "corroborated," meaning it has held up so far under serious challenge. Do not slide from that into claiming it is "probably true." Corroboration is a record of past performance, an appraisal at a moment in time, with no guarantee about the future. Apply it by tracking which theory has withstood the toughest tests, and let that guide your provisional choice without inflating it into a probability of truth.

8. **Anchor concepts to potential falsifiers.** The empirical content of a theory equals the class of observations it rules out. To analyze any theory, list its "potential falsifiers": the specific basic statements that, if true, would refute it. A theory with a large set of potential falsifiers says a lot; one with a tiny set says little. This list is your map of where the theory is exposed and where it is testing the world rather than merely describing it vaguely.

9. **Guard against ad hoc rescues.** When a refutation looms, the tempting move is to patch the theory with an exception that saves it but explains nothing new and predicts nothing new. Such patches reduce testability and should be treated as illegitimate. Apply a rule: any modification you make in response to a failed test must increase the theory's testable content, opening it to fresh refutation, rather than merely immunizing it. If the patch only blocks the current counterexample, you have weakened the theory rather than improved it.

10. **Accept basic observation statements by decision, while knowing they are revisable.** Tests bottom out in singular statements about observable events, and these too are theory-laden and could be questioned forever. To stop an infinite regress, agree by convention to accept certain basic statements as the testing floor, on the understanding that any one of them can be reopened and tested in turn if doubt arises. In practice, fix your observational ground for the test at hand, but never pretend that ground is bedrock truth.

11. **Welcome the counterexample as the engine of progress.** A single reproducible observation that clashes with a universal law refutes it, and that refutation is informative gold. Train yourself to feel the discovery of a counterexample as advance rather than loss. When a test breaks your theory, the correct response is to identify what the world is telling you it is not, then build a bolder successor theory that accounts for the new fact and exposes itself to further tests.

12. **Increase testability rather than merely fit.** Improving a theory means making it say more and risk more, so it can be checked in more ways. Measure progress by growth in empirical content and precision rather than by how comfortably the theory accommodates known data. A reformulation that is more precise, more universal, or that unifies previously separate domains is an advance because it multiplies the chances of being caught wrong.

13. **Separate the psychology of discovery from the logic of justification.** How you arrived at an idea, by hunch, dream, or accident, is irrelevant to whether it is scientifically sound. There is no logical recipe for generating theories. What can be examined logically is how a theory is tested and criticized once stated. Apply this by refusing to credit or dismiss a hypothesis based on its origin story, and judging it solely by its testable consequences and its survival under criticism.

14. **Treat probability statements as testable through methodological rules.** Statements about chances seem unfalsifiable because no finite sequence strictly contradicts a probability. To make them testable in practice, adopt a rule that improbable, reproducible effects of a certain kind will be treated as falsifying the probabilistic claim. This converts a slippery statement into one you can actually act on and reject, restoring it to empirical science.

15. **Build knowledge as open conjecture under permanent criticism.** Treat the whole enterprise as a self-correcting cycle: propose a problem, offer a bold tentative solution, attack it with every test and criticism available, then face the new problems that survival or refutation create. Apply this loop deliberately in any inquiry. State the problem, conjecture an answer that risks something, attempt to refute it, and let the residue of failed refutations and fresh problems drive the next round.

## Named concepts, biases, fallacies, and techniques
- **Falsifiability (the criterion of demarcation)**: a claim is scientific only if some possible observation could contradict it.
- **The problem of induction**: no finite set of observations logically justifies a universal law or a prediction about unseen cases.
- **Asymmetry of verification and falsification**: a universal law can never be conclusively proven, but a single counterexample can refute it.
- **Conjectures and refutations**: theories are guesses we test by trying to break them rather than truths we extract from data.
- **Empirical content**: the more a theory forbids, the more it says, and the easier it is to test.
- **Potential falsifiers**: the specific observations a theory rules out, which together define what it claims.
- **Corroboration**: a past-performance appraisal of how well a theory has survived severe tests, distinct from probability of truth.
- **Severity of a test**: a test counts only if the theory had a real chance of failing it.
- **Ad hoc hypothesis**: a rescue patch that blocks a refutation without adding any new testable content.
- **Basic statements**: singular, observable-event statements accepted by convention as the floor for testing, yet always revisable.
- **Theory-ladenness of observation**: there is no pure neutral fact; every observation already involves interpretation and expectation.
- **Bold versus cautious hypotheses**: prefer the high-content, improbable, risky theory over the safe one that explains everything.
- **Degree of falsifiability**: a comparative measure of how exposed a theory is to potential refutation.
- **The verifiability fallacy**: mistaking accumulated confirming instances for proof of a general law.

## What this book uniquely contributes
Popper reframes the central question of science from "how do we prove a theory true?" to "how do we expose a theory to being shown false?" That single shift dissolves the old worship of confirmation and the futile search for an inductive logic that could turn data into certainty. By making falsifiability the line between empirical science and everything else, and by treating bold, refutable conjectures as the unit of progress, he gives inquiry an honest engine: theories live by surviving attack rather than by collecting flattery, and the discovery that a theory is wrong becomes the most valuable thing that can happen.

## Memorable principles and examples (paraphrased)
- The white swan lesson: a thousand white swans never establish that all swans are white, yet one black swan settles the matter at once. The logic of the world is lopsided, and falsification is where the leverage lives.
- A theory that explains every possible outcome explains nothing. If a friend's worldview can absorb any event as confirmation, that flexibility is its weakness, because it has stopped forbidding anything and stopped telling you about the world.
- The fortune-teller dodge: when a forecast fails and the forecaster adds "the planets were unusually aligned that day," watch the move. That patch saves the prediction while making it untestable, which is the surrender of science rather than its defense.
- Prefer the prophet who names the exact date and place over the one who says "something significant may happen eventually." The first risks everything and teaches you something whether right or wrong; the second is safe and empty.
- Where ideas come from does not matter to whether they hold up. A chemist's insight from a daydream and a careful deduction stand on equal footing once stated, because both must then run the same gauntlet of attempted refutation.
