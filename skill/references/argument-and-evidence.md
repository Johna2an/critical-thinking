# Argument and Evidence

The everyday core toolkit: how to take an argument apart, grade its evidence, distrust its numbers, and name the rhetorical move that is trying to get past you. Load this when you are evaluating a claim, a study, a chart, a pitch, or any piece of persuasion. It pairs with `biases-and-judgment.md` (your own mind) and `forecasting-and-decisions.md` (acting under uncertainty).

The spine: separate the conclusion from the reasons, surface the hidden premise, grade the evidence by type, list rival causes before accepting any cause, interrogate every statistic at its inputs and outputs, and steelman before you strike.

---

## 1. The Question Checklist (Browne & Keeley)

A fixed, ordered set of probes to run against any persuasive message. The unit of progress is the question rather than the verdict (Browne & Keeley). Hold the **gold-panner** stance (shake the pan to separate worthwhile from worthless) over the **sponge** stance (soak up whatever you read). Aim for **strong-sense** critical thinking (turn the scrutiny on your own side first), because **weak-sense** scrutiny aimed only at views you dislike can never change your mind.

Run the checklist in this order:

1. **Find the issue.** The issue is the question that started the conversation. Sort it: a **descriptive** issue asks what the world is, was, or will be; a **prescriptive** issue asks what ought to be or what is good. Each demands different evidence, so name the type before you gather anything.
2. **Find the conclusion.** The conclusion is the position you are asked to accept. Hunt it first: check opening and closing sentences, watch for signal words ("therefore," "thus," "the truth of the matter is"). You cannot evaluate a claim you have not pinned down.
3. **Find the reasons.** Reasons are the evidence and statements offered to support the conclusion. Ask "Why does the author believe this?" Paraphrase each reason in your own words and keep it visibly separate from the conclusion. A conclusion with no reasons is a bare assertion that earns no weight.
4. **Surface the assumptions.** A **value assumption** is a hidden ranking of one value over a competitor (security over freedom, individual responsibility over collective welfare); read it from the author's background and from the consequences they treat as good or bad. A **descriptive assumption** is an unstated belief about how the world is that bridges a reason to the conclusion; find it by locating the gap between reason and conclusion and asking what unstated fact would close it. The weakness usually hides in the assumption.
5. **Hunt for ambiguity.** Identify the key terms (the ones that carry the conclusion). Ask whether each could carry more than one reasonable meaning in context. If swapping in a different reasonable meaning changes whether you accept the argument, you found a load-bearing ambiguity and the author owes you a definition. Flag **loaded language** that smuggles in an emotional verdict before any evidence arrives.
6. **Grade the evidence by type** (see section 6 below).
7. **Search for rival causes** (see section 7 below).
8. **Ask what is omitted.** Every argument is a curated case. Ask deliberately for the missing pieces: downsides and side effects, the counterarguments, the absolute numbers behind the percentages and the percentages behind the absolute numbers, the perspectives of people who would disagree, and the long-term as well as short-term consequences. Omitted information you cannot recover is itself a reason to lower confidence.
9. **Generate the alternative conclusions.** A given set of reasons usually supports several defensible conclusions rather than the single one offered. **Dichotomous thinking** (forcing every issue into two sides) hides the rest. Ask "two sides or many?" and attach **if-clauses**: under one set of conditions the reasons point one way, under another they point elsewhere. This converts a forced yes/no into a qualified judgment and reveals exactly what else you need to learn.

Backward reasoning is the central tell: healthy reasoning shapes a conclusion out of the evidence; backward reasoning picks the conclusion first and scrambles for reasons after. Watch for confident opinion that turns irritated or stumped when asked for support (Browne & Keeley).

## 2. Mapping Arguments: Premises and Conclusion (Weston, Warburton)

**Premises-and-conclusion mapping** is the first move on any argument. Separate the claim you want to establish (conclusion) from the reasons offered for it (premises), then write it as "Premises ... Therefore, conclusion" (Weston). Most muddled thinking comes from never naming the conclusion or from smuggling it in among the reasons. Of each premise ask: does this give independent support, or does it just restate the conclusion? The conclusion often appears first rather than last, so do not assume the final sentence is the point (Warburton).

**The enthymeme** is an argument with a suppressed premise (Warburton). Most real arguments are enthymemes. To evaluate one, restate it as numbered premises plus conclusion, then surface the unstated premise the conclusion silently depends on. Ask: "What would have to be true, but is not said, for this conclusion to follow?" The hidden premise is usually where the weakness lives.

**Mere redescription** is a premise that just restates the conclusion in new words ("a fine architect" because "a very capable designer of buildings"), or Moliere's dormitive-principle move that explains sleep by renaming it (Weston). Demand independent evidence, never a relabel.

## 3. Validity, Soundness, and the Conditional Forms (Warburton, Weston)

**Validity vs. soundness.** Validity is a property of structure: an argument is valid when true premises would force a true conclusion. Truth is a property of the individual premises. A **sound** argument is valid and has true premises, so its conclusion is guaranteed (Warburton). The trap: a valid argument can have a false conclusion (a premise is false), and a true conclusion can come from a broken argument. Check two things separately: does the conclusion follow from the premises (structure), and are the premises actually true (content)? Reject anything that fails either test, even when you like the conclusion. Because valid forms are valid by structure, the only way to attack a deductive argument is to challenge a premise (Weston).

**The four conditional forms.** Arguments built on "if p then q" come in four shapes; confusing them is one of the most common reasoning errors (Warburton).

- **Affirming the antecedent (modus ponens):** p, therefore q. **Valid.**
- **Denying the consequent (modus tollens):** not q, therefore not p. **Valid.**
- **Affirming the consequent:** q, therefore p. **Fallacy.**
- **Denying the antecedent:** not p, therefore not q. **Fallacy.**

Both fallacies work by secretly reading "if" as "if and only if," ignoring that the consequent can have other causes. Whenever someone reasons backward from an effect to one specific cause, ask whether other causes could produce the same effect.

**The valid deductive forms** (Weston) are the shapes that guarantee a true conclusion from true premises: modus ponens, modus tollens, hypothetical syllogism (if p then q; if q then r; so if p then r), disjunctive syllogism (p or q; not p; so q), dilemma, and reductio ad absurdum. Translate prose into the skeleton. Use modus tollens to refute (if p then q; not q; so not p). Use disjunctive syllogism to eliminate options. Chain simple forms into multi-step proofs, labeling intermediate conclusions so you can reuse them.

**Counterexamples.** To break a sweeping generalization, produce one genuine case that violates it ("no great women scientists" falls to Marie Curie alone) (Warburton). Hunt counterexamples early and on purpose against your own claims, then decide whether to qualify the scope, restate the claim, or rethink what you want to argue (Weston). The search usually yields a more defensible thesis ("many renewables are widely used" rather than "renewables are widely used"). The honest response to a real counterexample is to downgrade "all" to "most" or "some."

**Reductio ad absurdum.** Grant the opponent's premises in full, then derive a contradiction or an absurd consequence (Dennett, Weston). A single impossible conclusion proves at least one premise is false without needing to identify which one first. To expose an invalid argument form, build an obviously absurd argument with the identical structure; if the absurd twin fails, the structure is broken regardless of how reasonable the original sounded (Warburton). Failure mode: a parody only works if you reasoned validly, so check that you did not slip in a step the original never required (Dennett).

## 4. The Four-Part Authority Test (Weston)

When you must rely on a source, accept it only if it passes four filters (Weston). Experts are still human: overconfident, sometimes misled, sometimes biased.

1. **Cited.** The source is named clearly enough that a reader can find and verify it.
2. **Informed.** The source actually knows the field in question. Match the source to the exact question (climatologists for climate rather than politicians). **Universal expertise** is the error of trusting a brilliant physicist on morality or society (Warburton).
3. **Impartial.** Discount sources with a stake in the answer.
4. **Cross-checked.** Confirm the claim against independent sources; cross-checking catches the lone outlier.

Truth does not flow from who said it or how many agree. **Truth by consensus** and the **democratic fallacy** treat agreement or a majority vote as a truth detector, which fails when the crowd is uninformed (Warburton). Weight an opinion by the speaker's relevant expertise and the quality of their reasons rather than their fame or their numbers. Watch the **"O.K. name" maneuver**: borrowing the authority of a respected lab or university to lend weight to a conclusion that institution never endorsed, so confirm the authority stands behind the claim and not merely beside it (Huff).

## 5. Charity Before Attack: Steelmanning, Rapoport's Rules, the Principle of Charity

**The principle of charity** is to interpret an argument in its strongest reasonable form before you attack it (Browne & Keeley, Warburton). It is the discipline that makes strong-sense thinking possible. Assume people hold their views for serious reasons and state their position as they would (Weston). If you cannot imagine why anyone holds a view, you probably do not understand it yet.

**Rapoport's Rules** sequence the steelman (Dennett). Before you criticize a position: (1) re-express it so clearly and fairly that its holder says "I wish I had put it that way," (2) list every point you agree with, (3) name what you learned from it, and only then (4) state your disagreement. This disarms defensiveness, forces you to actually understand the view, earns you a hearing, and protects you from beating a strawman and mistaking the win for a real one. Apply it whenever the stakes or your irritation are high.

**Sturgeon's Law:** roughly ninety percent of everything is mediocre, so critiquing the weak examples of a field proves nothing about the field (Dennett). Direct your criticism at the strongest available version of a position.

**Devil's advocate and thought experiments** (Warburton). Strengthen a view by attacking it with the strongest possible objection, even one you do not believe; a position that survives sustained assault is probably solid. A thought experiment is an imagined scenario built to isolate one variable; dismissing it for being unrealistic misses its purpose.

## 6. Grading Evidence by Type (Browne & Keeley, Weston)

Ask "What kind of evidence is this?" first, then ask the questions specific to that kind. The rough hierarchy runs weakest to strongest:

- **Intuition.** Private and unverifiable. Trust it only when it likely encodes real buried experience (see the two conditions for valid intuition in `biases-and-judgment.md`).
- **Personal experience and case examples.** Vivid but unrepresentative. They prove something is possible, never that it is typical. Leaning on them invites the **hasty generalization** fallacy.
- **Testimonials.** Selected, often self-interested, stripped of context.
- **Appeals to authority.** Only as good as the source's expertise, firsthand access, and freedom from distorting bias (run the four-part authority test).
- **Personal observation.** Better, but still shaped by what the observer expected to see ("believing is seeing").
- **Research studies.** The strongest, because publicly verifiable, method-driven, and peer-reviewed.

**Interrogate research instead of bowing to "studies show"** (Browne & Keeley). Research is the best evidence and still breaks in predictable ways. Ask whether the **sample** is large, random, and representative enough to generalize, and whether the **measures** (surveys, instruments) actually capture what they claim. Watch for biased survey wording and leading question order. A single study rarely settles anything; look for the weight of replicated evidence.

**Use representative examples, and more than one** (Weston). A generalization needs several examples that mirror the whole population rather than a convenient or self-selected slice. Even many examples mislead when they share a bias. Ask "what would a fair cross-section look like, and does my sample resemble it?" Reject any sample people can opt into.

**Background rates (base rates) are often crucial** (Weston). A vivid success or failure means little until you know the ratio of hits to tries. For any "look, it happened" claim, ask: out of how many chances? Count the misses rather than merely the hits; the denominator decides whether a result is skill or luck.

**Test analogies by hunting for disanalogies** (Weston, Warburton). An analogy is strong only when the two cases share the feature the conclusion depends on rather than merely many incidental features. Name the property doing the work in the conclusion, ask whether both cases truly share it, then list the differences and judge whether any breaks the relevant similarity. Slippery slopes and domino effects are analogies dressed as inevitability; the same scrutiny defeats them.

## 7. Rival Causes: From Correlation to the Most Likely Cause (Browne & Keeley, Weston, Warburton)

When a writer claims X caused Y, generate competing explanations before accepting theirs. A correlation between two events is consistent with five stories (Weston): X causes Y, Y causes X, a third factor causes both, mutual causation, or coincidence. List the alternatives explicitly, then look for evidence that discriminates among them. Prefer the explanation that accounts for the data with the fewest unsupported assumptions, and expect real-world causes to be multiple rather than single.

**Post hoc, ergo propter hoc** is inferring causation from mere sequence in time (treating "after this" as "because of this"). Demand a plausible mechanism for how the cause produces the effect, and check whether a hidden common cause drives both series (larger shoe size tracks larger vocabulary because both grow with age) (Warburton). Treat correlation as the first clue in a causal investigation, never as the verdict. Be most suspicious when a correlational study offers a "do this" recommendation (prescriptive overreach).

## 8. The Fallacy Roster

Name the move and its force evaporates. Keep the high-frequency set loaded.

**Relevance and diversion fallacies:**
- **Ad hominem**, attacking the person instead of the argument. The claimant's character is irrelevant to whether the claim is true.
- **Straw man (straw person)**, refuting a caricature of the opposing view instead of the real one. Restate the opponent's view in its strongest form before replying.
- **Red herring**, diverting attention to an irrelevant, usually heated, side issue. Keep asking "is this actually responding to the point on the table?"
- **Poisoning the well**, disparaging a position or source with loaded language before it is even stated ("no sensitive person thinks that ...").
- **Tu quoque / companions in guilt**, "your position has the same flaw"; sometimes fair, often a deflection.
- **The politician's answer**, dodging the question asked and answering an easier one.

**Structure and inference fallacies:**
- **False dilemma**, presenting two options as the only ones when more exist ("love it or leave it"). Increase the number of options you consider.
- **Slippery slope / domino effect**, asserting an inevitable bad chain without showing the chain is real.
- **Begging the question (petitio principii)**, assuming in the premises the very thing to be proved.
- **Equivocation**, a term shifts meaning between premise and conclusion. Pin each term to one sense.
- **Non sequitur**, a conclusion that does not follow from or even relate to the evidence. Identify the missing link.
- **Hasty generalization / overgeneralizing**, generalizing from too few or unrepresentative examples.
- **Affirming the consequent / denying the antecedent**, the two invalid conditional forms (section 3).
- **Search for a perfect solution**, rejecting a fix because it does not solve everything.
- **Bad reasons fallacy**, concluding a claim is false because the argument for it is weak; a weak argument leaves the truth undecided (Warburton).

**Authority and crowd fallacies:**
- **Appeal to questionable authority**, citing a source that fails the four-part test.
- **Appeal to popularity**, treating wide agreement as proof.

**Volume tactics:**
- **Gish gallop**, burying an opponent under a flood of weak claims faster than any of them can be answered. Refuse the pace; pick the load-bearing claim and answer that.

**Loaded framing:**
- **Loaded (complex) question**, smuggling in an assumption you have not granted, so any short answer concedes it ("will you keep behaving like a brat?"). Reject the buried assumption explicitly and answer the parts separately (Warburton).
- **Loaded language and persuasive definition**, wording or a definition built to push emotion rather than inform ("abortion means murdering babies") (Weston).

## 9. The Statistics Defense Kit (Huff, Bergstrom & West)

### Huff's Five Questions

Run these on any reported number (Huff):
1. **Who says so?** Look for conscious bias and the borrowed "O.K. name" of a respected institution.
2. **How do they know?** Sample, method, response rate.
3. **What is missing?** The base, the range, the comparison year, the raw counts.
4. **Did somebody change the subject?** A semi-attached figure or a shifted unit.
5. **Does it make sense?** Sanity-check against common experience and known totals.

### Sampling and Selection

- **Mean, median, mode.** "Average" is a loose word for three numbers that sit far apart on skewed data. The **mean** (total over count) is dragged up by a few large values; the **median** is the middle value; the **mode** is the most common. Refuse any unqualified "average" for income, wealth, or anything lopsided. On a street with three millionaire weekenders, the mean flatters and the median tells the honest middle (Huff).
- **Range and spread.** A single central figure hides how widely the data scatter, and the spread often carries the decision-relevant information. Ask for the range or distribution shape; a child below the "average" may be perfectly normal once you see the range of normal (Huff).
- **Sample size and response rate.** Small samples produce wide, unstable estimates; a "4.9 percent" can rest on two cases out of forty-one. Always ask "out of how many sent?" A projection from the minority who replied can be eleven times too large when the silent majority differs systematically (Huff).
- **Selection bias**, a sample skewed by how members were chosen. Detect it by asking who was systematically left out (Huff, Bergstrom & West). **Self-selection (volunteer) bias**, participants opt in, so the group over-represents whoever cared to respond; classic in mail-ins, online polls, and volunteer studies (the metric-awareness poll inflated because the uninformed never clipped the coupon). **Non-response bias**, the people who do not answer differ from those who do, and they often share the very trait being measured (no conversions to report, low income to admit). Demand **randomization** to break selection: if group membership was chosen rather than assigned at random, treat any difference between groups as suspect until the selection mechanism is ruled out (Bergstrom & West).
- **Right-censoring**, dropping subjects still "in progress" at the study's end, which over-weights early failures and distorts longevity or duration estimates. The viral chart that "proved" rap stars die young is a censoring artifact: rap is too young a genre for its long-lived performers to have died yet, so only its early deaths are counted while older genres carry a full century of long lives. A gap that large is a signal to hunt for the artifact (Bergstrom & West).

### Denominators and Comparisons

- **The unsupported denominator**, a raw count or percentage is meaningless without the base it is drawn from and the comparison group it should be measured against. Always ask "compared to what?" and "out of what?" The claim that 34 percent of behaviorally challenged second graders sniffed markers means nothing without the rate among non-challenged kids, which might be higher (Bergstrom & West).
- **Shifting base / confusion of base**, percentages computed on different starting values cannot be summed or chained. A "50 percent and 20 percent off" discount is 60 percent off rather than 70, because the second cut applies to the already-reduced price; a 50 percent pay cut needs a 100 percent raise to undo it (Huff).
- **Percentage vs. percentage points**, a move from 3 percent to 6 percent is three percentage points and also a 100 percent increase; a manipulator picks whichever sounds better. Clarify which unit is in play, especially with polls, returns, and rates (Huff).

### Charts and Visual Deception

- **Gee-whiz graph and axis manipulation**, chopping the bottom off the y-axis turns a 10 percent rise into a cliff. Read the axes before the shape: check where the axis starts, whether it is labeled, whether zero appears, and whether the vertical axis runs the expected direction. The Florida Stand Your Ground chart inverted its vertical axis so a rise in gun murders read as a drop (Huff, Bergstrom & West).
- **One-dimensional pictograph**, scaling an icon in two dimensions to show a one-dimensional change. A money symbol drawn twice as tall is also twice as wide, so it looks four times larger for a value that only doubled (Huff).
- **Ducks and glass slippers**, a "duck" is a chart where decoration crowds out the data; a "glass slipper" forces data into a visual structure built for something else (a fake periodic table, a subway map of unrelated items) to borrow that form's authority. Check whether the visual structure actually encodes the relationships it implies (Bergstrom & West).

### The Semi-Attached Figure

A speaker proves one thing and then asserts a related but different thing the number does not support (Huff). A medicine "proven" effective in a lab dish, then advertised as curing colds in people, swaps a real result for an unearned one. Check whether the figure cited measures the exact claim being made.

### False Precision

Extra decimal places manufacture an aura of exactness the underlying measurement never supported. Reporting "7.831 hours" of sleep from rough recollections dresses a guess as a measurement; "about 8 hours" is the honest version (Huff). Treat suspiciously precise figures as a warning sign rather than a credential.

### The p-value / Large-Sample Trap and Effect Size

Statistical significance answers "could this be chance?"; it does not answer "is this big enough to matter?" A tiny real bias spread across a huge sample manufactures "significance" while the actual effect is trivial. Demand **effect size** (how large is the difference) alongside any p-value, and demand the sample size that produced it. A significant result on a massive sample can be practically meaningless (outline Part 4; reinforced by the large-sample treatment in `systems-science-and-creativity.md`).

### Black-Box Reasoning

Treat every statistic, model, algorithm, or study as a black box that takes data in and pushes results out (Bergstrom & West). You rarely need to understand the machinery to find the flaw, because errors almost always live at one of the two ends you can see. Ask three questions: (1) Are the input data unbiased, reasonable, and relevant to the question? (2) Do the outputs pass a plausibility check? (3) Do the results actually support the stated conclusion? The criminality-from-faces model collapsed this way: its "criminals" were unsmiling ID photos and its "non-criminals" were smiling professional headshots, so it learned to detect smiles. No knowledge of the neural net was required. Pair this with **garbage in, garbage out**: a model can be no better than the data it ingests, so always ask where the data came from. And with **too good (or too bad) to be true**: implausibly large effects usually signal an artifact, so the bigger the claimed effect, the harder you look for a mundane explanation before accepting the exciting one.

### Brandolini's Principle

The energy to refute bullshit is an order of magnitude greater than the energy to produce it, because a confident falsehood is generated in seconds while debunking it takes evidence and labor (Bergstrom & West). Spend your refutation budget strategically: attack the weakest load-bearing assumption rather than every detail, and remember that the spread of a falsehood says nothing about its truth. Refute charitably: do not assume malice where incompetence suffices, nor incompetence where a reasonable mistake suffices, so your correction survives scrutiny.

### Paltering, Weasel Words, and Implicature

- **Paltering**, misleading with statements that are technically true.
- **Weasel words**, hedged language ("up to," "people are saying," "mistakes were made," passive voice) that suggests a benefit or dodges blame without committing to anything.
- **Implicature**, the gap between what a sentence literally says and what it leads you to conclude. Listeners fill in meaning the speaker never stated and cannot be pinned to.

Defense: separate the literal claim from what you were nudged to infer, then check whether the literal claim alone actually supports the inference (Bergstrom & West, Warburton).

## 10. Dennett's Bad-Tool Detectors

Five labels for moves that look like illumination but spread confusion (a "boom crutch"). Treat each as a place to stop and check (Dennett).

- **The "surely" alarm.** The word "surely" (and italicized emphasis, triumphant repetition) usually marks the exact spot where an author asserts instead of argues, because it sits at the boundary of what the writer hopes you will wave through. Search any persuasive text for "surely" and treat each hit as a weak joint to test.
- **The rhetorical-question test.** A rhetorical question is a compressed argument that assumes its answer is too obvious to state, so the author dodges defending it. Silently supply a good, non-obvious answer to every rhetorical question; when you find one, its force evaporates.
- **The deepity detector.** A deepity sounds profound because it is ambiguous between two readings: one true but trivial, the other earth-shaking but false ("love is just a word"). Separate the two readings explicitly and ask "true-but-trivial, or thrilling-but-false?" The spell breaks.
- **Rathering.** Forcing a false either/or by saying "it is not X, rather it is Y" when both could be true, or when a third option is the better answer. When you meet a forced dichotomy, ask whether both horns could hold.
- **Occam's Broom.** The dishonest cousin of Occam's Razor: sweeping inconvenient facts under the rug by quietly omitting the data that would sink the thesis. When reading anyone's case, ask what facts a motivated author would have left out, then go looking for them.

Two related habits: **piling on** buries a weak central claim under a heap of loosely related true points, so find the load-bearing claim and test it alone. **Jootsing** (jumping out of the system) is the constructive inverse: ask which unstated rules you are taking for granted and what becomes thinkable if you suspend one.

## 11. The Four Grounds for Valid Disagreement (Adler & Van Doren)

Read and argue to interrogate ideas rather than absorb them. Comprehension must precede evaluation, so **suspend judgment until you understand**: do not say you agree or disagree until you can restate the author's case to the author's own satisfaction (Adler & Van Doren). Most disagreement is premature misreading rather than real conflict.

When you do disagree, you must show one of four specific faults, never a vague "I disagree":

1. **Uninformed**, the author lacks relevant knowledge.
2. **Misinformed**, the author asserts what is false.
3. **Illogical**, the author reasons invalidly.
4. **Incomplete**, the author leaves the problem unsolved.

Naming the specific charge turns reflexive contrarianism into a defensible critique and forces you to back it with reasons of your own. The four grounds double as a checklist of where any argument can fail, useful far beyond reading.

The four basic reading questions frame all of this: (1) What is the whole about? (2) What is being said in detail, and how? (3) Is it true, in whole or in part? (4) What of it, what follows and why does it matter? Refuse to answer 3 and 4 until 1 and 2 are settled. For surveying a contested question across many sources, use **syntopical reading**: bring all authors to a neutral shared terminology, frame your own questions, sort each author's answers, and define the debate, always citing where each position comes from.
