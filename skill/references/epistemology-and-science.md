# Epistemology and Science

A toolkit for judging how knowledge gets built, defended, and overturned. Use it to tell when a claim is testable, when an anomaly should bite, when two expert camps are talking past each other, and when someone who turned out right was only lucky. Load this when a debate runs on framework rather than facts, when you need to grade a hypothesis before testing it, or when "do they really know this" is the live question.

---

## Part 1, How frameworks govern inquiry (Kuhn, Feyerabend)

**Locate the paradigm before judging the work.** A paradigm is the shared package a community treats as doing it right: exemplars, assumptions, instruments, and the standard problems everyone trains on. It silently fixes what counts as a real question and a legitimate answer, so identify it first. Ask of any field, argument, or organization: which agreed exemplar is everyone here imitating, and what does that exemplar quietly rule out of bounds? (structure-scientific-revolutions)

**Sort the debate into normal work or foundational dispute.** Most activity inside a mature field is puzzle-solving within the framework, where a failed result reflects on the worker and the framework stays protected. A rarer activity challenges the framework itself. Sort any disagreement into one of the two layers before you weigh evidence, because a move-inside-the-rules dispute and a dispute-about-the-rules need different kinds of proof. (structure-scientific-revolutions)

**Track anomalies, and diagnose crisis by the loosening of the rules.** An anomaly registers only once the framework has made expectations precise enough for a violation to show, so log persistent misfits rather than explaining them away. When ad hoc fixes proliferate, practitioners reopen settled fundamentals, and the field starts trying wild alternatives, it is in crisis and a shift becomes possible. Expect a fresh framework to replace the old one rather than a patched version of it; frameworks get dropped for successors, never for emptiness. (structure-scientific-revolutions)

**Run the incommensurability check before comparing two big frameworks.** Rival frameworks define core terms differently, prize different problems, and disagree about what the facts even are, so no fully neutral measuring stick sits above both. When two camps talk past each other, check whether they are using shared words with shifted meanings before you call either side irrational. Frameworks also pre-sort perception, so distrust "just look at the data" as a tiebreaker, since the data arrives already labeled by one side's categories. (structure-scientific-revolutions, against-method)

**Reconstruct a past thinker in their own terms.** Rebuild the problem as the historical actor faced it, with their available concepts, instruments, and rival options, before grading them against current knowledge. The choice that looks irrational today was often the productive one given what that person could actually see. Read your own certainty the same way: some of your firmest convictions are conclusions you reached, and some are reflexes installed by training. (against-method, structure-scientific-revolutions)

## Part 2, Testing and demarcation (Popper, Feyerabend)

**Demarcate by falsifiability.** A claim is empirical only if some conceivable observation could clash with it. Before you spend effort testing, write down in advance the outcome that would force you to abandon the claim. If you cannot name one, you hold an unfalsifiable belief dressed as a hypothesis, and the world looks identical whether or not it is true. (logic-scientific-discovery)

**Hunt refutations, and proceed by conjecture and refutation.** Confirmations are cheap, since almost any theory finds supporting instances if you go looking. The honest test is the harshest one the theory could fail, the experiment you expected to break it. Treat a surviving theory as not yet refuted, and use corroboration as a past-performance report card rather than a probability of truth. Prefer the bolder, more improbable theory, because it forbids more, says more, and teaches you more whether it survives or falls. (logic-scientific-discovery)

**List the potential falsifiers to map where a theory is exposed.** A theory's empirical content equals the set of observations it rules out, so spell out the specific results that would refute it. A theory with a large set of potential falsifiers says a great deal; one with a tiny set says little while sounding confident. The list shows you exactly where the theory is testing the world rather than vaguely describing it. (logic-scientific-discovery)

**Guard against ad hoc rescues.** When a refutation looms, the tempting patch saves the theory while predicting nothing new. Apply a hard rule: any modification made in response to a failed test must increase the theory's testable content and open it to fresh refutation. A patch that only blocks the current counterexample weakens the theory. Watch for the fortune-teller dodge, where a failed forecast gets excused by "the planets were aligned that day." (logic-scientific-discovery)

**Hold theory pluralism: anything goes as a description of history rather than a license.** No fixed rulebook of method survived the actual record of inquiry; the celebrated advances came from breaking the rules later codified as method. Keep many incompatible ideas alive (proliferation), use counterinduction to build the rival no one is funding and expose the assumptions baked into the reigning view, and treat every fact as theory-laden, since a datum used against a new idea may be an artifact of the idea you are trying to leave. Defend strict method only as a temporary, situational choice you can put down rather than a permanent oath. (against-method)

## Part 3, Knowledge, luck, and scepticism (Pritchard)

**Pull apart belief, truth, and justification.** Treat any knowledge claim as three separable parts: the person holds the belief, the belief is true, and they have good grounds. Check each on its own. A person can be right by accident (true belief, no grounds) or sincerely justified yet wrong (good grounds, false belief). Isolating the three components shows exactly where a claim is weak instead of letting a vague sense of "they know it" cover the gap. (what-is-knowledge)

**Run the Gettier test and name the luck.** Build the smallest vivid case where a belief is true and well justified, yet the truth arrives by a lucky detour the reasoner never saw: the stopped clock that happens to read the right time, the one real barn among convincing facades. If luck made the belief true in a way that bypasses the thinker's competence (veritic luck), downgrade it from knowledge to opinion that landed. Distinguish luck in how the belief formed from luck in whether the world matched it, because that tells you whether to fault the thinker or the situation. (what-is-knowledge)

**Ask whether the belief tracks the truth.** Vary the facts and watch whether the belief moves with them. Sensitivity: if the claim were false, would the person have stopped believing it? Safety: could it easily have been false while they still held it? A belief that would persist even when false is not sensitive to reality and earns little credit. This gives you a mechanical check in place of a vague feeling that someone is reliable. (what-is-knowledge)

**Take the sceptic's argument apart valve by valve, and watch the shifting standard.** A sceptical chain runs tidily: you cannot rule out a deceiving scenario, so you cannot know the ordinary claim. Lay it out as explicit premises, find the single link you reject, and price what rejecting it costs, because that link is where any reply has to intervene. Notice that the bar for "knows" rises with the stakes (a casual claim, a courtroom, a seminar), which explains why people seem to both know and not know the same thing and keeps a debate from stalling on a hidden disagreement about strictness. (what-is-knowledge)

## Numbers and named anchors to keep exact
- **Kuhn's cycle:** normal science, anomaly, crisis, revolution, then a new framework that is incommensurable with the old, plus theory-ladenness of observation (structure-scientific-revolutions).
- **Popper's core:** falsifiability as the demarcation criterion, the asymmetry that one counterexample refutes a universal law while no number of confirmations proves it, potential falsifiers, corroboration distinct from probability, ad hoc immunization (logic-scientific-discovery).
- **Feyerabend's toolkit:** epistemological anarchism, "anything goes" as a description of history, counterinduction, proliferation, theory-ladenness, incommensurability (against-method).
- **Pritchard's anchors:** justified true belief, the Gettier case, epistemic and veritic luck, sensitivity and safety, contextualism (the standard for "knows" rises with the stakes), the stopped clock and barn-facade cases (what-is-knowledge).
