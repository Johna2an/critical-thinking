# Creativity and Ideation

A toolkit for the upstream half of thinking: manufacturing real options and understanding the cognitive engine that produces them, so idea generation runs as a disciplined phase with its own rules rather than a wish for inspiration. Most of the library teaches you to test and dismantle ideas; this file teaches you to flood the field with candidates first, because you cannot reason your way to a good decision from a single choice. Load it for any brainstorm, redesign, or generate-options task. Run generation and judgment as separate passes, and quarantine criticism so it does not strangle ideas before they form.

---

## Part 1, The engine of new ideas

### Bisociation: force two unrelated frames to collide
**The move.** Take the problem and force it into contact with a frame that has nothing obvious to do with it. Name the matrix you are stuck inside, list two or three foreign matrices (a discipline, a craft, a game, a metaphor), and describe the problem inside each until two rule-sets both apply at once. The new option lives where the frames overlap (act-of-creation).
**Why it works.** Originality is recombination of existing patterns rather than creation from nothing. The same collision produces a joke when it discharges as aggression and a discovery when it settles into synthesis, so a frame you already hold can light up a problem it was never built for. Gutenberg fused the coin-stamp and the wine-press into one device.
**How to apply.** Write the governing frame, import a distant one on purpose, and hunt for the single situation both describe. After any euphoric leap, cool down and test the combination back against the rules of both original frames, because the mind eagerly accepts a counterfeit insight that only felt good because it relieved tension.

### Use analogy as the generative engine
**The move.** Strip the problem to its relational skeleton ("A controls B which limits C"), search other domains for the same skeleton, and borrow their solution. Then mark every point where the analogy breaks (surfaces-and-essences, act-of-creation).
**Why it works.** All thought runs on analogy: categorizing a new case is the same act as drawing it toward a remembered cluster. Novices file by surface (both problems mention boats), experts file by deep structure (both are rate-and-distance problems). The boldest discoveries span the greatest surface distance, as when Einstein read a falling painter and gravitation as one situation.
**How to apply.** Restate a stuck problem in an unrelated field (a budget as plumbing, a standoff as physics). Audit each analogy in two columns: the correspondences it makes vivid and what falls outside the mapping, because the shadowed parts are where it misleads. Push the frame to a caricature extreme to find where its useful range ends.

### Reach into the adjacent possible
**The move.** Build the next option from parts available now, and prefer the recombination that opens the most further doors over a leap to a destination whose components do not exist yet (where-good-ideas-come-from).
**Why it works.** Every idea assembles from the current menu of parts, and each move you make unlocks new doors. Chasing the ultimate solution skips the reachable one.
**How to apply.** Stop asking for the final answer and ask for one recombination of things you already have access to. Take the most generative next step, the one that expands future options. Read the honest origin story of any innovation you admire (which parts existed first, who connected them) and recreate those conditions rather than waiting for lightning.

### Cultivate the slow hunch and let hunches collide
**The move.** Give unresolved questions a place to live and a chance to meet their missing half, in your own store and in conversation with others (where-good-ideas-come-from).
**Why it works.** Important ideas incubate for years as faint intuitions, and a private hunch usually completes itself only when it meets a complementary one held by someone else. Liquid, connected environments produce more breakthroughs than isolated ones.
**How to apply.** Keep one long-lived searchable store (a commonplace book) of quotes, observations, and stuck problems, and browse it nonlinearly so old and new entries spark. Get half-formed thoughts out of your head early into shared documents and mixed rooms, since the point of sharing before you have the answer is to find the piece you are missing.

### Mine exaptation and cross-fertilize from outside your field
**The move.** Repurpose a tool built for one job into an unrelated one, and treat "that is not my area" as a signal to look there (where-good-ideas-come-from, whack-on-the-side-of-the-head).
**Why it works.** Feathers warmed birds before they enabled flight; the richest moves often arrive from a domain with no connection to the problem, because expertise inside one field becomes a cage. People with the widest range of pursuits hold the deepest reservoir of parts to repurpose.
**How to apply.** Ask what works in a totally different domain and what it would do here. Graze junkyards, other professions, history, and sports, and keep many side interests so the parts are on hand when a problem needs them.

## Part 2, Breaking the locks that block generation

### Name the mental lock you are obeying, then break it
**The move.** List the constraints you are silently treating as fixed and ask which are real and which are inherited habit (whack-on-the-side-of-the-head).
**Why it works.** Adults stop generating because of learned attitudes that help execute known tasks and ruin finding new ones. The ten locks: the right answer, that is not logical, follow the rules, be practical, play is frivolous, that is not my area, do not be foolish, avoid ambiguity, to err is wrong, I am not creative. Each "you cannot" is a hypothesis rather than a wall.
**How to apply.** Before solving, write down the rules you are obeying without noticing, the way a chess player should make the board's lines of force explicit. Many problems dissolve once you see that a "law of nature" was only the convention of the matrix you happened to start in.

### Hunt for the second right answer
**The move.** Treat the first acceptable idea as the floor rather than the ceiling, and keep searching past it (whack-on-the-side-of-the-head, six-thinking-hats).
**Why it works.** The first good answer ends the search and gives you nothing to compare against, so you cannot judge its strengths. The valuable solution often sits just past where most people quit.
**How to apply.** Ask for answers, meanings, and results in the plural and set a quota you meet whether or not you already like an answer. Change the question's vocabulary to change the answer: an architect who asks "what door goes here" designs a door, one who asks "what passageway goes here" might design a courtyard. Use a wrong-looking idea as a stepping stone for the movement it creates, riding it forward to ask where it leads rather than whether it is correct.

### Provoke on purpose and reverse the frame
**The move.** Float a deliberately unreasonable statement to launch movement, then follow where the jolt leads; reverse the situation and argue the opposite case in earnest (six-thinking-hats, whack-on-the-side-of-the-head).
**Why it works.** The brain channels thinking down established grooves, so escaping a groove needs a jolt from outside current logic. De Bono marks a provocation with the word "po" so others treat it as a launch point rather than a claim to judge. The fool's licensed job is to mock the rules everyone respects, which exposes assumptions you cannot see while you obey them.
**How to apply.** State the provocation explicitly ("suppose cars had square wheels"), or feed in a random unrelated word and force a link. Take your holiest principle and build the case against it. Ask how to cause the failure you want to prevent.

### Borrow another mind and add a constraint
**The move.** Attack the problem as a specific other person would, and impose a deliberate limit or deadline (whack-on-the-side-of-the-head, change-by-design, creative-habit).
**Why it works.** You are trapped in your own assumptions, so importing someone else's (a child, a rival, a craftsman, a fool) supplies constraints they would ignore and twists you would not invent. Total freedom paralyzes, while a well-placed limit channels energy that open-ended comfort dissipates. The unlimited-resources trap is real: abundance dilutes the focus that constraint forces.
**How to apply.** Run the problem through two or three personas. When stuck, narrow the scope on purpose (half the space, no budget, one sentence, a hard deadline) and search for the surprising move within or just past those walls.

## Part 3, Running generation as a managed phase

### Run the Six Hats as separate scheduled passes
**The move.** Do facts (white), feelings (red), caution (black), benefit (yellow), generation (green), and process control (blue) one at a time, never at once (six-thinking-hats).
**Why it works.** The mind can be sensitized in only one direction at a time, so trying to log facts, feel emotion, judge risk, and invent simultaneously produces a confused blur. Running each as its own pass lets a group think in parallel, all looking the same direction together, instead of arguing from opposite corners.
**How to apply.** Quarantine criticism inside black-hat time so it does not frost new ideas, and assign hats to moments rather than to people. During green-hat generation replace judgment with movement: ask where an idea leads rather than whether it is right. During yellow-hat time build the positive case with the same rigor you give to caution.

### Diverge then converge, twice, and solve the correct problem first
**The move.** Widen options with judgment suspended, then switch modes cleanly and narrow; run the double diamond once to find the right problem and once to find the right solution (change-by-design, design-of-everyday-things, creative-confidence).
**Why it works.** Divergent and convergent thinking are opposite modes that cancel each other when mixed, so early criticism collapses good options before they form. A brilliant solution to the wrong problem can be worse than nothing, and the framing of the brief silently fences the range of answers.
**How to apply.** Set explicit phases: generate widely with no judgment (chase quantity, build on others, ban "but"), then switch to evaluating. Treat the stated problem as a hypothesis, restate it as a "how might we" question pitched broad enough to invite fresh ideas, and ask repeatedly whether you are solving the underlying problem or a surface symptom of it.

### Prototype to think, and fail early and cheap
**The move.** Build a rough, disposable artifact to externalize a vague idea, and front-load the riskiest assumption into the cheapest possible test (change-by-design, creative-confidence, design-of-everyday-things).
**Why it works.** A prototype turns abstract debate into concrete feedback and surfaces problems no discussion would, because real use reveals gaps no armchair reasoning predicts. The cost of a mistake rises sharply the later it is caught, so you want your mistakes on a five-dollar mockup rather than a launched product. Prolific creators have an ordinary hit rate and simply run far more attempts.
**How to apply.** Make the lowest-fidelity thing that still tests the question (a sketch, a storyboard, a role-play, a cardboard model), put it in front of a real person, and treat each stumble as a precise pointer to the next change. Optimize for the number of cheap cycles you can complete rather than for getting one attempt perfect. Make "where might this break, and how do I find out by Friday" a standing question.

### Schedule the start, then incubate
**The move.** Ritualize the transition into work, load the problem to saturation, then deliberately disengage and capture the answer when it surfaces (creative-habit, act-of-creation).
**Why it works.** Dread clusters at the beginning, so a fixed low-thought trigger ends the inner debate about whether to start. Insight arrives after you stop chasing it: load the problem until frustration, then a walk, sleep, or an unrelated task lets distant fragments meet. The blocked state before discovery is the precondition for a frame-shift rather than a defeat.
**How to apply.** Choose one repeatable trigger and treat its execution as the whole commitment for the day. Anchor every project to a one-sentence spine that tells you instantly whether a new element belongs. When you hit a hard part, skip it, finish the surroundings, and return once the full context makes both the solution and the motivation clear.

### Reframe failure as design feedback, and blame the system before the person
**The move.** When someone trips, ask what about the structure invited the error; if others repeat it, treat it as a system problem (design-of-everyday-things, designing-your-life).
**Why it works.** Calling a person careless ends inquiry and leaves the cause untouched, while any competent person in the same setup tends to trip the same way. Failures are inevitable inputs to iteration, so the question is what you extract from each one.
**How to apply.** Diagnose with the seven stages of action and close the gulfs of execution (can the person tell what they can do here) and evaluation (can they tell what just happened). Separate affordances (what the thing genuinely allows) from signifiers (the cue that says how to act), since missing signifiers rather than missing capability, usually frustrate people. Sort recent failures into screwups, weaknesses, and growth opportunities to convert a vague sense of having failed into actionable items.

## Part 4, How the mind that generates is built

### Treat the mind as a society of agents rather than one doer
**The move.** Decompose any capacity into dumber sub-processes and locate where a skill actually breaks instead of blaming a vague global failure (society-of-mind).
**Why it works.** Intelligence is the emergent product of many mindless agents organized into agencies, none intelligent alone. Naming an inner power (will, insight, memory) only relabels the mystery; showing how smaller parts produce it does the explaining. Big leaps in growth often come from better internal management of skills you already had (Papert's Principle) rather than new horsepower.
**How to apply.** When stuck despite having the pieces, suspect the missing manager that decides which sub-skill to deploy. Reformulate the problem rather than merely the answer, because many hard problems become easy under a different description, so list two or three ways to state it and pick the framing with the most handles. Promote felt confusion into a concrete question about goals: noticing you are confused is itself the trigger that turns thinking onto its own broken procedure.

### Watch for the strange loop and keep your levels straight
**The move.** Separate the symbol game from the meaning laid on top, hunt isomorphisms to transfer understanding across domains, and expect emergence (godel-escher-bach, minds-i).
**Why it works.** A formal system is tokens and rewrite rules; meaning is an interpretation laid on from outside. A structure-preserving mapping between two systems lets understanding flow across the bridge. Emergence is the property that appears at the level above the parts: the colony thinks, no single ant does, just as a thought is in no single neuron.
**How to apply.** When a problem feels confused, check whether you have slid between levels, attributing a property of the symbol level (it follows rules) to the meaning level (it understands). Build a toy that embodies an abstract structure (a small rule-system you run by hand, a self-referential sentence) so you have touched it rather than merely defined it. When you extend a precise result into a loose domain, name which parts of the mapping are tight and which are suggestive, so the rigor of the source is not silently claimed for the target.

### Pair every position with its strongest reply, and vary one knob at a time
**The move.** State the best objection to your view in its own terms before trusting the view, and test any vivid scenario by changing one detail at a time (minds-i).
**Why it works.** Understanding lives in the tension between a claim and its sharpest rebuttal rather than in either alone, and a fully specified case forces a verdict where an abstract question yields a vague one. An intuition pump can smuggle in a bias through its incidental details, so if your verdict flips when you change something you thought irrelevant, that detail was doing secret work.
**How to apply.** Follow each thesis with a reflection that argues the other way as forcefully as you can, then judge which survives. List the dials in a thought experiment, turn each independently, and watch for self-flattering conclusions you reached by wish rather than argument.
