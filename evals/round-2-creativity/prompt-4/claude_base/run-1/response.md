# Five Concepts for a Museum of Failure

## 1. The Autopsy Theater

A museum built like a forensic morgue, where every exhibit is a corpse on a slab and visitors are the coroners. You don't walk past failures, you dissect them: pull the drawer, lift the sheet, follow the chain of small wrong decisions down to the moment of death. The curatorial logic is causal anatomy, every object tagged with its proximate cause and the deeper systemic rot beneath it. What you learn is that failure is a sequence you could have interrupted, and you leave holding the scalpel, suddenly suspicious of the healthy-looking projects in your own life. The feeling is clinical dread sharpening into competence.

## 2. The House That Survived

There is no gallery and no labels. Visitors enter what looks like an ordinary lived-in house, and only gradually realize every object in it is the product of a catastrophic failure that the household absorbed and kept living with: the warped table from the flood, the rewired lamp from the fire, the marriage that cracked and held. Curatorial logic is domestic continuity, failure as the ordinary sediment of a life rather than a museum event. The lesson is endurance, not redemption: most failures are never resolved, they are simply outlived and folded into the furniture. The feeling is quiet recognition, the warmth of a place where nothing is hidden and nothing is fixed.

## 3. The Vault of Withheld Verdicts

A pitch-dark spiral descending underground, where each chamber holds an artifact whose failure has not yet been decided: the cancelled drug that might have worked, the abandoned city plan, the rejected manuscript. Nothing is captioned as a failure, because the museum's thesis is that failure is a verdict history hands down late and often wrongly. Visitors vote, in real time, on whether each thing failed, and watch the aggregate judgment shift as later visitors disagree. You learn that the label "failure" is a social act of accounting performed by survivors, and you descend further unsure whether you are looking at corpses or seeds. The feeling is vertiginous humility about who gets to call anything a loss.

## 4. The Participation Floor

The exhibits are empty, because you are the exhibit. Each room is a rigged task designed so that you, the visitor, fail it in real time: a bridge of materials that cannot hold, a negotiation with an actor instructed to never yield, a code with no solution. Cameras record your failures and project them, anonymized, onto the walls of the next room for the following visitors to study. Curatorial logic is reflexive: the museum has no collection except the accumulating evidence of its guests breaking against the same walls. You learn failure from the inside, as a felt bodily state of flailing, and you leave having watched a thousand strangers fail exactly as you did. The feeling is sweaty solidarity.

## 5. The Erosion Garden

An outdoor museum with no roof and no climate control, where every failed object is left exposed to weather and instructed to keep decaying for the entire life of the institution. The Wright brothers' rival's broken wing rusts in the rain; the failed seed bank composts back into soil that feeds a real garden growing through the ruins. The curatorial logic is entropic and slow: the museum changes every visit, and a child who returns at sixty will find the exhibits gone, returned to dirt and bloom. You learn that failure is not an endpoint but a phase in a longer material cycle, that decay is the soil of the next attempt. The feeling is geological calm, the relief of being small inside a very long process.

---

**Strangest / riskiest:** The Erosion Garden, because a museum that deliberately destroys its own collection and lets exhibits rot into mulch defies the entire preservationist premise of museums; it might still work because the disappearance *is* the exhibit, and a place that visibly turns ruin into a living garden teaches the lesson through time itself rather than text on a wall.
