**1. The Museum That Forgets**  
A museum where exhibits slowly delete themselves: labels fade, audio guides contradict the plaques, rooms are renamed overnight, and entire wings become inaccessible without explanation. Failure here is treated as loss of institutional memory. Visitors learn that the worst failures are not dramatic collapses, but the quiet disappearance of evidence.

**2. The Apology Factory**  
The building is laid out like a claims office, tribunal, and confession booth combined. Each gallery is organized not by failed invention or failed leader, but by the people who paid the price: patients, workers, children, citizens, ecosystems. Visitors leave with a stamped “unresolved” document showing that apology is often a system for delaying repair.

**3. The Hall of Almosts**  
Nothing in this museum is fully failed: every object came terrifyingly close to changing the world, then missed by one variable. A medicine rejected too early, a peace treaty ruined by translation, a technology five years ahead of infrastructure. The spatial logic is a maze of near-exits, teaching visitors that failure often feels indistinguishable from being early.

**4. The Museum of Successful Failures**  
Every gallery celebrates things that “worked” exactly as designed but failed morally: addictive feeds, profitable pollution, efficient surveillance, weapons with perfect reliability. The layout resembles a luxury showroom, with immaculate lighting and seductive product displays. Visitors feel the discomfort of realizing that failure is not always malfunction; sometimes it is flawless execution of the wrong objective.

**5. The Broken Rehearsal**  
This museum is a live performance space where every exhibit is attempted again and again by visitors, actors, machines, and staff, but never completed. A bridge model collapses hourly, a choir loses the note, a negotiation restarts, a rocket countdown aborts. Its curatorial logic is repetition: failure becomes practice, and the visitor learns to distinguish embarrassment from learning.

Strangest/risky: **The Museum That Forgets**, because it makes the museum itself unreliable, but that instability could make visitors feel failure as lived disorientation rather than as a tidy lesson.
