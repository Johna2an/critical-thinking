**Bread That Ages**
A sealed installation begins with warm yeast and toasted crust, then drifts into cool flour, sour tang, stale paper, and finally the faint bitterness of crumbs. The day is not shown, it is smelled as a kitchen losing its morning.

**The Room That Changes Skin**
A wall-sized panel shifts texture under your palm: damp linen, sun-warmed plaster, dry grit, velvet shadow, cold glass. You understand the day by touch, as if light had left fingerprints.

**Choir Of Ordinary Machines**
No music, only domestic sounds staged in sequence: kettle breath, elevator doors, lunch trays, printers, distant traffic, dishwater, a refrigerator taking over the night. The day becomes a soundscape of human routines without ever naming time.

**Thermal River**
A narrow handrail carries a slow thermal gradient: cool metal, living warmth, almost-hot stone, fading warmth, then nocturnal chill. Walking along it feels like passing through one day held inside your palm.

**The Tasting Menu Of Light**
A single spoonful changes across courses: mint dew, buttered grain, sharp citrus, smoked tea, black salt, chamomile, mineral water. The passage of the day is translated into appetite, fatigue, and the way the mouth expects the world to change.

Strangest or riskiest: **The Tasting Menu Of Light**, because taste is the least literal way to represent time, but it could work by tying the day to bodily rhythm instead of visual symbols.
