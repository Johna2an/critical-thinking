**1. The Blank Sponsor**  
Brands buy empty airtime, blank pages, and unfilled billboard slots, then sign them: “This silence was paid for by...” The customer is not the advertiser but the exhausted public; the business monetizes attention by deliberately not taking it. It works because restraint becomes a prestige signal.

**2. Absence Insurance**  
People subscribe to a service that certifies they are unreachable for chosen periods: no replies, no explanations, no guilt. The company becomes a socially accepted excuse layer for burnout, grief, recovery, or deep work. It monetizes silence as protection from obligation.

**3. Quiet Credits**  
Cities, factories, venues, and developers earn measurable “silence credits” by reducing decibel exposure in real neighborhoods, then sell those credits to noisy industries under regulation or ESG pressure. Residents get paid from the credit pool when their environment becomes quieter. Silence becomes an environmental commodity, like carbon.

**4. The Unsaid Archive**  
Customers pay to deposit messages, confessions, apologies, or family truths that will never be delivered unless strict conditions are met. The product is not communication, but dignified withholding. It serves people who need containment more than expression.

**5. The Silence Exchange**  
Public figures, streamers, pundits, or even AI companions auction off periods of total non-output. Fans, haters, companies, or communities bid to make someone stop posting, speaking, or broadcasting for an hour, day, or week. It monetizes silence through scarcity, spectacle, and collective relief.

Strangest/riskiest: **The Silence Exchange**, because paying people to disappear sounds hostile, but it could work if framed as performance, charity, or voluntary attention fasting.
