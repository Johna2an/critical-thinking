**1. Borrowed Gravity**  
A chair that is not below you, but above you: a ceiling-mounted counterweight harness that quietly removes 40 percent of your body weight. You “sit” by leaning into gravity instead of folding into a surface, hovering somewhere between standing, floating, and being held.

**2. The Conversational Chair**  
This chair has no fixed shape until two people sit near it. It rearranges into distance, angle, and height based on the kind of conversation: confession, negotiation, flirtation, apology. It treats sitting as a social instrument, not a private ergonomic problem.

**3. The Temporary Skeleton**  
Instead of furniture, it is a soft exoskeleton that locks your joints into rest positions while still letting you walk slowly. You carry your chair on your body, and “sitting” becomes a mode you can enter anywhere: in a line, in a gallery, halfway through cooking.

**4. The Chair That Spends You**  
A metabolic chair that powers itself from your fidgeting, warmth, and pulse, then gives feedback only when your body has been still too long. It makes rest visible as an exchange: you are not using the chair, you are feeding it, and it decides when sitting has turned into stagnation.

**5. The Absence Chair**  
A marked empty zone on the floor where architecture, sound, and light make your body naturally lower itself without any object being there. The room cools, the acoustics soften, the floor subtly yields, and you realize the “chair” was never the object, but the permission to stop.

Strangest or riskiest: **The Absence Chair**, because it removes the chair entirely, but it might work if sitting is really a ritual cue rather than a piece of furniture.
