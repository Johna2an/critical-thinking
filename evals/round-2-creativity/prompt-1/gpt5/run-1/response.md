**1. Collapse Choir**  
Players begin locked into a wide 3D formation around a central bell, then all release at once and “fall” inward using only tiny wrist thrusters. The sport is about timing collective collapse: points come from striking the bell in prescribed body-order sequences without touching another player. It replaces falling with intentional convergence.

**2. Axis Duel**  
Two players enter a transparent sphere with no fixed up, down, or walls marked as “floor.” The objective is to force the opponent to lose their declared body-axis by tagging pressure points that make them spin, while maintaining your own orientation for ten seconds. It turns balance into pure rotational discipline.

**3. Mass Court**  
Players wear suits with attachable ballast pods that can be passed, stolen, or magnetically docked mid-play. The goal is not to score with a ball, but to become temporarily “heaviest” at the exact moment a sensor gate flashes, while opponents try to shed or load mass onto you at bad moments. Weight becomes a tactical resource instead of a constant.

**4. No-Floor Siege**  
The arena has six floating goal frames and no permitted surface contact at all. Teams can only move by pushing off teammates, rebounding off legal free-floating blocks, or catching momentum ropes fired by allies. It is built around the absence of ground: territory is not where you stand, but where your team can create usable contact.

**5. Broken Parabola**  
Players launch soft luminous “comets” that never naturally arc, so scoring depends on bending their paths with timed air jets, moving gravity-mimic fields, and player bodies acting as deflectors. Each round is a live geometry puzzle where the throw is only the opening claim, not the trajectory. It replaces the predictable thrown arc with negotiated pathmaking.

Strangest/riskest: **Mass Court**, because making “who owns the weight” the central drama is alien to Earth sports, but it could work because the rules create clear steals, reversals, and last-second tactics.
