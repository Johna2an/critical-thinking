**1. The Apprenticeship Passport**  
A small booklet of “trusted with” stamps: trusted with sharp tools, trusted to lead a younger student, trusted to revise without prompting, trusted to ask for help early. The real audience is not parents but future mentors, coaches, and employers who need to know what kind of responsibility this person can carry.

**2. The Question Museum**  
Instead of answers, the student curates their best questions from the year: the one that changed their mind, the one nobody could answer, the one they are still chasing. The audience is the student’s future self, measuring intellectual appetite rather than compliance.

**3. The Friction Map**  
A visual map of where learning got hard: boredom zones, panic zones, breakthrough zones, avoidance loops, places where support actually helped. The philosophy is that struggle is not a defect but the most useful diagnostic signal; the audience is teachers designing the next environment.

**4. The Contribution Ledger**  
The report card records what the student added to the room: explanations given, conflicts cooled, patterns noticed, tools built, people included, standards raised. The audience is the learning community, and the measure is not “how did you perform alone?” but “what changed because you were here?”

**5. The Unofficial Biography**  
Written like a short literary profile, it captures the student as a character in motion: what they protect, what they resist, what wakes them up, what they misunderstand beautifully. The audience is the adults who keep reducing children to metrics; its philosophy is that education should preserve personhood before it ranks performance.

Strangest/riskest: **The Unofficial Biography**, because it is subjective and hard to standardize, but it might work precisely because it restores the human texture that report cards usually erase.
