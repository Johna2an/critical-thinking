Five zero-gravity sports, each rebuilt around a different thing gravity normally hands you for free:

**1. DELTA-V DUEL — breaks "falling" (replaces it with momentum debt)**
There is no floor to fall toward, so the sport makes you fall sideways forever. Two players start clipped to opposite walls of a spherical arena and have exactly one hard push each, plus a single magnetic "anchor" they can fire to a wall to swing around. You score by tagging the opponent, but every push you make permanently spends a finite momentum budget tracked on your suit, so a careless first shove leaves you drifting helplessly with nothing left to redirect with. The whole game is the dread of free-fall converted into an economic resource: you are always "falling," and the loser is whoever runs out of ways to steer their own fall.

**2. THE TETHER WALTZ — breaks "balance" (replaces it with shared rotation)**
On Earth balance is a private fight against toppling; here there is no toppling, so balance becomes a thing two bodies negotiate. Pairs are joined by a 4-meter elastic tether and must spin, fling, and counter-spin to thread their linked bodies through floating rings in sequence, like a two-person yo-yo doing calligraphy. If one partner over-commits their rotation, both of them tumble into an uncontrolled wobble that takes seconds to damp out, so the skill is reading and canceling your partner's angular momentum in real time. It is figure skating where your "edge" is literally another human being you are conserving angular momentum with.

**3. SUMP — breaks "weight" (replaces it with inertia you can hide)**
On Earth a heavy object announces itself by being hard to lift; in orbit weight vanishes but mass does not, and the sport weaponizes the gap. Players pass around opaque identical-looking spheres, some hollow and some packed with dense ballast, and must build a stable floating "tower" lattice between two goal posts before the buzzer. You only learn a sphere's true mass by shoving it, and a heavy sphere shoved like a light one will plow through your half-built structure and scatter it. Bluffing is structural: you can hand an opponent a "light" sphere that is secretly a wrecking ball, so the game is poker played with conservation of momentum.

**4. SHROUD — breaks "ground" (replaces it with a membrane you collectively own)**
Gravity normally gives everyone a shared, permanent reference surface; remove it and the radical move is to invent a temporary one. Two teams play inside a giant slack fabric sphere, and the "field" is a smaller free-floating elastic sheet that any player can grab, stretch taut between teammates, and briefly use as a trampoline, a wall, or a sail. Points come from launching a puck through the opponent's zone, but the sheet is contested terrain: whoever has more hands on it controls its shape, so the real contest is the constant fight to define where "the ground" even is this second. The surface is socially constructed, in the most literal possible sense.

**5. PAROLE — breaks "the arc of a thrown object" (replaces it with curved space you draw yourself)**
On Earth you aim by trusting a parabola; here a thrown ball flies dead straight forever, which sounds boring until the arena fights back. The court is studded with player-triggered fan-jets and a slow rotational drift, so to make a shot curve into a goal you must throw straight AND fire a precisely-timed crosswind to bend the path mid-flight. Defenders score by triggering counter-jets that nudge an incoming ball just enough to miss, turning every shot into a brief two-party tug-of-war over a single trajectory. You are not predicting the arc, you are authoring it, frame by frame, against someone trying to author a different one.

---

**Strangest / riskiest: SUMP (#3).** It throws out the entire visual-athletic premise of sport and replaces it with deception about an invisible property (mass), which sounds like a spectator nightmare. It still works because mass-without-weight is the one physical intuition Earth-bred audiences and players genuinely lack, so the "reveal" of a heavy sphere plowing through a structure is a spectacle gravity can never produce, and the bluffing layer gives it the social drama that pure physics demos lack.
