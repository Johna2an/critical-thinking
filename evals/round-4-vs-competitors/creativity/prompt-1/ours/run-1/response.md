Each version is anchored to a different gravity-gift so they cannot collapse into each other. I removed one Earth-given thing per sport and replaced it with a mechanic that only zero-g makes possible, then pushed past the obvious "floaty dodgeball" answer using reversal, analogy, and repurposing.

**1. TETHER (breaks: FALLING / there is no "down" to fall toward)**
On Earth, falling is an involuntary force you fight; in zero-g it vanishes, so this sport manufactures it on purpose. Each player is clipped to an elastic tether bolted to the chamber wall; the whole game is about storing and releasing stored tension. You "fall" only when a teammate or rival cuts, grabs, or crosses your tether, converting its pull into a sudden slingshot you cannot stop. Scoring is positional: be the last one whose tether is still loaded when the round-clock fires, so the strategy is a web of who is winding whom, and the spectacle is six bodies snapping across the cube on lines no one can see until they go taut.

**2. THE FLOCK (breaks: BALANCE / there is no single body to balance)**
Earth balance is one nervous system keeping one center of mass over a base; remove the base and the sport relocates balance from the individual to the group. Eight players link hands into a closed ring and must keep their shared center of mass parked over a glowing target zone while an opposing ring tries to torque them off it by yanking through the same grip-chain. No one can stabilize alone, because any push you make spins the whole flock; the only control is collective, read through your hands. It plays like tug-of-war fused with a murmuration of starlings, won by the ring that can think as one organism faster than the other can be jerked into a tumble.

**3. BALLAST (breaks: WEIGHT / mass exists but weight does not)**
In zero-g a sumo wrestler and a child have identical weight (zero) but not identical inertia, and this sport is built entirely on that gap. Players wear modular mass-pods they can clip on and jettison mid-match; a heavy loadout is hard to shove but slow to redirect, a light one darts but gets flung by any contact. You score by being the one to pass through a moving gate at the instant the gate "weighs" what you weigh (the gate samples inertia on contact), so you are constantly re-balancing your own mass against where the gate will be. The deep move is jettisoning a pod backward to rocket yourself forward, spending weight as fuel, which makes mass a currency you bet and burn rather than a fixed trait.

**4. SECOND SKIN (breaks: GROUND / the playing surface is the inside of a sphere, and it moves)**
On Earth the ground is fixed, flat, and underfoot; here the "field" is the entire inner surface of a slowly rotating spherical shell, and you play *on* it from the inside using sticky gloves and boots. Because the shell rotates, the wall under you is always sliding, so a stationary defender drifts away from the goal while a clever attacker rides the rotation toward it for free. The ball is magnetic and clings to the shell too, so it rolls along a curving surface that has no edges and no corners to trap it. Mastery is reading the shell's spin like a tide and timing your sprint so the ground delivers you to the target, a sport where the field is a slow conveyor wrapped into a globe.

**5. ECHO (breaks: THE ARC / a thrown object travels in a straight line forever, so the throw is reframed as the threat)**
On Earth a throw is a parabola you lead and catch; with no arc, a thrown puck travels dead straight until something stops it, and that turns the whole chamber into a cushion-shot billiards table. Players fire a single hard puck and it ricochets off walls forever, so you score not by catching it but by being absent from the line it will cross three, four, five bounces later. The skill is purely geometric prediction: you read angles and place your body in the one volume the puck will never reach, while spinning it on release to bend its bounce off the padded faces. It is dodgeball inverted into a chess problem, where the danger is not the throw you see but the rebound you forgot to compute.

**Strangest / riskiest: THE FLOCK.** It is the only one that removes the individual athlete as the unit of play and makes the team a single distributed body, which risks feeling like a group-trust exercise rather than a sport. It might still work because the failure state (a flock that tumbles into chaos the instant coordination slips) is exactly the visible, high-stakes, instantly-readable drama that good spectator sports are built on, and "can eight people share one balance under attack" is a genuinely new athletic skill with no Earth equivalent to fall back on.
