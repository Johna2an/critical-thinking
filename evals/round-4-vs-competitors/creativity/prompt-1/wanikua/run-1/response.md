Five zero-gravity sports, each built around a different service gravity normally provides on Earth. I decomposed gravity into its five distinct "jobs" (falling, balance, weight/inertia, the ground plane, the thrown arc), then attacked each with a provocation so the result is a new game rather than an existing sport that happens to float.

---

**1. WICK** — built on the absence of FALLING (the vector)
On Earth, gravity is the one force you can never turn off, so every game has a permanent "down." Wick replaces gravity's constant pull with a single artificial one: a slow-drifting magnetic "ember" the size of a fist that everyone is tethered to by a thin elastic line clipped at the waist. The ember is the only "down" in the arena, and it wanders. Players score by being the last to let their line go taut (you must stay slack, i.e. "falling" toward the ember faster than it pulls you), so the whole match is a migrating, breathing scrum of bodies orbiting a wandering center of gravity that you collectively create and fight to evade. What makes it distinct: it doesn't remove falling, it makes falling a thing players aim and chase, a portable down that betrays you when you stop paying attention.

**2. KEEL** — built on the absence of BALANCE (the reference frame)
With no up or down, your inner ear has nothing to level against, so Keel weaponizes disorientation directly. Two players sit inside a clear spherical cockpit with no markings, spun and released; the goal is to be first to point a laser-pen at a hidden target light only THEY can see (each has a different one). You cannot see your opponent's progress, only feel the sphere tumble as you both push off the inner walls to rotate it. It's a duel of dead-reckoning and nausea management, won by whoever holds a mental model of "which way am I facing" longest while actively trying to scramble the opponent's. Distinct because the contest is entirely internal: the playing field is your own vestibular system, and the sport rewards a sense Earth athletes never have to train because gravity hands it to them for free.

**3. CLOT** — built on the absence of WEIGHT/INERTIA-COUPLING
On Earth, heavy means hard-to-move AND hard-to-stop, because weight and inertia ride together. In free fall, mass keeps its inertia but loses its weight, so Clot is a sport of pure momentum banking. Teams pass a dense 20kg "core" between them; whoever holds it inherits its full inertia and can barely change direction, becoming a slow guided missile, while empty-handed players are nimble darts. You score by docking the core into a goal-ring, but the core's momentum is conserved across every catch, so a team builds up "stored speed" through a chain of perfectly-timed catches like a Newton's cradle made of people. Distinct because it isolates the half of weight we forget exists: the sport is about steering inertia you can't feel as heaviness, only as a refusal to turn.

**4. LICHEN** — built on the absence of the GROUND PLANE (the surface to push against)
Every Earth sport assumes a floor you can plant against to generate force; remove it and you can only move by throwing mass away from yourself. Lichen gives each player a small bag of soft beanbags as their entire propulsion budget for the match. You travel the arena by throwing beanbags backward (Newton's third law as your only engine), and once your bag is empty you're stranded, drifting, unable to maneuver until you catch a teammate's discarded mass. The strategic core is conservation: the goal is to tag three anchor-pads in sequence using the fewest throws, so it becomes a game of ballistic budgeting where running out of "ground" means floating helplessly past the finish. Distinct because it turns the ground itself into a consumable resource you carry, throw away, and beg to borrow.

**5. SUTURE** — built on the absence of the THROWN ARC (the parabola)
On Earth a thrown object curves, so aiming means leading a parabola; in zero-g everything flies dead straight forever, which sounds boring until you make the straight line the whole point. In Suture, two teams sit on opposite walls and fire glowing darts that travel in perfect rays; the floating space between them slowly fills with a fixed three-dimensional lattice of these frozen straight trajectories, and you score by threading a new dart cleanly through a closed triangle of three existing ones without touching them. The arena becomes a hardening web of light, a cat's-cradle you're both weaving and trying not to snag, and late-game the open lanes shrink to nothing. Distinct because it takes gravity's most beloved gift, the graceful arc, deletes it, and discovers that a universe of straight lines is a geometry sport, not a ballistics one.

---

**Strangest / riskiest: KEEL.** It is the only one where there is no visible playing field, no ball, and the opponent's state is hidden, so a spectator sees two people sitting still in a tumbling ball doing apparently nothing. That is a brutal handicap for a sport. It might still work because it turns the body's own confusion into the spectacle: with a head-up display showing each athlete's (wrong) belief about which way they face versus reality, the audience watches two minds losing and regaining their grip on space in real time, which no Earth sport can offer because gravity never lets us get that lost.
