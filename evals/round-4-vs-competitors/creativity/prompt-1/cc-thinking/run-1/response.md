Here are five zero-G sports, each built on a different function gravity quietly performs on Earth. I worked each one by isolating one gravity-function (falling, balance, weight, ground, the thrown arc), asking what that function silently guarantees in every Earth sport, and designing the entire tension around that guarantee being absent. I deliberately pushed each onto a separate conceptual axis so they would not collapse into five floating variants of the same thing.

---

**1. TETHER (gravity-function removed: the GROUND as a free anchor)**
On Earth, the floor gives you something to push against for free. Remove it and the only way to change direction is to throw mass away or pull on something. Tether is played in a spherical cage with eight elastic cords radiating to the walls. Players have no thrust of their own; you move solely by grabbing, stretching, and releasing the cords, and every pull yanks the cord's other anchor and whoever is holding it. The game is a live tug-of-war network where claiming a cord starves an opponent of the only leverage in the room. The core skill is reading the whole tension web at once, because cutting your own line to lunge can fling a teammate into a wall.

**2. BALLAST (gravity-function removed: WEIGHT as a fixed, honest number)**
On Earth your mass is constant and visible, so strength contests are legible. In free-fall, mass still resists acceleration but no longer "weighs," so it becomes a hidden, tradeable resource. Each player starts with a belt of water-filled pucks (their ballast). You score by docking pucks onto a slow central spindle, but a heavier player accelerates sluggishly while a stripped-down player darts but gets swatted aside in any collision. The whole match is a momentum economy: spend mass to become nimble, or hoard it to become an immovable gatekeeper at the spindle. The strange beauty is that the leading scorer is also the lightest and most fragile, so winning makes you easier to bully.

**3. PARALLAX (gravity-function removed: FALLING, and with it "down" as a shared reference frame)**
Gravity gives everyone the same private compass; in zero-G there is no agreed "up," so two players can perceive the same geometry rotated 180 degrees and both be right. Parallax weaponizes that. The arena is a lattice of glowing gates that only score when you pass through them spinning at the correct orientation, and the gates' "correct" side is defined relative to a slowly tumbling beacon that every player sees from a different angle. You must constantly re-derive which way is "valid" from your own spin while disoriented opponents nudge your axis to corrupt your sense of frame. It is a sport about perception and dead-reckoning your own rotation, where the opponent's main weapon is making you wrong about which way you are facing.

**4. CAROM (gravity-function removed: the predictable ARC of a thrown object)**
On Earth a thrown ball traces a parabola you can read instantly; the arc is doing the geometry for you. Strip gravity and a thrown object goes dead straight forever until it hits something, which turns the playing field itself into the entire puzzle. Carom is billiards inflated to a room: you fling a heavy ball that ricochets off angled, repositionable bumper-panels, and points come from making it strike target zones after a declared number of bounces. Players spend the match physically grabbing and re-angling the wall panels to author the ball's future path, so you are not aiming at a target, you are building the corridor that delivers the ball there. Long-range geometric planning replaces the instinctive lob, and a single mis-angled panel three bounces ago decides the round.

**5. QUORUM (gravity-function removed: BALANCE, and the solitary stable body it allows)**
On Earth one body stands upright alone; balance is a private act. Without it, a lone player tumbles uncontrollably from any exertion, because every push spins you, so stability can only be manufactured collectively. In Quorum, players physically link into rotating rings of three to six bodies, and only a closed ring is stable enough to act, throw, or resist. Scoring requires a ring to stay intact while reaching a contested zone, so the opposing team's whole strategy is to break your circle by peeling off one member, after which the rest spin out helplessly. It is a sport where the unit of play is never the individual; balance is a social contract you assemble in real time and your opponents try to dissolve.

---

**Strangest / riskiest: PARALLAX.** It is the only one whose core obstacle is invisible and lives inside each player's head rather than in the physical arena, which makes it the hardest to officiate and the easiest to feel broken. It might still work because it is the purest expression of the actual weirdness of zero-G, the loss of a shared "down," and that disorientation is exactly the thrill a spectator could never get from any Earth sport. If the scoring beacon and gates can be made readable enough to be fair, the confusion stops being a bug and becomes the entire sport.
