Five radically different concepts for a museum devoted to failure. Each breaks a different assumption about what failure is, who the visitor is, and what they should walk out believing.

**1. The Confessional Furnace**
A museum with no permanent collection. Visitors arrive carrying their own failure, write it on a thin sheet of pressed clay, and that becomes the only exhibit until they leave. You wander a dim, warm hall reading the still-wet failures of strangers who came this morning, then add yours and watch an attendant feed the oldest tablets into a kiln at closing. The curatorial logic is decay: nothing is preserved, the archive is destroyed nightly, and the lesson is that failure is metabolized, not enshrined. Distinct because it removes the artifact entirely and makes the visitor the exhibit, the donor, and the witness in one loop.

**2. The Counterfactual Wing**
Every display is a success the world actually got, shown beside the failed prototype, dead-end, or rejected version that made it possible. The spatial layout is a forking path: you physically choose at each junction whether to follow the failure branch or the success branch, and the branches are deliberately the same length, so half your visit is spent inside roads that led nowhere. The learning is causal, not emotional. You leave understanding that the success you admire was statistically downstream of a graveyard you never see. It breaks the assumption that failure and success are opposites by curating them as a single continuous supply chain.

**3. The Calibration Casino**
Failure here is not a story but a probability you misjudged. The museum is built as a working betting floor: at every exhibit you stake tokens on outcomes (will this bridge design hold, will this startup survive, will this drug pass trials) before the result is revealed, and your running miscalibration score is projected on the wall above your head. The architecture is a single spiraling ramp with no exits until the end, so you cannot retreat from a bad streak. The theory is that the deepest failure is overconfidence, and the visitor learns it in their gut by losing tokens, not by reading a placard. It breaks the assumption that a failure museum should make you feel safe and sympathetic; this one makes you feel exposed and wrong in real time.

**4. The Museum That Is Failing**
The building itself is the exhibit, and it is visibly malfunctioning on purpose. Escalators stall, the wayfinding contradicts itself, a gallery promised on the map turns out to be a broken door, and the audio guide occasionally gives wrong directions. Staff are trained never to apologize or fix anything, only to document each breakdown on a live wall of "today's failures." The curatorial logic is performative and recursive: you do not look at failure, you are inconvenienced by it, and the museum measures its own success by how gracefully it degrades. The visitor learns that systems fail continuously and that the real skill is tolerating friction, not eliminating it. It breaks the frame that a museum should function.

**5. The Decompression Chamber for the Recently Ruined**
Not a tourist attraction but a recovery facility you book like a clinic, for people in the immediate aftermath of a real catastrophe (a fired CEO, a failed founder, a defaulted family). There are no historical exhibits at all. The space is a sequence of private rooms tuned to one emotional task each: a soundproof room to say the thing out loud, a room of strangers who failed at the exact same thing, a room where an actuary calmly shows you how common your outcome actually is. The theory is therapeutic, not educational, and the only artifact is your own nervous system on the way out. It breaks the assumption that the museum is about the past or about other people; here failure is present-tense and yours, and the visitor is a patient.

**Strangest/riskiest: The Museum That Is Failing.** It deliberately sabotages the one thing visitors expect from any institution (that it works), risking pure frustration and bad reviews. It might still work because the discomfort is the curriculum: by the end, the bewildered visitor has lived the truth that no system is ever fully functional, a lesson no placard could deliver, and the recursive joke (a failure museum that succeeds by failing) is the kind of thing people argue about for years.
