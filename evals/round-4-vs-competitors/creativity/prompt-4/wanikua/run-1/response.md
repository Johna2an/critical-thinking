Five radically different concepts for a museum devoted to failure. Each breaks a different assumption about what "failure" and "museum" even mean.

**1. The Mortuary**
A hushed, low-lit building organized like a funeral home, where every exhibit is a eulogy. You move through viewing rooms, each holding one dead thing: a bankrupt company, a collapsed bridge, a discontinued drug, a marriage. There are no captions explaining what went wrong, only an obituary written by someone who loved the thing and a guestbook you sign. The visitor should feel grief, not amusement, and learn that most failure is mourned by someone, that the cost was real and borne by people. Spatial logic: a procession with no shortcuts, exit only through the door you entered, so you carry the weight back out.

**2. The Lab That Won't Let You Win**
A fully interactive arcade of rigged machines, impossible puzzles, and games whose rules quietly change so you can never finish. Every station is engineered to defeat you, and your wristband logs exactly how, when, and how fast you quit. At the end a printout shows your personal failure signature: do you rage, freeze, blame the machine, keep hammering past the point of sense? The learning is somatic and self-directed; you do not study other people's failures, you produce your own and read the data. Layout is a noisy open floor with no fixed path, the opposite of the reverent gallery.

**3. The Museum That Is Failing**
The building itself is the exhibit, and it is collapsing in real time, by design. Galleries flood, the cafe runs out of food, signage contradicts itself, the lift breaks on a published schedule, staff give visitors conflicting instructions. Nothing is behind glass because the failure is happening to you, the visitor, right now, in the queue and the cloakroom and the broken audio guide. You feel the friction of systems decaying and learn that failure is rarely an event, it is a slow ambient condition you are standing inside before you notice. There is no curatorial voice, only the structure misbehaving.

**4. The Reversal Hall**
A bright, triumphant museum that looks exactly like a hall of fame, except every object on its pedestals is something the world judged a failure that later turned out to seed something vital. Penicillin from a ruined petri dish, the Post-it glue that "didn't work," rejected manuscripts, the failed business whose alumni built three giants. The emotion is vertigo, the floor tilting as you realize the verdict was premature. Spatial logic: every plinth has a hinged placard you physically flip, "the failure" on one face and "what it became" on the other, so you perform the reframe with your own hand. The lesson lives in the time delay between judgment and outcome.

**5. The Confessional Archive**
There are no objects at all. The museum is a vast quiet hall of soundproof booths where the only content is failure that visitors deposit: you enter, record your worst, most private failure anonymously, and in exchange you are given headphones and a stranger's confession to sit with. The "collection" grows every day from its own audience and is curated by an algorithm that pairs each new confession with an uncannily resonant old one. You feel exposure and then strange relief, learning that your most isolating failure is statistically unremarkable, already lived a thousand times in the booth next door. Layout: a honeycomb of identical cells, no center, no hierarchy, no famous failures, only everyone's.

**Strangest / riskiest: #3, The Museum That Is Failing.** It deliberately sabotages the visitor's own day, which courts genuine anger, refund demands, and one-star reviews, and it blurs the line between art and a building that is simply broken. It might still work because it is the only concept that makes failure a present-tense bodily experience instead of a thing you safely observe from behind glass, and the discomfort itself is the irreproducible exhibit no other museum can offer.
