Five report cards, each breaking a different load-bearing assumption about who is measured, what counts as data, and who the document is actually for.

**1. The Wake** — *Audience: the student at 40, not the parent this term.* Drop the subject grid entirely and replace it with a single time-released letter the child cannot open for thirty years. Each teacher contributes one paragraph not about performance but about the specific human they watched: the way she defended a kid no one else would, the question he asked that stopped the room, the thing she was quietly afraid of. It measures what a transcript structurally cannot hold: who the person was becoming, addressed to the only reader who will ever fully understand the reference.

**2. The Friction Log** — *Measures the environment, not the kid.* This card grades the school's fit to the student rather than the student's fit to the school. Every entry is a recorded mismatch: "lost 40 minutes a day to a class scheduled against his energy peak," "the one teacher who reached her left in March," "thrives in groups of three, sat in rows of thirty." It borrows the frame of an accessibility audit or a bug tracker, treating every struggle as a defect report against the institution. The student's failures become the building's failures, and the document is addressed to whoever can change the building.

**3. The Portfolio of Wrongness** — *Inverts the optimization target: measures productive failure.* Grades vanish; the card archives the student's three best mistakes of the term and what each one cracked open. A wrong hypothesis that took two weeks to disprove scores higher than a right answer copied in thirty seconds. It imports the logic of a research lab notebook, where a clean failure is data and a lucky guess is noise. The metric is the depth and honesty of the dead ends, on the bet that a kid who has never been graded for a good wrong turn learns to fear them.

**4. The Constellation** — *Measures the network, not the node.* The student is never assessed alone; the card maps who they made better and who made them better. It tracks the tutoring they gave, the project they unblocked, the classmate whose confidence rose in their orbit, the table that got louder or quieter when they sat down. Borrowing from ecology and social-graph analysis, it treats a classroom as a system of flows and asks what each person does to the whole, because the grid-of-grades can only see isolated atoms and a class is not made of isolated atoms.

**5. The Seismograph** — *Discards the fixed interval; measures trajectory and volatility.* No term-end snapshot. Instead a continuous trace of slope and tremor: the rate of change, the moment something clicked, the plateau, the crash and what preceded it. A student climbing steeply from a low floor reads as a strong signal; a flat line at a high score reads as stalled and at risk. It steals the instruments of seismology and stock-chart technical analysis, reporting derivatives instead of levels, on the principle that where a child is going matters more than where they happen to stand on the day someone measured.

---

**Strangest / riskiest: The Wake.** It abandons every conventional function of a report card (no feedback loop, no parent, no actionable signal, useful to no one for three decades) and gambles the entire document on a payoff that can only be collected once, far in the future. It might still work because it answers the one question school records never do and the one a forty-year-old most wants answered: who was I, really, to the adults who watched me before I knew myself? That is data no grid can store and no other reader can verify.
