## Frame the question

The real decision is not "is $400k good money" (it obviously is). It is: **does taking this contract raise the probability that this company becomes a venture-scale product business, or lower it?** The contract is a bet that trades roadmap time for cash and one customer. I'll grade the bet on what's knowable now, not on how it turns out.

Watch the attribute-substitution trap: "we'd run out of money, $400k fixes that" is the easy question your mind answers instead of the hard one about whether you're still building the thing you set out to build.

## Quantify the runway math

- Burn: 9 months of runway means roughly **$X/month** for the 6 people; the contract pays $400k over 5 months, so ~$80k/month of inflow. Against a typical 6-person burn of $60-90k/month, the contract is **roughly cash-flow-neutral to slightly positive while it runs**, and it pushes the runway cliff out by perhaps 4-6 months once the lump lands.
- So the headline "extends runway" is real but smaller than it sounds: you buy **~4-6 extra months**, and you spend **5 months of 100% team capacity** to get them. The product clock doesn't extend; only the bank clock does.

## The hidden costs (what the frame first excludes)

1. **Opportunity cost / roadmap freeze.** 5 months is ~40% of your remaining year. In an early product company, 5 frozen months can be the difference between hitting PMF signal and not. This is the load-bearing cost and it's unpriced in the $400k.
2. **One-customer specificity.** A feature "only that customer wants" has near-zero reuse value. You're being paid to build someone else's roadmap. Risk of becoming a **disguised dev shop**: the system archetype here is *shifting the burden* — the contract is the easy fix that atrophies the muscle (product velocity, market learning) you actually need.
3. **Team morale and signal.** Builders who joined to make a product often disengage during long bespoke contract work. Attrition during the contract would be catastrophic.
4. **Counterparty/timeline risk.** Enterprise contracts slip, scope-creep, and pay late. A 5-month full-team commitment to one enterprise is a fat-tail exposure: if they delay payment, change scope, or churn, you've burned the runway AND the cash.

## The case *for* taking it

- Cash de-risks death. A dead startup ships nothing.
- An enterprise willing to pay $400k is a strong demand signal; if the custom feature is **adjacent to your core** (80% reusable), this is really paid R&D plus a flagship logo and reference customer.
- It can be a wedge: land-and-expand, case study, future ARR.

## The decision hinges on three diagnostics

1. **Reusability**: Is the feature ≥~50% generalizable into the core roadmap? If yes, this is paid product development and the calculus flips toward yes. If it's truly one-off, lean no.
2. **Strategic fit**: Does this customer/segment represent where you want PMF? Or does it pull you into a market you'd otherwise avoid?
3. **Capacity carve-out**: Must it be the *entire* team? Almost never. Negotiate to **2-3 engineers** on the contract, keep 3 on core. That converts an all-or-nothing bet into a barbell.

## Premortem

It's 6 months out and this was a mistake: you delivered late, the enterprise's champion left, payment stalled, two engineers quit from boredom, the roadmap is 5 months stale against funded competitors, and the next raise has no product-progress story. Every one of those is foreseeable now.

## Verdict

**Default to no — but with a specific escape hatch.** Do not freeze the whole team for a one-off, non-reusable feature; that converts a product company into a contract shop right when product momentum matters most, and the runway extension is only modest (~4-6 months) for a steep price (~40% of your year).

**Take it only if you can restructure it into a positive-expected-value, bounded bet:**
- Carve out **≤50% of the team**, protecting core roadmap progress.
- Require **≥40-50% reusability** into your roadmap, or a meaningfully higher price to compensate for pure opportunity cost.
- Secure **milestone-based payment** (large upfront) to cap counterparty/payment-timing risk.
- Get a **reference-customer + case-study** commitment so you capture market signal, not just cash.

If the enterprise insists on the full team, full bespoke build, slow payment, then this is a roadmap-killing deal dressed as a lifeline. Decline, and instead use the demand signal it revealed to go raise or to sell the *near* version of what they want to several customers.
