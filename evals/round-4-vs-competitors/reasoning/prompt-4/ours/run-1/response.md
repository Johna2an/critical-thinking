**Framing the bet.** The real question is not "is $400k good money?" (it obviously is, against a 9-month clock) but "does taking this contract raise or lower the probability the company is alive and on-mission 18 months from now?" Taking it is a bet: I stake 5 months of my entire team's output and my roadmap momentum, against the alternative future where I spend those 5 months building core product and raising or selling into my actual market. Survival is the hard constraint that sits above expected return, so I evaluate it first.

**The numbers.** 9 months runway for 6 people implies a burn around $55-70k/month (call it $60k, so ~$540k of runway). The $400k contract, net of the cost of consuming the team for 5 months (~$300k of burn), adds roughly $100k of true net cash, plus it defers ~$300k of spend onto the customer's dime. So runway extends from ~9 months to perhaps ~14-15 months. That is real and not trivial. But notice the asymmetry: the headline is $400k; the survival-relevant figure is more like a 5-6 month extension, and zero of those months advance the thing that determines whether the company has a future.

**The base rate (outside view first).** The reference class is "early startup takes a large single-customer custom-build contract." The well-documented base rate here is poor: these contracts routinely convert a product company into a de-facto services/consulting shop, the custom feature rarely generalizes to other customers, and the team emerges 5 months later with a stale roadmap and the same fundamental product-market question unanswered, now on a shorter strategic clock even if a longer cash clock. The inside view ("this customer is huge, the logo helps, maybe the feature does generalize") is exactly the vivid detail that base rates warn you to discount. Adjust off the base rate only modestly.

**Fragility check.** This move concentrates the company on one counterparty for nearly half a year. That is a fragile posture: scope creep, a delayed payment, a champion leaving on the customer side, or an acceptance dispute can each turn the lifeline into a liability, and you have no parallel options running. The barbell logic argues the opposite of an all-in bet: keep a safe base and take capped-downside shots, do not bet the whole team on one concave contract.

**Premortem.** It is 14 months out and the company failed. The most likely story: the team shipped the custom feature, the customer renewed at a smaller services scope or not at all, the core product is 5 months behind competitors, the next fundraise stalled because metrics on the actual product did not move, and morale eroded because engineers built someone else's roadmap. That failure path is disturbingly easy to write, which is itself a signal.

**What would change my answer.** I am not at "nothing could." Concrete falsifiers that flip me toward yes: (1) the feature is 60%+ reusable and sits on the existing roadmap rather than diverging from it; (2) the contract front-loads cash (large deposit, milestone payments) so it is not 5 months of exposure before any money lands; (3) it can be staffed with 2-3 people, not all 6, possibly with a contractor or two, preserving core work; (4) the customer is a credible design partner whose needs proxy the broader market; (5) kill criteria and scope are contractually fixed so it cannot balloon. Hitting most of these turns a fragile all-in into a robust, capped bet.

**Verdict.** Default is **do not take it as structured** (entire team, 5 months, custom-only, roadmap frozen). The cash buys calendar but spends the scarcer resource, which is product momentum and strategic optionality. Counter-offer instead: carve it to a 2-3 person subteam, demand a substantial upfront/milestone payment schedule, contractually fix scope with kill criteria, and require that the build reuse or extend the core roadmap. If the customer accepts that reshaping, take it: it becomes runway plus a marquee design partner with capped downside. If they insist on the whole team building a one-off, decline and use the 9 months to fix the actual problem, which is finding repeatable demand. (Confidence in the directional call: ~70%; the main thing that would move it is the reusability and staffing facts I do not yet have.)
