## Verdict: Conditional yes — take it, but only if you can ring-fence the team and negotiate the terms below. A naked "freeze everything for 5 months" yes is a trap.

### Framing the numbers first
Assumptions (flag if wrong): 6 people, 9 months runway, so your burn is whatever 9 months of payroll plus overhead costs. The $400k roughly funds the 5 months of effort it consumes (it is close to break-even on labor, not pure profit). The real prize is the ~3-4 months of *additional* runway it buys once the contract cash lands and offsets burn. So the decision is buying time, not buying money.

### Opportunity cost (the core lens)
What you give up by saying yes is 5 of your 9 months of remaining team-capacity, applied to a feature exactly one customer wants. The next-best use of that capacity is shipping core roadmap toward product-market fit and a fundraise. Status quo (decline, keep building) is the explicit alternative and it is not safe either: with 9 months of runway and presumably no signed revenue, the do-nothing path has its own failure mode (run out before PMF or a raise). The contract's true value is that it converts a hard 9-month cliff into a longer, partially-funded runway. The true cost is 5 months of roadmap progress at the most fragile stage of the company. Whether that trade is worth it hinges entirely on what those 5 months of core work were realistically going to produce. If your roadmap was on a clear line to PMF or a term sheet, the opportunity cost is enormous and you should decline or shrink the deal. If your roadmap was speculative and you had no near-term funding catalyst, the contract is the more certain bet.

### Second-order effects (and then what?)
- First order: runway extends, immediate death risk drops.
- Second order: you become a services/consulting shop for one enterprise. Sales and your own attention reorient around that customer. The custom feature is dead weight to every other prospect.
- Third order: 5 months later the contract ends, the team returns to a roadmap that is now stale relative to competitors who kept shipping, and you may have *trained the org* to chase the next big check instead of the product. This is the classic services-trap feedback loop: each contract buys survival and erodes the product flywheel that justifies the company's existence and valuation.
- Counter-effect that helps: if the feature, the logo, or the contract structure feeds back into the core product (reference customer, reusable platform work, a beachhead in a vertical), the loop becomes reinforcing instead of draining.

### Pre-mortem (assume it failed)
It is 7 months out and this was a disaster. Likely causes: (1) scope ballooned past 5 months because enterprise requirements always expand, and the fixed $400k now funds 8 months of work, torching the runway it was supposed to save; (2) payment was net-60/90 or milestone-gated, so cash arrived too late to matter; (3) the feature was genuinely single-customer and bought zero product leverage; (4) two engineers burned out or quit mid-contract and you lost capacity you priced as fixed; (5) the customer churned or renegotiated and you absorbed the loss; (6) you lost your fundraising window because you had no roadmap progress to show.

### What turns this from a trap into a good deal (negotiate these)
1. Money up front or fast milestones — a meaningful deposit and net-30 max, so cash actually extends runway.
2. Cap and lock scope with paid change-orders — protect against the 5 months becoming 8.
3. Carve out 15-20% of capacity (or 1 person) to keep core product alive so you do not return to a fully frozen roadmap.
4. Capture reusable value — build the feature on your core platform, keep IP rights, and get the logo as a named reference.
5. Confirm it is not actually a wedge into a repeatable enterprise segment, in which case lean in harder.

If you cannot get cash terms and scope protection, the contract is more likely to accelerate death than prevent it, and you should decline. With those terms, it is a rational bridge that buys time to reach PMF or a raise.
