**The argument, mapped**

- Premise 1: Since the 2020 shift to full remote, revenue per employee fell 12%.
- Premise 2: Some competitors who stayed in-office grew.
- Hidden premise (unstated, load-bearing): remote work *caused* the decline, and removing it will *reverse* it.
- Conclusion (prescriptive): mandate a full 5-day return to office.

The structure is "after this, therefore because of this, therefore undo this." The whole case hangs on the hidden premise, and that is exactly where it breaks.

**Flaw 1 — Post hoc and rival causes (the central error).** 2020 is the most confounded date in modern business history. A correlation between two events is consistent with at least five stories, and here the field of rival causes for a revenue dip is crowded: the pandemic-era demand shock and recovery, supply chains, inflation and rate hikes, sector-specific cycles, pricing changes, a single lost large account, or simple regression after a 2019 high. "Remote work" is one candidate among many, and not the prior favorite. No mechanism is offered for *how* remote work depressed revenue. Until those rivals are listed and discriminated against, causation is unearned.

**Flaw 2 — The competitor comparison is selection-biased and underspecified.** "Competitors who stayed in-office grew" invites survivorship and cherry-picking. Out of how many in-office competitors? The ones that stayed in-office *and failed* are invisible. To make the case you would need the *base rate*: growth among in-office firms vs. growth among remote firms across the whole field, not a hand-picked few. As stated, this is "look, it happened" with no denominator.

**Flaw 3 — The metric is a black box, and "per employee" hides a second story.** Revenue-per-employee is a ratio. It can fall because revenue dropped *or because headcount grew* (hiring ahead of revenue, an acquisition, lower attrition). A 12% fall could even coexist with rising total revenue. Read the inputs before the output: we are never told whether the numerator or the denominator moved. The chosen proxy may not measure "productivity" at all — and optimizing a return-to-office to lift this proxy could miss the real driver entirely.

**Flaw 4 — "Clearly" and "destroying" are doing argumentative work.** "Clearly" marks the spot where an author asserts instead of argues, and "destroying" is loaded language importing the verdict before any evidence arrives. Strip both and the certainty evaporates.

**Flaw 5 — False dilemma in the remedy.** The argument jumps from "remote may be costing us" straight to "full five days in office," skipping every option C: hybrid (2–3 days), team-by-team policy, fixing specific collaboration or onboarding gaps, or addressing the actual revenue driver. Even if remote were partly to blame, "full five-day mandate" does not follow.

**Flaw 6 — Resulting and small-sample.** One firm, one outcome, one period. Judging the 2020 *decision* by this *result* is resulting; treating a single noisy data point as a stable signal ignores regression to the mean.

**Flaw 7 — Affirming the consequent / reversibility assumed.** Even granting remote hurt revenue, it does not follow that reversing it restores the prior state. The 2020 baseline is gone; the market, the workforce, and competitors have moved. And a forced return carries its own costs the argument omits: attrition of people who will not return, recruiting from a smaller pool, morale, real-estate spend. These belong on the decision tree and are absent.

**What would actually settle it.** Decompose revenue-per-employee into numerator and denominator over time; pull the base rate of growth for in-office vs. remote peers across the full set; quantify the pandemic/macro contribution; and run a premortem on the mandate (what attrition would make it a net loss?). State a falsifier in advance: "if peer remote firms grew at the same rate as in-office ones, remote is not the cause."

**Verdict.** The argument is unsound. It rests on a post-hoc causal leap, a selection-biased comparison, an unexamined ratio metric, and a false-dilemma remedy, dressed up with "clearly" and "destroying." On the evidence given, the probability that full remote is the primary cause of the 12% fall is low (I would put it well under 30%), and the probability that a five-day mandate is the best available fix is lower still. Recommendation: do not mandate the return on this basis. Run the decomposition and the peer base-rate analysis first; if remote genuinely shows a cost, address the specific mechanism with the least disruptive intervention (likely targeted hybrid), not a blanket five-day order.
