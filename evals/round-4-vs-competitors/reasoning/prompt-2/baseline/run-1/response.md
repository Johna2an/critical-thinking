**Critique: A weak causal argument resting on confounded data and a false binary.**

The argument has a clean surface (premise, premise, conclusion) but nearly every load-bearing joint is cracked. Here are the flaws, roughly in order of severity.

**1. Confounding: 2020 changed far more than the office.** The decisive flaw. The company went remote *in the same year* a global pandemic hit. Supply shocks, demand collapse, inflation, layoffs, hiring freezes, customer churn, and macro recession all landed simultaneously. Attributing a 12% revenue/employee drop to remote work alone requires ignoring every other variable that moved at once. This is a textbook third-variable problem.

**2. Correlation treated as causation.** "Ever since X, Y fell" establishes temporal sequence, not mechanism. Post hoc ergo propter hoc. No causal pathway is shown linking remote work to the specific revenue decline.

**3. The metric is a ratio, and the denominator is unexamined.** Revenue per employee = revenue ÷ headcount. A 12% fall can come from flat revenue with a *growing* headcount, or falling revenue with steady headcount, or any mix. If the company hired aggressively (common 2020–2022), revenue/employee mechanically drops even with healthy total revenue. We aren't told which. The headline number is uninterpretable without decomposition.

**4. Selection bias in the competitor comparison.** "Competitors who stayed in-office grew" cherry-picks survivors who happened to grow. We don't hear about in-office competitors that *shrank* or died, or remote competitors that *thrived*. Comparing one self-selected slice to your own firm is not a controlled comparison: the in-office firms likely differ in sector, product, funding, and starting position.

**5. Missing base rate / counterfactual.** What would *this* company's revenue/employee have done had it stayed in-office through 2020? Possibly worse (office disruption, sick staff, no contingency). The argument assumes the counterfactual is "+growth," but never establishes it.

**6. False dichotomy.** The options aren't "fully remote" vs. "five days in-office." Hybrid schedules, partial RTO, team-by-team policies, and targeted fixes (onboarding, collaboration tooling) all exist. The argument collapses a spectrum into two poles.

**7. Non-sequitur from diagnosis to remedy.** Even if remote work *did* cause the drop, it doesn't follow that a *full five-day* mandate is the right dose. Maybe two days recovers most of the benefit at a fraction of the cost (attrition, morale, real-estate, narrowed talent pool). The remedy is asserted, not sized.

**8. Reversibility assumed.** "Restore productivity" presumes the decline is fully reversible by reversing the one change. If causes are external or structural, mandating RTO restores nothing and adds costs.

**9. Ignored downside risks.** A five-day mandate has well-documented costs: voluntary attrition of top performers, harder recruiting, and morale hits. None are weighed. The argument optimizes one number while ignoring the system around it.

**What evidence would actually settle this:** decompose the ratio (revenue trend vs. headcount trend separately); benchmark against the *full* set of peers including failures; isolate the COVID/macro shock (e.g., compare to 2019 baselines and industry indices); and, ideally, run a controlled pilot — RTO for some teams, remote for matched others — to measure a real treatment effect.

**Verdict:** The argument is unsound. It confuses correlation with causation, leans on a confounded year, uses an undecomposed ratio, cherry-picks competitors, and jumps from a shaky diagnosis to an oversized, one-option remedy. The 12% figure is real but uninterpreted; it does not license a full RTO mandate.

**Recommendation:** Don't mandate yet. First decompose revenue vs. headcount, benchmark against all peers (not just the winners), and net out the 2020 macro shock. If a remote-specific drag survives that scrutiny, pilot a hybrid policy on matched teams and let the measured effect — and a real cost-benefit including attrition — size the response.
