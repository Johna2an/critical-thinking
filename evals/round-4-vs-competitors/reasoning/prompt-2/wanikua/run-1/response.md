## The Argument, Restated

P1: Revenue per employee fell 12% after going fully remote in 2020.
P2: In-office competitors grew.
C: Therefore remote work is destroying productivity, so mandate a 5-day return to office to restore it.

It looks tight, but nearly every joint between the premises and the conclusion is loose. Here are the flaws, grouped.

## 1. Correlation treated as causation (the central flaw)
"Ever since X, Y fell, therefore X caused Y" is post hoc reasoning. 2020 changed dozens of things at once: a global pandemic, demand shocks, supply-chain disruption, hiring surges, inflation, interest-rate swings, and possible product or market shifts. Remote work is one candidate cause competing with all of these. The argument never rules out a single alternative.

## 2. The metric does not measure what the conclusion claims
The conclusion is about *productivity*; the evidence is *revenue per employee*. These come apart easily:
- If headcount grew faster than revenue (aggressive hiring, juniors not yet ramped), the ratio falls even if every worker is equally or more productive. A denominator effect, not a productivity effect.
- Revenue is driven by pricing, demand, churn, and sales mix, much of which is exogenous to how hard people work.
- Productivity properly means output per unit input. Revenue/employee is a financial ratio, not an output measure.

## 3. Survivorship and selection bias in the competitor comparison
"Competitors who stayed in-office grew" cherry-picks survivors. We are not told how many in-office competitors *shrank or died*, nor how many remote firms *grew*. Without the full 2x2 (remote/in-office x grew/shrank), the comparison is anecdotal. The competitors may differ in sector, funding, product cycle, or geography, none controlled for.

## 4. No baseline, no counterfactual
There is no evidence of what revenue/employee would have done had the company stayed in-office through 2020-2024. Maybe the firm was already declining and remote work cushioned a steeper fall. The 12% is presented without trend context (was it falling before?) or industry benchmark (did the whole sector fall 12%?).

## 5. Quantitative looseness
- "12%" over roughly four to six years is about 2-3% annually, plausibly within normal noise for a single firm.
- "Competitors grew" gives no magnitude. Grew 1% or 40%? Over what period?
- Single-company n=1 on one side, an unspecified handful on the other. No statistical weight.

## 6. False dichotomy in the remedy
The options are framed as "fully remote" vs "full five-day RTO," ignoring hybrid (2-3 days), team-by-team policies, or fixing the actual underlying cause if it is not location at all. Even if remote *were* a drag, the proportionate fix might be two anchor days, not five.

## 7. The remedy doesn't follow even if the diagnosis held
"Restore productivity" assumes RTO reverses the loss with no offsetting costs. Red-team scenario: mandating 5-day RTO triggers attrition of the highest performers (most mobile, most options), real-estate and commute costs return, hiring radius shrinks, and revenue/employee falls *further*. The cure plausibly worsens the very metric cited.

## 8. Motivated reasoning / confirmation framing
"Clearly destroying" front-loads the conclusion. The word "clearly" does rhetorical work that the evidence does not earn.

## What would actually settle it
Decompose revenue/employee into revenue trend and headcount trend; benchmark against sector peers over the same window; build the full remote-vs-office competitor 2x2; isolate cohorts hired pre- vs post-2020; and check whether the decline tracks macro shocks rather than the work-location date. A small RTO pilot on volunteer teams would test causation before a company-wide mandate.

## Verdict
The conclusion rates **Weak**. Every link is contestable: correlation is assumed to be causation, the metric is a proxy that doesn't track the claim, the competitor evidence is survivorship-biased and uncontrolled, no counterfactual or baseline is offered, and the proposed remedy is a false dichotomy that could backfire. Recommendation: do not mandate a 5-day RTO on this basis. Decompose the metric, benchmark against peers, and run a small controlled pilot before any company-wide change.
