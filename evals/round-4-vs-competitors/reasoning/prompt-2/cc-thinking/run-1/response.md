## Critique: "Remote work is destroying our productivity"

The argument has a clean surface logic, but nearly every load-bearing step is unsupported. I'll take it apart by flaw, then give a verdict.

**1. Correlation treated as causation (the central flaw).** The whole case rests on "since we went remote, revenue per employee fell 12%." 2020 is the single most confounded year in modern business history: a global pandemic, supply shocks, demand collapse in many sectors, hiring freezes, and macro turmoil all hit simultaneously. Remote work is one of dozens of things that changed at once. Apply the counterfactual test, the cleanest way to expose this: *would the 12% drop NOT have happened if the company had stayed in-office?* Nothing in the argument shows that. Many in-office firms in hard-hit sectors also fell. Until you can answer that counterfactual with evidence, "remote caused it" is unproven.

**2. Cherry-picked, undefined comparison group.** "Competitors who stayed in-office grew" is selection bias on display. We're not told how many competitors stayed in-office, how many of *those* shrank, or how many remote competitors grew (many tech firms boomed remote in 2020-21). Citing only the in-office winners while ignoring in-office losers and remote winners is survivorship/selection bias. The honest comparison needs the full 2x2: remote-grew, remote-shrank, office-grew, office-shrank. We're shown one cell.

**3. Wrong metric for the conclusion.** "Revenue per employee" is not "productivity." It moves with pricing, demand, product mix, sales cycles, and especially **headcount**. If the company hired aggressively in 2020-21 (common then), the denominator grew faster than revenue and the ratio falls even if every worker got more done. Falling revenue/employee is consistent with rising output per worker. The metric doesn't measure what the conclusion claims.

**4. Confounded "competitors."** Competitors differ in sector, size, capital, and product, not just office policy. Attributing their growth solely to being in-office is a single-variable explanation for a many-variable outcome.

**5. Magnitude and baseline are unexamined.** Is 12% large relative to normal year-to-year variance? Over what period, peak to trough or steady decline? Has it already recovered by 2024-25? A one-time 2020 dip that has since reversed argues *against* a structural remote-work problem.

**6. The remedy doesn't follow even if the diagnosis were right.** Suppose remote *did* cost something. The conclusion jumps straight to the maximal, costliest intervention: a full five-day mandate. That ignores hybrid options (2-3 days), team-level differences, and the real costs of a hard mandate: attrition of your best people (who have the most outside options), lower morale, recruiting disadvantage, and real estate expense. This is a false dichotomy (fully remote vs. five-day office) with the extreme option asserted, not argued.

**7. Reversibility ignored.** A blanket mandate that triggers senior attrition is hard to undo. The argument commits to an irreversible, high-cost action on weak evidence.

**What would actually support the claim:** a within-company comparison of teams or individuals by remote intensity holding role constant; output metrics (units shipped, tickets closed, deals won per rep) rather than a revenue ratio; the full competitor distribution including losers; and a control for the 2020 macro shock (e.g., trend vs. industry peers).

## Verdict

The argument fails. It is a chain of correlation-as-causation, a cherry-picked comparison, and a metric that doesn't measure productivity, capped by a remedy that wouldn't follow even if the diagnosis held. **Do not mandate five days back on this evidence.** Recommended next step: run the within-firm analysis above on real output metrics, benchmark against the full peer set adjusted for the 2020 shock, and if a remote penalty genuinely appears, test the cheapest reversible fix (targeted hybrid for affected teams) before imposing a company-wide irreversible mandate. Current confidence that remote work is the cause of the 12% drop: low.
