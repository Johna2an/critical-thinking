**Verdict up front: No, you should not believe that coffee reduces depression on this evidence, and you should especially not believe a coffee-extract supplement will. The study is consistent with a real protective effect, but it is equally consistent with several mundane explanations the design cannot rule out. Treat the brand's claim as marketing built on a correlation.**

**1. Pin the claim and catch the swap.** The study reports an *association* (descriptive: coffee drinkers have lower depression rates). The brand asserts *causation plus a product bridge* (prescriptive: take our extract to lower your risk). Three separate leaps are stacked here: correlation → causation → "and a supplement reproduces it." Each leap needs its own evidence, and none is supplied.

**2. List the rival causes before accepting theirs.** A correlation between coffee (X) and lower depression (Y) fits at least five stories, and four of them are not "coffee helps":
- **Reverse causation (Y→X):** depressed people drink *less* coffee. Anhedonia, fatigue, social withdrawal, and disrupted routines all cut coffee intake. This alone could produce the entire effect, and it is highly plausible.
- **Confounding (Z→both):** coffee-drinking tracks employment, social life (coffee is social), income, regular daily structure, and overall health. Each of those independently protects against depression. This is the strongest rival.
- **Coincidence / it's a fishing result:** if researchers tested many cutpoints (1 cup, 2 cups, 3+) and exposures, "two cups" may be the slice that happened to clear p<0.01.
- **Coffee → less depression** is only the fifth candidate, and observational data cannot privilege it over the first four.

**3. Interrogate the statistics.** p<0.01 answers "could this be chance?" not "is it big or real?" With n=8,000, even a tiny or wholly confounded difference clears significance easily; significance here is nearly free and is not evidence of size or of causation. The 20% is a *relative* reduction, missing its denominator: if base depression is ~8%, a 20% cut is ~1.6 absolute percentage points, modest and easily eaten by confounders. We're also not told it's adjusted; an unadjusted gap is almost certainly inflated by the lifestyle factors above.

**4. The semi-attached figure (the supplement leap).** Even granting that *drinking brewed coffee* is protective, that result does not transfer to a *coffee-extract supplement*. Different compound profile, different dose, no caffeine-context, and no study on the pill itself. The brand proves one thing (an association with a beverage) and sells another (a capsule). This is the classic substitution of an unearned claim for a real one, and the seller is not impartial, which discounts the source further.

**5. Prescriptive overreach and the missing falsifier.** Be most suspicious when a correlational study is turned into a "do this" recommendation. Ask what would make the claim false: a randomized trial where coffee/extract is *assigned* (breaking self-selection) and depression is measured prospectively. None of that is on the table. Note also the broader base rate: most nutrition/wellness correlations that reach headlines fail to replicate in randomized trials, so the prior on "this causal story survives an RCT" is low (a minority, plausibly well under a third).

**What would move me:** (a) a randomized controlled trial of caffeine or the actual extract showing reduced incidence; (b) a dose-response gradient that persists after adjusting for income, employment, social activity, and baseline health; (c) replication across independent cohorts; (d) a plausible mechanism tested directly.

**Bottom-line recommendation:** Drink coffee if you enjoy it; it's cheap and mostly benign. Do not buy the extract to prevent depression. The honest reading of n=8,000 with p<0.01 is "an interesting correlation worth a real trial," and the brand has converted that into a causal sales pitch it has not earned. Confidence that coffee *causes* lower depression, on this study alone: low, roughly 15-25%. Confidence the *supplement* does: lower still.
