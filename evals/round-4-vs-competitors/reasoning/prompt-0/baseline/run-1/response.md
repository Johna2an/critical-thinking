**Short answer: No. The study, even taken at face value, does not show that coffee reduces depression, and the supplement pitch adds a second leap on top of the first.**

**Frame the question.** Two separate claims are riding together. (1) The descriptive finding: coffee drinkers have ~20% lower depression rates. (2) The causal claim the brand wants you to accept: drinking coffee *causes* lower depression. (3) The product claim: a coffee-*extract supplement* delivers that same benefit. Each step is weaker than the last.

**Correlation is not cause; list the rival explanations first.** This is observational, so before crediting causation I have to clear the alternatives:
- **Reverse causation.** Depression blunts energy, motivation, and appetite, including for coffee. People who are already low may drink less coffee. The arrow could run depression → less coffee, not coffee → less depression.
- **Confounding.** Coffee drinking tracks with employment, social routines, regular work hours, income, and physical health. A person holding down a job with morning coffee rituals differs from a non-drinker in dozens of ways that independently lower depression risk. Unless the study adjusted for these (and "study reports a raw 20%" suggests it may not), the coffee is a marker, not a lever.
- **Selection / who abstains.** Some non-drinkers avoid coffee precisely because of anxiety, poor sleep, medication, or illness, conditions linked to depression. The comparison group is contaminated.

**Interrogate the numbers.** "20% lower rate" is a *relative* reduction with no baseline given. If depression prevalence is 10%, this is 10% vs 8%, a 2 percentage-point absolute difference. Relative figures inflate the felt importance; always ask "out of what?" And p<0.01 only says the association is unlikely to be pure chance in this sample, it says nothing about effect size, causation, or confounding. With n=8,000, even a trivial real difference clears significance easily. A small p-value is not a large or causal effect.

**The dose claim is unsupported.** "Two cups a day" is one slice. Was there a dose-response gradient (more coffee, monotonically less depression)? Or is two cups just where the data happened to peak? A single cut-point with no gradient is a red flag for noise or cherry-picking the strongest contrast.

**The supplement is a bait-and-switch.** Even if coffee itself were protective, the brand sells a *coffee-extract supplement*, not coffee. Coffee is a complex matrix (caffeine, chlorogenic acids, polyphenols, plus the behavioral ritual and social context). An extract isolates some compounds and drops the rest, including the morning-routine and social mechanisms that may be doing the real work. The study did not test the pill. This is the classic move: borrow the credibility of a whole-food correlation to sell a processed isolate. Follow the incentive (Gall): the brand exists to sell the supplement, so it will frame any friendly finding as proof.

**Selective-skeptic check.** If a study with this exact design (observational, raw 20%, single dose point) found coffee *raised* depression, the same brand would call it confounded and demand a randomized trial. That asymmetry is the tell.

**What would actually move me:** a randomized controlled trial of the *supplement* (not coffee) versus placebo, showing a clinically meaningful drop in depression with a dose-response pattern, replicated, ideally pre-registered. Mendelian randomization on caffeine metabolism would also help untangle reverse causation. None of that is on offer here.

**Verdict.** Believe only the modest descriptive fact: in this sample, coffee drinkers reported somewhat less depression. Do not believe coffee *reduces* depression, the design cannot support it, and reverse causation plus confounding are live and plausible. Do not believe the supplement does anything, it was never tested. Treat the brand's citation as marketing dressed in a p-value. Practical advice: if you enjoy coffee, fine; do not buy the extract on this evidence. One honest caveat: there *is* broader literature with some signal for caffeine and mood, so the underlying hypothesis is not absurd, but this study and this product do not establish it.
