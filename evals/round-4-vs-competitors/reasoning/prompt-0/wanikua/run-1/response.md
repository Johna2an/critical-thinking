## Should you believe coffee reduces depression? Verdict: No, not from this.

The study is evidence that coffee drinking and lower depression *travel together*. It is weak evidence that coffee *causes* lower depression, and the wellness brand's pitch overshoots it badly.

### Set up the competing hypotheses
The same correlation is consistent with at least four stories:
- **H1 (causal):** Coffee/caffeine biologically lowers depression risk.
- **H2 (reverse causation):** Depression lowers coffee drinking. Depressed and pre-depressed people lose energy, appetite, and routines, and often cut caffeine because it worsens anxiety or sleep. The arrow runs the other way.
- **H3 (confounding):** A third factor drives both. Healthy, employed, socially active people with steady morning routines drink more coffee *and* are less depressed. Income, employment, social contact, and baseline health are textbook confounders.
- **H4 (artifact):** Selection, measurement, or analysis quirks (who got surveyed, how "depression" was self-reported, garden-of-forking-paths in the analysis).

### What does the evidence actually move?
My prior on H1 being the *full* explanation is low, maybe 15 to 20%. Caffeine has plausible mechanisms (adenosine, dopamine), and some prior literature leans this way, so it isn't crazy. But H2, H3, and H4 are individually very plausible for any cross-sectional coffee-mood association, so most of the prior mass sits there.

Now the key Bayesian point: **how diagnostic is this finding?** A 20% lower rate with p<0.01 in n=8,000 is exactly what you'd expect to see under H2 and H3 too. The evidence is roughly equally likely whether coffee protects mood or whether healthy/routine-having people simply drink more coffee. When evidence is about equally consistent with several hypotheses, it barely shifts the posterior between them, no matter how statistically "significant" it looks. So my posterior on the strict causal claim moves only modestly, to perhaps 20 to 25%.

### Red-team the specific numbers
- **"p<0.01" is being asked to do work it can't.** Significance addresses "is this likely just noise," not "is this causal." With n=8,000 even a trivial or fully confounded association clears p<0.01 easily.
- **20% relative, not absolute.** If baseline depression is ~8%, a "20% lower rate" is ~6.4%, an absolute gap of ~1.6 points. Real, but small, and the kind of gap a modest confounder can manufacture.
- **Observational, cross-sectional, single study.** No randomization, so confounders are uncontrolled by design. We aren't told whether they adjusted for income, employment, exercise, or sleep, and "adjustment" never fully removes confounding anyway. One study is also not a literature; publication and the garden of forking paths inflate first reports.
- **The dose-claim is invented.** "Two cups a day" is one slice of the data. Selling that as the optimum hints at cherry-picked subgroups.

### The product is the weakest link
Even if you fully granted H1, the brand commits a second, larger leap: from *coffee, as drunk by these people* to *a coffee-extract supplement*. That is a different molecule, dose, and matrix in a different population. Coffee contains hundreds of compounds; the mood signal (if real) might be caffeine, behavior (the morning ritual, getting outside, socializing over coffee), or none of the above. None of that transfers to a capsule. This is the part to distrust most.

### What would change my mind
Move me toward causation: a randomized trial of caffeine vs. placebo showing lower depression scores; consistent replication across cohorts; a dose-response gradient that survives adjustment; Mendelian-randomization evidence; a mechanism confirmed in humans. Move me further away: the effect vanishing after adjusting for income/employment/exercise, or appearing only in one subgroup.

### Verdict
Do not believe that coffee reduces depression on this basis, and definitely do not buy the supplement for that reason. Reasonable read: coffee drinking is a **marker** of people who are already less depressed, with reverse causation and confounding the front-running explanations. Posterior on a real, useful causal effect of coffee: maybe 20 to 25%. Posterior that *this supplement* delivers a mood benefit: very low. If you enjoy coffee, keep drinking it; that is a fine reason on its own.
