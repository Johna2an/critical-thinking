## Reframing the problem

The failed fixes (parking, rents, festival) share one hidden premise: that downtown's job is **retail**, and the cure is to make shopping there cheaper or more pleasant. That premise is probably the real failure. Five reframings worth holding:

1. **The substitution, not the symptom.** "Foot traffic collapsed" answers the wrong question. People aren't choosing not to come downtown; they're choosing Amazon, curbside pickup, and the screen. Downtown lost a competition it didn't know it was in, against the lowest-friction shopping ever invented. You cannot out-convenience a phone. So stop competing on the dimension you lose.

2. **A supply problem disguised as a demand problem.** Officials assume "nobody wants to come." But foot traffic is downstream of who *sleeps* nearby. Most dying downtowns are zoned for daytime commerce and almost no residents. A street with 4,000 residents within a 5-minute walk does not die; one with 200 does. The shops didn't leave because of parking; they left because there was no resident base to sustain weekday demand once office and retail habits shifted.

3. **A monoculture, not a downturn.** Like Jacobs' dying districts: single-use zoning produced single-use timing. Everything opens 9–6, so the street is dead 6pm–9am and all weekend. A monoculture has no antifragility; one shock (e-commerce, remote work) kills the whole crop at once.

4. **Goodhart on "vibrancy."** "More events" and "more parking" optimize a proxy (bodies present on event days) while the underlying stock (permanent reasons to be there) keeps draining. A festival is a sugar high that papers over the structural deficit.

5. **The wrong unit of analysis.** The city treats "downtown" as the patient. The real unit may be **the individual storefront economics**: rent is a small line item next to staffing, insurance, and the cost of a slow Tuesday. Lower rent on a dead street is still a money-loser.

## Three unconventional options worth testing

**1. Convert vacant retail to residential and maker space (zoning + a vacancy tax), not new shops.**
Reasoning: this attacks reframing #2 directly. Demand can't be summoned by events; it has to live there. Allow ground-floor and upper-floor conversion to apartments, studios, clinics, daycares, trades. A modest vacant-storefront tax flips the landlord incentive: holding empty for a fantasy tenant gets expensive, so they accept residential/maker tenants now. *Test:* rezone 2–3 blocks, 12–18 months, measure resident headcount and weekday 7pm foot traffic. Cheap, reversible, and it builds the customer base the *surviving* shops need.

**2. Anchor with a non-retail daytime generator the internet can't ship.**
Reasoning: reframing #1 says compete where the phone can't. People still need to be physically somewhere: a community college satellite campus, a clinic cluster, a government service one-stop, a coworking hub. These import hundreds of bodies daily who buy lunch and coffee as a byproduct. *Test:* relocate one mid-size public tenant downtown, measure spillover spend in a 2-block radius vs. a control area.

**3. "Empty by default, full by exception" — turn the street into reversible infrastructure.**
Reasoning: attacks the monoculture (#3) and the festival sugar-high (#5). Instead of one summer festival, make low-cost, repeated activation the *default state*: pop-up leases (30-day, plug-and-play), permanent regular night/weekend markets, closed-street programming on a fixed cadence so habits form. The goal is a *standing* reason to show up weekly, not one annual spike.

## Verdict

Bet hardest on **Option 1 (residential conversion + vacancy tax)**. It is the only one that fixes the demand *stock* rather than renting it for a day, it is the highest-leverage point (rules and incentives, per the systems hierarchy), it is cheap and reversible, and the other two compound off it. Run all three as parallel low-cost experiments on a few blocks; kill what doesn't move weekday-evening foot traffic within 18 months.

**Key uncertainties:** conversion costs and code can be prohibitive in some building stock; a vacancy tax invites lobbying and legal challenge; and any downtown with a deeper cause (crime, a collapsed regional employer, a competing mall) needs that diagnosed first, since none of these fix an exodus driven by safety or jobs. Confidence that residential density beats more retail or more events: ~75%.
