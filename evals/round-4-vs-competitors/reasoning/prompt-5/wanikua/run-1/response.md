## The buried assumption

Everything tried (parking, rents, festivals) assumes the goal is "get more people to come downtown to shop." But shopping itself is the dying method, not the goal. The real goal is a financially viable, inhabited center. Retail was just the historical vehicle for it, and e-commerce killed that vehicle structurally, not locally. If you accept that, the city has been pouring money into reviving a corpse instead of asking what a downtown is FOR once shopping leaves.

## Non-obvious reframings

1. **It's not a demand problem, it's a supply-mix problem.** Foot traffic didn't collapse because people stopped wanting downtowns. It collapsed because downtown only sells one thing (retail) and that thing moved online. A district with one revenue model is as fragile as a one-crop farm.

2. **The landlords are the actual customers, and they're optimizing against the city.** Empty storefronts often persist because holding a vacancy preserves a high "market rent" on the books (for loan covenants and asset valuation) while a signed cheap lease would crystallize a loss. Vacancy can be economically rational for the owner even as it kills the street. The visible problem (closed shops) is a symptom of a financing incentive nobody is addressing.

3. **Downtown's competitor isn't the suburban mall, it's the living room.** The thing Amazon and streaming cannot ship is co-presence (being among other people doing something). So the asset to sell is reasons to be physically together, not goods.

4. **A festival is a defibrillator, not a pacemaker.** One-off events prove latent demand exists, then let it evaporate. The failure of festivals is actually evidence the place CAN draw crowds. The missing piece is permanence, not appeal.

## Three unconventional options worth testing

**Option A: Tax the vacancy, or take it.** Pass a progressive vacancy tax (rising with months empty), paired with a city-run "master lease" program: the city leases empty storefronts at the depressed real rent, shielding the owner's headline valuation, then sublets at near-zero to pop-ups, makers, nonprofits, and first-time founders. This attacks reframing #2 directly: it changes the landlord's payoff so that an occupied space beats a held vacancy. Reasoning: the binding constraint is an incentive misalignment, not a shortage of would-be tenants. Test it on two blocks for 18 months and measure occupancy and street footfall against a control block.

**Option B: Rezone downtown to be lived in, not visited.** The cheapest foot traffic is residents who never leave. Convert upper floors and dead retail to housing (especially small, cheap units for students, seniors, remote workers), and legalize ground-floor uses that retail zoning usually bans: clinics, daycares, trade schools, workshops, a library annex, a gym. This follows first principles: people sustain a place by living their daily lives in it, not by making special trips. The kernel is that residents generate demand 365 days a year; shoppers generate it on good-weather Saturdays. Pilot via adaptive-reuse permitting fast-tracks on one corridor.

**Option C: Sell co-presence as the product.** Recruit uses that are inherently social and online-proof and cluster them so they feed each other: a food hall, live music, recreation leagues, maker classes, a coworking floor, evening markets. The provocation here ("make the constraint the feature"): Amazon's strength is solitary convenience, so weaponize its blind spot. Anchor with one always-on draw (a community college satellite or a transit hub) that guarantees a daily baseline crowd the way a mall anchor once did.

## Verdict

The downtown is dying because it's a monoculture whose single crop (retail) was structurally displaced, and because landlord incentives reward vacancy. Stop trying to restore shoppers. Run a controlled 18-to-24-month pilot on a few blocks combining all three: a vacancy tax plus city master-leasing (A) to break the incentive trap immediately, residential conversion (B) to build a permanent daily population, and clustered social/experiential uses (C) to sell what cannot be shipped. Sequence A first (fast, cheap, changes behavior), B as the structural backbone, C as the draw. Measure occupancy, residential units, and weekday-evening footfall against control blocks. The biggest single lever, if you could only pull one, is challenging the assumption that storefronts must hold retail at all. Highest uncertainty: whether enough residential demand exists in a shrinking mid-sized city, which is exactly why you pilot rather than commit citywide.
