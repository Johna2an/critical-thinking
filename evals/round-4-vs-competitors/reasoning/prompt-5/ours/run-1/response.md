**First, solve the right problem.** "Downtown is dying" is a symptom dressed as a problem. The tried-and-failed fixes (parking, rents, festivals) share one buried premise: the job is to refill the existing retail box from the demand side. If that premise is wrong, every fix inside it fails for the same structural reason. So challenge the premise rather than iterate inside it.

**Non-obvious reframings:**

1. **A supply-side fixed-cost problem, not a demand problem.** Storefronts carry retail-grade rent, rigid leases, and zoning that permits only a narrow band of uses. The reinforcing loop: vacancy lowers traffic, lower traffic kills the next tenant, more vacancy. The binding constraint is the cost and rigidity of the box, not the absence of a festival.

2. **Competing on the wrong axis.** Downtown is fighting suburban malls and Amazon on convenience and price, a fight it cannot win. Reframe it to compete on what cannot be shipped: presence, dwell time, the third place. Retail is a lagging indicator of foot traffic, not its cause. People do not drive downtown to buy socks; they buy socks because they are already there.

3. **Measuring the wrong stock.** "Occupied storefronts" and "retail sales" are legible proxies. The stock that keeps a district alive is hours of human presence per block per day, especially evenings and weekends. A district can be 75% leased and dead, or 60% leased and alive. Optimizing occupancy can produce zombie leases that add no presence (Goodhart).

4. **The high-modernist reading.** Every prior fix was top-down and single-shot (one festival, one parking deck). Healthy districts grow bottom-up from many small experiments by people with local knowledge. The city pulled weak leverage points (subsidies, numbers) and ignored strong ones (rules, information flows).

**Three unconventional options (cheap, reversible, falsifiable):**

**A. Legalize the small.** Rewrite zoning and lease norms so a storefront can flex across uses (day-care by morning, workshop by afternoon, supper club by night); permit live-work and residential above and behind retail; pre-clear carts, kiosks, pop-ups. This pushes the strongest cheap levers (rules, information flow). You cannot run bottom-up experiments if each needs a six-month permit. *Test:* suspend rules on two blocks for 12 months, count net new uses and presence-hours. *Falsifier:* if vacancy does not fall and presence does not rise in a year, the constraint was demand, not regulation.

**B. Subsidize residents and anchors, not retail.** Fund upper-floor housing and a non-retail daytime anchor (community-college satellite, clinic, municipal offices, library annex). Retail follows rooftops and routines, never the reverse. Festivals fail because they import a one-day presence spike with no stock behind it; residents are permanent inflow to the presence stock. Like a mall's anchor tenant generating ambient traffic that small shops feed on, downtown's anchor is people who must be there daily. *Falsifier:* if 200 new downtown residents over two years do not lift evening foot traffic, the third-place thesis is wrong.

**C. Run a portfolio of cheap bets, not one big swing.** Stand up a small revolving fund and a fast-track desk that places ~20 micro-experiments a year (each under a few thousand dollars, each reversible): tactical street closures, parklets, a night market, free short-term maker leases, lighting and seating. Prior fixes were large, slow, single-shot, and unfalsifiable. A portfolio is antifragile: most bets die cheap, a few compound, each teaches something. *Falsifier:* track presence-hours per block; if no experiment moves the metric after 20 tries, the district has a fundamental constraint (no nearby population, severed transit) that placemaking cannot fix.

**Verdict.** The obvious fixes failed because they share one false premise: refill the retail box from the demand side. Reframe the goal from retail sales to hours of human presence, then push the strong cheap levers (rules and residents) over the weak ones (subsidies, one-day events). Run all three together on two pilot blocks: A makes experiments legal, B builds the permanent presence stock, C generates and grades the experiments. Measure presence-hours and kill what does not move the metric within a year. If presence rises but vacancy still does not, the city has a deeper structural problem (no nearby population, dead transit, regional decline) that downtown tactics cannot solve, and learning that cheaply is itself worth the price.

Most load-bearing uncertainty: whether the binding constraint is regulatory rigidity (fixable cheaply) or genuine absence of nearby population and transit (not fixable downtown). The pilot is designed to separate the two within a year.
