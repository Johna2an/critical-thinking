Assumptions: I interpret “Chicago” as the city proper, and “work as piano tuners” as people doing paid piano tuning/technical work, including self-employed technicians. For the CEO question, I use undergraduate field only, not MBAs.

**(a) Piano tuners in Chicago**

Chicago has about 2.73M people and 1.16M households per Census QuickFacts. The U.S. has about 132.7M households, and one music-career source cites roughly 20M pianos nationally, with home pianos needing tuning once or twice a year but many not actually tuned that often. That raw household-share calculation gives:

`20M * 1.16M / 132.7M ≈ 175,000 pianos`

That is too high for regularly serviced pianos. Chicago has many apartments, renters, and old unused uprights, so I discount total piano ownership to maybe 100k-140k instruments and assume only 35%-50% are active enough to be tuned in a given year. That implies roughly 40k-65k home tunings annually. Add schools, churches, studios, colleges, venues, and concert pianos, which are fewer but tuned more often, and I get maybe 55k-80k tunings/year.

A working tuner might do 2.5-3.5 tunings/day after travel, admin, repairs, cancellations, and part-time schedules. At 220 workdays/year, that is about 550-770 tunings per full-time equivalent. So demand implies around `55k-80k / 650 ≈ 85-125` FTE-like tuners. A BLS sanity check points the same direction: it reported 6,170 U.S. musical instrument repairers/tuners in 2023, excluding self-employed, and 360 in the Chicago metro in 2021; city proper would be a concentrated minority of that metro total.

**Estimate:** about **100 piano tuners in Chicago city proper**, with an **80% confidence range of 60-170**. If “Chicago” means the full metro area, I would raise that to roughly **250-450**.

**(b) Fortune 500 CEO undergraduate engineering/hard-science probability**

The best current-ish data I found is Crist|Kolder’s 2025 Volatility Report, covering Fortune 500 plus S&P 500 companies through December 31, 2025. It reports 672 sitting CEOs, 589 with known educational backgrounds, with **29% engineering** and **7% sciences**, or **36% engineering/science combined**. It separately reports **business or dual business at 36%**, and notes economics is counted under liberal arts; economics appears as the top individual CEO major with 95 cases, or about 16% of known CEO backgrounds.

That mixed Fortune 500/S&P 500 sample is not exactly “current Fortune 500 only,” so I would haircut it slightly. Another Fortune-500-specific analysis found engineering at 19.2% and economics at 11.8%, but it appears less current and less complete for hard sciences. Combining the evidence, I would not use the full 36% mechanically, but I also would not go as low as 20%, because sciences, computer science, mathematics, chemistry/biology, and engineering-heavy sectors lift the STEM bucket.

**Estimate:** a randomly chosen current Fortune 500 CEO has about a **34% probability** of holding an undergraduate degree in engineering or a hard science, with an **80% confidence range of 28%-40%**. Business/economics is probably more common overall, roughly around 50%, so if conditioning only on “STEM vs business/econ,” STEM’s share is closer to **40%**.

**Verdict:** Chicago city proper likely has **around 100 piano tuners**. For Fortune 500 CEOs, the undergraduate engineering/hard-science probability is **about one-third**, not a majority, but high enough to be a major CEO pathway alongside business and economics.

Sources: U.S. Census QuickFacts for Chicago population/households, Census Reporter for U.S. households, Berklee on U.S. pianos/tuning frequency, BLS OEWS for musical-instrument repairers/tuners, and Crist|Kolder 2025 Volatility Report.
