# Reviving a dying downtown: reframings and three options to test

## Starting assumptions

I assume a mid-sized US/Canadian city (population ~100k–400k), a downtown built before 1960, a metro area where most residents already live and shop in car-oriented strip and big-box zones, and an online retail share that has taken the easy commodity transactions away. The "obvious fixes already tried" tell me the real constraint is not surface friction (parking, rent, one-off events). When cheap supply-side tweaks fail repeatedly, the problem is usually structural demand, not friction.

## Non-obvious reframings

**1. Downtown is not a retail problem; it's a residency problem.** Foot traffic is a derivative of people who are *already there for another reason*. Healthy downtowns are anchored by people who live, work, learn, or get treated there 16 hours a day. If nobody sleeps downtown, every shop is fighting to import its entire customer base by car against suburban competitors that don't have to. Retail vacancy is the symptom; daytime/nighttime population is the cause.

**2. The unit of competition is the wrong size.** Individual shops compete against Amazon and the mall and lose. But a *bundle* (a reason to spend three hours, not buy one item) is something neither Amazon nor a strip mall can replicate. The city has been subsidizing inputs (rent, parking) instead of curating the bundle.

**3. "Dying" may be a measurement artifact of the old use.** Foot traffic at noon on a weekday measures a 1985 retail downtown. The relevant question is whether the *real estate* is productive, not whether shops are full. A downtown can be economically alive (housing, services, makers) while looking dead by retail-occupancy metrics.

**4. Vacancy is a coordination failure, not a price failure.** Lowering rent didn't work because no single tenant wants to be the first one on a dead block. The binding constraint is sequencing and co-location, which a price signal cannot solve.

## Three unconventional options worth testing

### Option A: Convert, don't lease (upper-floor and ground-floor residential conversion)
Stop trying to fill ground-floor retail with retail. Use a streamlined zoning overlay plus a conversion fund to turn vacant retail and dead office floors into apartments, with ground-floor live/work units. Reasoning: this attacks reframing #1 directly. Even 300–500 new downtown residents create permanent, weatherproof, car-free demand that no festival matches. Conversions are cheaper than new builds and reuse existing stock. Test cheaply: pre-approve 3–5 pilot buildings, measure lease-up speed and the change in evening foot traffic on those blocks over 6–12 months.

### Option B: Curated cluster, not scattered subsidy ("anchor a single block")
Concentrate every incentive on *one* block to reach a tipping density: recruit a complementary set of tenants (a daytime anchor like a community college satellite or clinic, plus food, plus a maker/repair space) to open in the same quarter, coordinated by a single curator with a vacancy backstop. Reasoning: this attacks #2 and #4. One vibrant block beats ten half-dead ones; success is visible and copyable. Test: pick the block with best bones, fund 90 days of coordinated opening, measure dwell time and tenant survival vs. a control block.

### Option C: Change the building's job, not its hours (programmable "third place" anchor)
Lease a large vacant anchor as a publicly-subsidized civic/commercial hybrid: library-plus-maker-plus-coworking-plus-childcare, open 7am–10pm. Reasoning: foot traffic needs a *generator* that runs on non-discretionary visits (work, childcare, learning), which then spill into surrounding shops. This converts downtown from a destination you must choose into a place you already have to be. Test: a 12-month pop-up lease in one anchor, measuring adjacent retail sales and visit frequency.

## Verdict

The strongest single bet is **Option A (residential conversion)**, because it changes the demand base permanently rather than renting attention for a weekend. Run it together with **Option B** as a fast, cheap, visible proof point on one block. Treat **Option C** as the demand generator that makes both work. What's uncertain: conversion economics depend heavily on local construction costs and financing, and a single anchor closing can sink Option C. Recommendation: pilot A and B in parallel on adjacent blocks, instrument with foot-traffic and lease-up data, and scale only what the numbers confirm.
