# Reviving a dying downtown: reframings and three options worth testing

## Framing the real question

The brief hands me a conclusion already wired in: "downtown is dying, the fixes failed, find new fixes." Before I generate, I should check what easier question my mind wants to answer. The easy substitution is "what marketing or amenity could pull shoppers back?" That is the same class of move that already failed (parking, rents, festival). The harder and truer question: **what is downtown a stock of, what are its inflows and outflows, and which feedback loop is now dominant?**

Note the hidden premise: that downtown should be a retail-and-shopping district. That premise is doing most of the damage. The three failed fixes all assume the goal is "more shoppers buying things in physical stores," and they fight a structural shift (e-commerce, remote work, big-box and online price competition) with parameter tweaks. On Meadows' leverage hierarchy, parking and rent and a festival are all leverage point 12 (numbers) and 11 (buffers): the weakest places to push. The failure was predictable, not surprising.

## Non-obvious reframings

**1. Downtown is not a shopping problem, it is a residency and dwell-time problem.** Retail is a flow that follows a stock: the number of people who have a daily reason to be downtown at varied hours. A district populated only at lunch on weekdays cannot support shops no matter the rent. The reinforcing loop ran in reverse: fewer residents and workers, fewer customers, more closures, fewer reasons to come, fewer residents. Shops are the symptom; the eroded stock is "reasons to be physically present."

**2. The vacancy is a self-reinforcing signal, not just lost income (the "broken windows" loop).** Empty storefronts tell every potential entrant and visitor "this place is failing," which suppresses entry, which produces more vacancy. The relevant variable is the *visible occupancy rate*, and it has a tipping threshold. Below it the loop runs down; above it, momentum carries.

**3. "Success to the successful" has flipped against downtown.** Capital, foot traffic, and attention flow to whatever is already winning (the suburban mall, Amazon, the new lifestyle center). Downtown lost the resources to compete, so it lost more. The exit Meadows names is to stop competing on the winner's terms and change the game.

**4. Commercial rent is a stock-limited resource priced for a market that left.** Landlords hold out for old rents because lowering them locks in a lower asset valuation on the books. Vacant-but-overpriced is privately rational and collectively ruinous: bounded rationality producing a bad equilibrium. The lever is the landlord's incentive, not the tenant's.

## Three unconventional options worth testing

**Option A: Convert the obsolete stock instead of refilling it (zoning and adaptive reuse).**
Aim at leverage point 5 (rules). Rezone ground-floor commercial to allow residential, clinics, daycare, trade schools, maker space, and offices, and offer conversion permits and tax abatement for upper-floor apartments. Reasoning: this rebuilds the *residency* stock (reframe 1) that retail depends on, and it attacks reframe 4 by giving landlords a profitable alternative to holding empty. A resident base seeds a balancing demand loop for the cafes and shops that remain. Test small: a two-block pilot with a 24-month conversion window. Measure resident headcount and evening foot traffic, not store count.

**Option B: Tax the vacancy, not the activity (reverse the incentive).**
A graduated vacant-storefront tax that rises with months empty, paired with a low- or zero-rent fast lease for pop-ups, with revenue earmarked for facade and streetscape. Reasoning: this is a reversal (de Bono) of the usual "subsidize tenants" move and it targets the landlord incentive directly. It restores the missing feedback loop (reframe 2): holding a storefront empty now costs money, so the privately rational choice shifts toward filling it. Risk to red-team: landlords may pass cost to weak tenants or the tax may simply yield revenue while units stay empty, so the pop-up off-ramp must be genuinely frictionless. Test on one corridor with a sunset clause.

**Option C: Make downtown the venue for things that cannot be done online.**
The thing e-commerce and remote work cannot replicate is co-present, recurring, identity-anchored activity: a permanent food hall, a weekly evening market with prepared food and music, a clinic, a library-makerspace, a community college satellite, sports leagues, civic offices. Reasoning: this is the "success to the successful" exit (reframe 3) and Deutsch's reach idea inverted: pick the function that has structural protection from the dominant competitor. Online wins on price and convenience for shippable goods; it loses on presence, service, and belonging. Stop competing on shippable retail.

## Verdict and confidence

The high-confidence claim (roughly 80%) is diagnostic: the three failed fixes failed because they pushed weak leverage points against a structural shift, treating a residency-and-presence problem as a shopping problem. On the remedies I am less certain. **Option A (conversion) is the strongest bet** because it rebuilds the underlying stock and aligns landlord incentives, and adaptive-reuse precedents exist in many mid-sized cities. Option B is higher-variance and depends on execution of the pop-up off-ramp. Option C is durable but slow and needs an anchor institution to commit.

Caveats I cannot resolve from the brief: the city's actual residential demand, the landlord ownership structure (concentrated vs. fragmented changes which lever works), and the fiscal room for abatements. Run all three as bounded, instrumented pilots on separate corridors with a frozen baseline, judge them on the residency and dwell-time metrics rather than on store count, and grade the decisions on what was knowable rather than on whether any single pilot happens to land.
