Assumption: the downtown is not “under-marketed”; it has lost its repeat-use engine. Festivals, cheaper rents, and parking help only if people already have a reason to come often. A shop district usually needs hundreds or thousands of ordinary weekly visits, not three big event days.

**Reframing 1: downtown is not a retail problem, it is a habit problem.**  
Most dying downtowns were built around forced routines: office work, courthouse visits, banking, transit transfers, school pickups, church, errands. Retail was the parasite on those routines, not the source. If remote work, suburban services, online shopping, and safety concerns removed the routine, adding boutiques is like planting flowers in dry soil.

**Reframing 2: the competition is not the mall; it is frictionless life.**  
People compare downtown against Amazon, drive-throughs, home delivery, suburban strip centers, and staying home. “More parking” solves only one friction. The bigger frictions are uncertainty: Will anything be open? Is it safe? Can I bring kids? Is there a restroom? Will I feel awkward if the street is empty?

**Reframing 3: empty storefronts may be an asset, not just a symptom.**  
Vacancy creates a rare chance to test uses that normal retail rents would block: production, care, education, repair, logistics, civic services, short-term residencies, night uses, and weird hybrid formats.

Three unconventional options worth testing:

**1. Convert downtown into an “obligation district,” not an attraction district.**  
Move recurring civic and semi-civic functions into storefronts: municipal permitting, a small health clinic, community college classrooms, childcare, workforce training, library services, tax help, legal aid, school arts programs, probation check-ins, senior services. The goal is not glamour; it is repeat necessity.

Reasoning: one relocated office with 80 workers might generate 200 to 400 daily trips including staff, clients, lunch, deliveries, and errands. Five such anchors could create the base foot traffic that ten boutiques cannot create by themselves. Test it for 12 months with pop-up leases in vacant spaces, then measure pedestrian counts, adjacent sales tax receipts, and vacancy changes within one block.

Uncertainty: this requires institutional coordination and may face resistance from agencies that prefer purpose-built buildings.

**2. Make downtown a live production and repair zone.**  
Instead of asking “what shops should fill the storefronts?”, ask “what activities are interesting enough to watch and useful enough to return to?” Recruit businesses that make, fix, teach, or customize things in public: bike repair, clothing alteration, small-batch food production, electronics repair, tool library, 3D printing, woodworking, instrument repair, ceramics, print shop, urban farm lab.

Reasoning: ordinary retail loses to the internet. Production, repair, classes, and customization are harder to digitize. They also create visible activity even when sales are modest. A dead street looks less dead when people are working behind glass. Test with subsidized 6-month “working storefront” leases where rent relief is tied to open hours, workshops hosted, and visible street-facing production.

Uncertainty: not every maker can run a storefront; the city would need an operator/curator, not just a landlord subsidy.

**3. Create a “downtown membership utility” instead of isolated businesses.**  
Bundle downtown into one practical subscription: secure bike parking, public restrooms, coworking drop-in, package pickup, kids’ activity hours, event access, merchant credits, and evening shuttle or valet. Employers, hospitals, colleges, and apartment buildings could buy memberships in bulk.

Reasoning: people avoid downtown partly because each visit feels uncertain. A membership turns downtown from a set of disconnected shops into a usable service layer. If 1,000 residents or workers paid $20/month, that is $240,000/year to fund shared amenities while steering predictable spend to local merchants. Test with one compact zone, 10 to 20 participating businesses, and a simple app/card.

Uncertainty: execution matters. A weak discount program would fail. It must solve real frictions: bathrooms, safety, storage, convenience, evening mobility.

**Verdict:** stop treating downtown as a shopping district that needs promotion. Treat it as a weakened ecosystem missing recurring obligations, visible activity, and low-friction usefulness. The best first test is the obligation district, because repeat foot traffic is the upstream variable the other fixes depend on.
