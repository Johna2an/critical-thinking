# Critique: "Remote work is destroying our productivity"

The argument moves from a single correlation to a sweeping causal verdict to a costly mandate, and it leaks at every joint. Here is every flaw I can find, grouped by where the reasoning breaks.

## 1. The core causal leap (correlation ≠ causation)
The decisive flaw: 2020 is when the company went remote, but 2020 is also when a global pandemic, demand shocks, supply-chain disruption, inflation, labor-market upheaval, and macro recession all hit at once. Revenue per employee could have fallen for any of these reasons while remote work was neutral or even cushioning. The argument never rules out a single confounder. This is a textbook **post hoc ergo propter hoc** error: temporal coincidence is treated as proof of cause.

## 2. The metric does not measure what's claimed
The title word is "productivity," but the evidence is **revenue per employee**, which is not productivity. Revenue per head moves with pricing, market demand, product mix, churn, sales cycles, and especially **headcount**. If the company *grew headcount* during 2020 to 2025 (hiring ahead of revenue, onboarding lag, acquisitions), revenue per employee falls *by arithmetic* even if every worker produced more. Output per labor-hour, on-time delivery, defect rates, and unit costs are unmeasured. The argument equivocates between two different concepts using one number.

## 3. The "competitors grew" comparison is not a controlled experiment
- **Selection / survivorship bias:** the cited competitors are the ones who "stayed in-office and grew." We hear nothing about in-office firms that shrank or died, or remote firms that thrived. Cherry-picking the favorable cases manufactures the pattern.
- **Not like-for-like:** competitors may differ in sector, size, capital, leadership, product, and pricing power. Their growth may have nothing to do with their office policy.
- **No baseline:** a 12% *decline* is reported against an unstated starting point and unstated time window. Was the trend already falling before 2020? Is 12% over five years even outside normal variance? One data point is not a trend.

## 4. The remedy doesn't follow even if the diagnosis were right
- **Non sequitur on dosage:** even granting that some remote work hurt output, nothing shows the cure is a *full five-day* return. The argument never considers hybrid (2 to 3 days), better async tooling, or targeted fixes. It jumps straight to the maximal intervention.
- **No cost-benefit:** mandating five days back carries real costs the argument ignores entirely: attrition of high performers, narrowed hiring pool, reduced morale, real-estate expense, and the competitive risk that rivals offer flexibility. A "restore productivity" claim that omits the downside of the fix is incomplete.
- **Reversibility assumed:** it presumes that re-imposing the office returns the world to 2019 conditions. The market, workforce expectations, and the company have all changed; you can't reset one variable in isolation.

## 5. Hidden assumptions and rhetorical tells
- **"Clearly"** does heavy unearned work; the conclusion is asserted before it's argued (begging the question).
- **False dichotomy:** the only options presented are "fully remote" vs. "full five-day office," ignoring the entire hybrid middle.
- **Single-cause fallacy:** revenue per employee has many drivers; the argument pins it on one.
- **Regression to the mean and reverse causation** are unaddressed: perhaps the firm went remote *because* it was already struggling, or struggling units were the ones who went remote.

## What would actually be needed
A credible case would: define and measure productivity directly (output per FTE-hour, throughput, quality); decompose the revenue-per-employee change into headcount vs. revenue effects; compare against a matched control set of similar firms across *both* policies; isolate the 2020 macro shock; and run a bounded pilot (e.g., hybrid vs. remote teams) before any company-wide mandate.

## Verdict
The argument is **unsound**. It rests on a single uncontrolled correlation, conflates revenue per employee with productivity, cherry-picks competitors, ignores the pandemic confounder, and leaps to the most expensive remedy without cost-benefit analysis or intermediate options. The evidence presented cannot support a five-day RTO mandate. Before acting, the company should measure productivity directly, control for headcount and macro effects, and pilot a hybrid model. Reject the conclusion as stated; treat the revenue decline as a question to investigate, not a settled case against remote work.
