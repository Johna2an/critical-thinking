# Critique: "Remote work is destroying our productivity, so mandate five-day RTO"

## The argument, mapped

- **Conclusion (prescriptive):** mandate a full five-day return to office.
- **Stated reason 1:** revenue per employee fell 12% since going fully remote in 2020.
- **Stated reason 2:** in-office competitors grew.
- **Bridging claim (descriptive):** remote work caused the productivity drop.
- **Hidden premises:** revenue per employee measures productivity; remote work is the cause of the gap; five days in office is the fix and would reverse it; office presence had no offsetting benefits.

The chain only holds if every hidden premise holds. Each is weak.

## Flaw 1: correlation read as cause (the load-bearing error)

Two things moved together (went remote, revenue per head fell), and the argument treats sequence as proof. A correlation is consistent with five stories: remote caused the drop, the drop caused the remote choice, a third factor caused both, mutual causation, or coincidence. None is ruled out. This is also **post hoc, ergo propter hoc**: "after going remote" is not "because of remote."

## Flaw 2: the 2020 confound is enormous

The switch to remote happened in the exact year of a global pandemic, recession, supply shocks, and demand collapse. 2020 is the single most confounded baseline year available. Revenue per employee could fall from the macro shock, sector headwinds, customer churn, a pricing change, an acquisition that added headcount, or hiring ahead of revenue. The pandemic is a textbook **third factor** that plausibly drove both the remote decision and the revenue change. Attributing the whole 12% to work location requires ruling these out, and nothing here does.

## Flaw 3: the metric does not measure what is claimed (measurement validity)

Revenue per employee is not productivity. It moves with headcount changes (hire 15% more people and the ratio falls even if output rises), pricing, product mix, M&A, and revenue recognition timing. A company that hired aggressively, cut prices to gain share, or expanded into a lower-margin line would show this drop with productivity flat or rising. The argument needs output-per-hour or output-per-cost; it substitutes an accounting ratio. This is a **semi-attached figure**: one number stands in for a different claim it cannot support.

## Flaw 4: the competitor comparison is selected and uncontrolled

"Competitors who stayed in-office grew" invites three checks. First, **the denominator**: out of how many in-office competitors? If we hear only about the ones that grew, that is **selection bias** (cherry-picking survivors). Second, **compared to what among remote firms?** Many fully remote firms grew over the same span; one counterexample breaks the implied rule. Third, the competitors differ on everything (sector exposure, capital, product, geography), so any difference cannot be pinned on office policy. This is correlation across non-comparable units, not a controlled test.

## Flaw 5: false dilemma in the remedy

The frame offers two states: fully remote (bad) or five days in office (good). The middle is erased. Hybrid schedules (2 to 3 days), team-level choice, better async tooling, role-specific policies, and fixing whatever actually caused the revenue drop are all live options. The remedy also leaps from "remote is the problem" to "five days, mandated for everyone" with no argument that the full dose is needed over a partial one. A **search-for-a-perfect-solution** flavor hides here too: even if remote has costs, that alone does not make a blanket mandate the right fix.

## Flaw 6: base rate and reversibility ignored

What is the base rate that an RTO mandate restores a revenue ratio? Mandates carry well-documented costs: attrition (often of the strongest, most mobile staff), rehiring and real-estate expense, and morale hits. The argument counts none of these against the upside, and assumes the 12% is fully recoverable by reversing one variable. If the drop came from macro or headcount, RTO cannot restore it and the costs land anyway.

## Flaw 7: hard-to-vary failure

The explanation is too flexible. If revenue had risen under remote, the same arguer could credit "talent we could only hire remotely." An account that fits either outcome explains neither. A real test would compare productivity before versus after on a stable per-output measure, control for the pandemic and headcount, look across many remote and in-office peers, and check whether teams that returned actually improved.

## What would actually settle it

Output-per-FTE or per-cost (not revenue-per-head), decomposed by what changed since 2019 (not 2020); a like-for-like peer panel; an internal natural experiment (teams or offices that returned versus stayed remote); and an honest tally of mandate costs. Absent that, the diagnosis is unproven and the prescription is premature.

## Verdict

The argument fails on both structure and content. Its core inference is correlation-as-cause built on the most confounded year on record, its key metric does not measure productivity, its competitor evidence is selected and uncontrolled, and its remedy is a false dilemma that ignores hybrid options and the costs of a mandate. **Confidence that the argument as stated justifies a five-day mandate: very low (roughly 5 to 10%).** Recommendation: do not act on this reasoning. Re-measure productivity properly, decompose the revenue change against a 2019 baseline, run or find a controlled remote-versus-office comparison, and only then weigh a policy, with hybrid on the table and mandate costs counted.
