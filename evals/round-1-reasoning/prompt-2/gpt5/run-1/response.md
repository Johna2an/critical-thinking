Assumption: the 12% decline is cumulative since going fully remote in 2020, not a one-year drop. If so, that is roughly a 2% annual decline in revenue per employee. That may be meaningful, but it is not automatically evidence that remote work caused it.

The core flaw is causal overreach. The argument observes: remote work began, then revenue per employee fell. But “after” does not mean “because of.” 2020 also brought a pandemic, supply-chain disruption, inflation, interest-rate changes, labor-market churn, changes in customer demand, and likely internal changes in hiring, pricing, product mix, sales cycles, and management. Any of those could affect revenue per employee.

Second, revenue per employee is an imperfect productivity measure. It mixes employee output with pricing power, market demand, sales effectiveness, headcount strategy, capital intensity, and product margins. A company can become operationally more productive while revenue per employee falls because it hired ahead of growth, entered a lower-price market, lost a major customer, or shifted to longer-term R&D. Conversely, competitors can show higher revenue per employee because they underhired, raised prices, sold different products, or had stronger demand.

Third, the competitor comparison is vague and likely confounded. “Competitors who stayed in-office grew” does not tell us whether they had the same size, market segment, product quality, capital access, geography, customer base, sales motion, or starting revenue per employee. It may also be cherry-picked: did all in-office competitors grow, or only the ones being noticed? What happened to remote competitors? What happened to hybrid competitors? Without a broader benchmark, the comparison is weak.

Fourth, the argument ignores the counterfactual: what would this company’s revenue per employee have been if it had stayed in-office? Maybe it would have fallen 20% instead of 12%. Maybe remote work helped retention, widened hiring pools, reduced facilities costs, or preserved operations during disruption. The argument assumes the observed decline is the worst case caused by remote work, but it does not estimate the alternative.

Fifth, the proposed remedy does not follow from the evidence. Even if remote work contributed to lower productivity, it does not prove that a full five-day office mandate is the optimal fix. The productivity issue could come from weak management practices, poor onboarding, unclear goals, bad tooling, meeting overload, insufficient mentorship, or misaligned incentives. A five-day mandate might help some collaboration-heavy teams, hurt deep-work teams, increase attrition, reduce candidate quality, and raise office costs.

Sixth, it treats “remote work” as one uniform variable. Fully remote in a well-run async organization is different from emergency remote work with poor processes. Team function matters: sales, engineering, customer support, operations, and executive planning may have different optimal arrangements. A blanket mandate ignores heterogeneity.

A better analysis would compare productivity trends before and after 2020 against industry benchmarks, adjust for headcount mix and macro conditions, segment by team and role, and measure concrete outputs: sales productivity, cycle times, customer retention, shipping velocity, support resolution, employee retention, and quality. The company could also run a controlled pilot: some teams return two to three days, some remain remote with improved operating practices, and outcomes are tracked for 3 to 6 months.

Verdict: the argument identifies a real signal worth investigating, but it does not prove remote work is destroying productivity and does not justify a full five-day return mandate. The recommendation should be diagnosis plus targeted experiments, not a company-wide policy based on a single weak correlation.
