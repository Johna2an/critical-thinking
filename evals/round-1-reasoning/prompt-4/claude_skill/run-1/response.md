# Should we take the $400k custom contract?

## Frame the real question

The decision is prescriptive: with 9 months of runway, do I commit all 6 people for 5 months to a $400k single-customer feature that freezes the core product and roadmap?

The easy question my mind wants to swap in is "is $400k a lot of money for a startup that might run out of cash?" Phrased that way the answer is an obvious yes, which is exactly the tell of attribute substitution. The real question is a bet about what state the company is in after the 5 months, and whether that state is better than the state I reach by declining. I am betting attention and momentum, not just choosing whether to bank cash.

## Assumptions, stated up front

- 6 people, all consumed for 5 months. No partial capacity for the core product.
- $400k is roughly 5 months of payments. At a 6-person burn that implies somewhere near $40k to $60k/month all-in, so the contract roughly covers its own 5 months and adds a few months on top. The "extends runway" framing means it converts 9 months of runway into something closer to 11 to 14 months, not a transformation.
- The feature is genuinely single-customer ("only that customer wants"). I treat that as load-bearing and stress-test it below.
- This is a startup with a product and roadmap, so the implicit goal is product-market fit and a growth story, not a services business.

## Base rate first (outside view)

What is this a case of? It is a venture-backed product company being offered custom services revenue that pulls the whole team off the product. The reference class is well known: startups that drift into bespoke enterprise services tend to become de facto consulting shops. The pattern recurs because the cash is real and immediate while the cost (lost roadmap, lost compounding on the product) is deferred and invisible. The base rate on "one big custom contract quietly became our whole business" is uncomfortably high. That outside view argues against, before I look at any specifics.

The competing base rate: design-partner contracts where a real enterprise pays to shape the product are a legitimate and common path to PMF. The difference between the two reference classes is entirely whether the work generalizes. That is the hinge.

## The hinge: does the feature generalize, and is the logo strategic?

The case for taking it gets strong only if one or more of these hold, and I should hunt for the disconfirming version of each:

1. **The feature is 60 to 80% reusable.** If "only that customer wants it now" but the underlying capability serves the roadmap, the $400k is paid R&D. Selective-skeptic test: would I still call it reusable if a skeptic read the spec? If I cannot point to specific roadmap items it advances, it does not generalize.
2. **The logo is a referenceable anchor customer** that unlocks the next ten deals. A marquee enterprise reference can be worth more than the cash. But a logo that signed a one-off custom build is a weaker reference than one that bought the standard product.
3. **The runway extension changes my fundraising position** materially: it buys the months to hit a milestone that makes the next round happen. Here the math bites. 5 frozen months of roadmap to buy ~2 to 5 extra months of runway, with no core-product progress, likely leaves me weaker at raise time, not stronger. Investors fund product momentum, and I will have none to show.

## Premortem

It is 6 months from now and this was a mistake. The most likely story: the build slipped to 7 months, the customer kept changing scope, two engineers are now the permanent maintainers of bespoke code, a competitor shipped the roadmap feature we paused, and we enter fundraising with a flat product and a services-revenue line that investors discount. The runway we bought is gone and we are now dependent on selling the next custom contract.

The failure mode is not the cash. It is the freeze plus the precedent: the team learns that revenue comes from bespoke work, and that is hard to reverse (escalation of commitment to a services model).

## Generate the hidden option C

The binary "take all of it" versus "decline" is a false dilemma. Real alternatives:

- **Scope down.** Negotiate a smaller paid pilot (1 to 2 engineers, 6 to 8 weeks) that delivers the highest-value slice. Preserves most of the team on the roadmap, tests the relationship, keeps optionality.
- **Take it only if the spec is rewritten toward the roadmap.** Make reusability a contract condition, not a hope.
- **Raise the price for full team exclusivity.** $400k to freeze 6 people for 5 months is cheap if it truly costs me the roadmap. If they will not pay a premium that reflects that opportunity cost, the contract is underpriced for what it actually consumes.
- **Decline and use the 9 months on the product**, if the roadmap is the faster path to a fundable milestone.

## Verdict

Default to **no on the deal as offered**: 6 people for 5 months on a non-generalizing single-customer feature is a poor bet, because the opportunity cost (a frozen roadmap going into a fundraise) likely exceeds the modest runway gain, and it risks an irreversible drift into services. I hold this at roughly **65%** confidence; it is genuinely contingent on facts I would verify first.

**Flip to yes only if** the work is concretely 60%+ reusable for the roadmap, OR the logo is a referenceable anchor that opens a clear pipeline, OR the price is renegotiated upward to reflect full-team exclusivity. The strongest move is almost always **option C: a scoped-down paid pilot** that captures the cash and the relationship without freezing the company. Take the meeting, not the whole contract.

Uncertain and worth pinning down before deciding: the exact burn-to-contract ratio (how many months it really buys), how much of the spec maps to the roadmap, and whether this customer is a credible reference. Those three numbers decide it.
