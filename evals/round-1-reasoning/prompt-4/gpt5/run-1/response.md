Assumptions: the $400k is net cash collected during the 5 months, no major hiring is needed, and your current burn is typical for a 6-person startup: roughly $60k-$120k/month depending on salaries and overhead. That means $400k buys about 3.3 to 6.7 months of extra runway.

The key comparison is not “$400k or no $400k.” It is “does buying 3-7 months justify losing 5 months of full-team product progress?”

If you decline, after 5 months you have about 4 months of runway left, but your core product has advanced. If you accept, after 5 months you may have roughly 7-11 months of runway left, but the core product is mostly where it is today, possibly with added custom code and support obligations. So financially, the contract improves survival odds. Strategically, it may reduce company value if it turns the startup into a services shop.

The strongest reason to take it would be if the contract creates leverage: a credible enterprise reference, proof that a painful market exists, a repeatable wedge into similar customers, or a feature that can be generalized into the roadmap. But the prompt says the feature is custom and only that customer wants it. That is a major red flag. A 6-person company spending 30 person-months on non-repeatable work is expensive, even if cash comes in. It also risks demoralizing the team, delaying learning from the broader market, and creating a large customer who now expects custom support.

I would not take it as offered.

I would try to restructure it. The minimum acceptable version should include most of these terms:

1. Upfront or milestone-heavy payment, not net-60 after delivery.
2. Startup retains IP and can reuse/generalize the work.
3. Scope limited so only 1-2 people are consumed, not the whole team.
4. Feature must map to the core roadmap or become configurable infrastructure.
5. Customer pays a premium for roadmap disruption, likely more than $400k if they truly require the whole company for 5 months.
6. Clear support limits after delivery.
7. A path to a larger annual contract or referenceable case study.

If they refuse and require the full team for a one-off build, I would decline unless the company is otherwise nearly dead or has no credible path to financing, revenue, or product traction within the current 9 months. In that emergency case, take it only as a bridge and be explicit internally that you are buying time, not validating the business.

Verdict: decline the contract as proposed. Counter with a narrower, roadmap-aligned, prepaid version. Take it only if it becomes strategically reusable or if survival risk is so high that the alternative is likely failure.
