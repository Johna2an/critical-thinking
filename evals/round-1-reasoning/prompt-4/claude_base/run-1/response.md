# Should you take the $400k custom-build contract?

## Framing the decision

The contract trades $400k and roughly 4-5 months of added runway against 5 months of frozen product development for the entire team. The instinct to grab cash when you have 9 months of runway is strong, but the real cost here is not the dollars, it is the opportunity cost of your only six builders spending the bulk of a year on work that does not compound into your product.

**Assumptions I am making:**
- "Consumes the entire team for 5 months" means near-zero roadmap progress during that window.
- The $400k is gross revenue, not margin. With a 6-person team, monthly burn is plausibly $80k-120k (salaries, benefits, overhead). Call it $100k/month, so $900k total runway at 9 months.
- $400k therefore extends runway by roughly 4 months (from 9 to ~13), assuming the contract covers its own delivery cost and adds the surplus.
- The custom feature has little or no reuse value for other customers ("only that customer wants").

## The case against taking it

1. **You burn your scarcest resource on non-compounding work.** A startup's product and the learning that comes from shipping it are the assets that create enterprise value. Five months of frozen roadmap means competitors move, your market window narrows, and you arrive at month 13 with a stale product and the same core questions unanswered.

2. **It can quietly convert you into a dev shop.** The most common failure mode here is path dependency: the enterprise asks for "just one more thing," the revenue feels safe, and 18 months later you are a services business with one client and no product. Custom-build contracts are how product companies sleepwalk into consulting.

3. **Single-customer concentration risk.** One client at 100% of attention is a hostage situation. If they slip payment, change scope, or churn, you have no diversification and a cold product.

4. **The runway math is thinner than it looks.** Trading 5 months of product work for ~4 months of runway is close to break-even on time, and you come out the other side without product progress. You have bought survival, not momentum.

## The case for taking it

1. **9 months of runway is genuinely dangerous.** If you have no near-term fundraise and no other revenue, refusing $400k can be fatal. Dead startups ship no roadmap at all.
2. **Enterprise validation and a logo** can de-risk future fundraising and sales, even if the feature itself is bespoke.
3. **Possible roadmap overlap.** If even 30-40% of the custom feature generalizes into the core product, the opportunity cost shrinks materially.

## What changes the answer

- **Fundraising state:** If a round is close or your metrics are strong, decline and protect the roadmap. If fundraising is dead and this is survival, the math tips toward taking it.
- **Reusability:** Push hard to reframe the build as a configurable capability that lands on your roadmap, not a one-off. That single change can flip a bad deal into a good one.
- **Team allocation:** "Entire team for 5 months" is the worst version. Can 3 people deliver in 6-7 months while 3 hold the roadmap? Negotiate scope, timeline, and milestone payments so you are not fully frozen.

## Verdict

Default answer: **do not take it as offered.** A full team freeze for a non-reusable build is a strategic mistake at 9 months of runway unless you are out of fundraising options. But rarely accept the framing as binding.

**Recommended move:** Counter-propose. Ask for (a) milestone-based payments to improve cash timing, (b) a partial team allocation that keeps 2-3 people on the core roadmap, and (c) scope that lets you ship the feature into your product (reusable, not bespoke), ideally with a design-partner discount in exchange. If they agree, the deal becomes runway plus a reference customer plus roadmap progress, which is worth doing. If they insist on the full-freeze, single-purpose version, decline unless this is the only thing standing between you and insolvency. Cash that costs you your product is the most expensive cash there is.
