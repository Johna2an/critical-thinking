I would believe the study shows a real association, but I would not believe it proves coffee reduces depression, and I would not treat it as evidence for a coffee-extract supplement.

Assumptions: this is an observational study, not randomized; “20% lower rate” means relative reduction; and depression was measured either by diagnosis or symptom scale. With `n=8,000` and `p<0.01`, the association is unlikely to be pure sampling noise. But statistical significance is not causal evidence. If the baseline depression rate among non-coffee-drinkers were 10%, a 20% relative reduction means 8% among two-cup drinkers, an absolute difference of 2 percentage points. If baseline risk were 5%, the absolute difference is only 1 point. That may matter, but it is not a dramatic effect.

The biggest problem is confounding. Coffee drinkers may differ from non-drinkers in work status, morning routine, income, social habits, exercise, sleep schedule, smoking, alcohol use, diet, chronic illness, pregnancy, medication use, or anxiety sensitivity. Some non-drinkers avoid coffee because caffeine worsens anxiety, insomnia, reflux, palpitations, or other conditions. Those factors can also correlate with depression risk. Even good adjustment cannot fully remove unmeasured or poorly measured confounding.

Reverse causation is also plausible. People with emerging depression may sleep poorly, lose routines, change appetite, avoid stimulants, or stop social coffee habits. In that case, lower coffee intake could be a consequence or early marker of depression rather than its cause. Prospective studies help, but they still do not solve all selection and behavior-change problems.

The broader evidence is suggestive but not decisive. A 2023 systematic review/meta-analysis of observational studies found lower depressive-symptom risk with higher coffee intake in cohort studies, but rated the coffee evidence as low quality and explicitly said more longitudinal work is needed to substantiate causality. It estimated about a 4% lower risk per additional 240 ml/day of coffee, smaller than the wellness brand’s highlighted 20% comparison. Earlier meta-analyses also report inverse associations, so the finding is not isolated, but the evidence base remains mostly observational. Caffeine has biologically plausible mood effects, but it can also cause anxiety, restlessness, insomnia, and irritability in some people, and sleep disruption can worsen mood. The same exposure can help one person and hurt another.

The supplement claim is the weakest part. Evidence about drinking coffee does not automatically transfer to a coffee-extract pill. Coffee is a beverage with caffeine, polyphenols, dose patterns, taste, social context, and replacement effects. A supplement may differ in caffeine dose, extract composition, absorption, contaminants, and use context. FDA guidance also draws a sharp line: supplements marketed to treat, prevent, or cure depression are effectively making drug-like disease claims, and FDA has warned companies against depression-treatment claims for supplements.

Verdict: coffee may be modestly protective, but the claim “coffee reduces depression” is not proven. I would treat two cups/day as a reasonable habit for adults who already tolerate coffee, not as a depression-prevention strategy and definitely not as a basis to buy a coffee-extract supplement. For depression symptoms, rely on evidence-based care, not a wellness-brand extrapolation from observational nutrition data.
