# Reframing what a library is for

## Frame the question and catch the swap

The question asks me to invent new reasons a library should exist, then test directions that follow. The easy substitute question is "how do we make people visit the building more?" That swap is exactly what produced the failed moves. More computers, a cafe, longer hours are all parameter tweaks (Meadows' weakest leverage level, number 12): they treat the building's hourly footfall as the stock to refill and assume the purpose is fixed. Foot traffic is a symptom, not the goal. I will work the goal and paradigm levels instead.

One assumption worth challenging out loud: a declining building visit count may not be a problem at all if the institution's real purpose is being served through other channels. The library that lends ten thousand ebooks a month with an empty reading room may be winning, not losing. So before reframing, separate two stocks: people-in-building (collapsing, and the thing budget-cutters count) and value-delivered-to-the-public (unknown, possibly rising). The danger is that funding tracks the first while the mission lives in the second.

## What a library actually accumulates

A library is not a book warehouse and not a study hall. Strip it to fundamentals (Hamming) and three durable functions remain that almost nothing else in a town provides:

1. A trusted, non-commercial space where being present costs nothing and no purchase is expected. This is rare and getting rarer.
2. A neutral institution with public legitimacy, paid staff, and a roof, embedded in every neighborhood.
3. Curation and navigation of information by people whose incentive is the patron's interest, not ad revenue or engagement metrics.

Each reframing below pushes on a goal or a paradigm rather than a parameter, and each names what it would do to the second stock (value delivered), since that is the one the obvious moves never touched.

## Four directions worth testing

**1. The library as the town's trust infrastructure.** Reframe the core asset from "books" to "a neutral verifier." In an environment where information is cheap and trust is scarce, the library's rarest property is institutional credibility with no commercial agenda. Test: a staffed "is this true?" desk and service, helping residents check a medical claim, a scam, a contract, a piece of local news, or a government form, with librarians trained in source evaluation. Reasoning: this leans on function 3 and treats the manufacture-of-doubt environment as the demand. Risk to watch: scope creep into legal or medical advice the staff cannot give. Cheap to pilot; reversible; measure questions answered and repeat use, not footfall.

**2. The library as the default civic venue (reversal).** Flip the model: instead of pulling people in to consume the collection, push the institution out as the host of things people already need to do together. Local government meetings, mediation, vaccination, tax help, repair workshops, citizenship classes, small-claims clinics. The collection becomes backdrop; the room and the staff are the product. Reasoning: this monetizes functions 1 and 2 (neutral space, public legitimacy) and aims at leverage point 5, rules, by making the library the designated site for civic functions. It also directly attacks the budget threat: an institution other agencies depend on is far harder to cut than one measured by book circulation.

**3. The library as a lending system for everything scarce, not just media.** Challenge the assumption that the thing lent must be information. The deeper competence is operating a trusted, fine-free, equitable lending system at neighborhood scale, which is genuinely hard and which the library already runs. Test a "library of things": tools, instruments, scientific kits, mobility aids, internet hotspots, even time on expensive equipment. Reasoning: this targets a real stock the town lacks (access to costly items used rarely) rather than competing with a phone for attention. Quantify before scaling: a tool people borrow twice a year is a strong candidate; a tool borrowed daily should be bought, not lent. Watch the order-of-magnitude point, lending physical things has very different storage, repair, and liability costs than lending books.

**4. The library as a custodian of local memory and local data.** Reframe from consuming the world's published record to producing and holding the town's own. Oral-history capture, a local-business and local-news archive, a community data commons (who lives here, what they need) that residents own rather than a platform. Reasoning: no commercial actor will preserve a small town's record, so this is a near-monopoly function with no competitor, and it converts the library into something whose absence would be permanent and irreplaceable. This raises the value-delivered stock in a way no rival can replicate, which is the strongest defense against being cut.

## How to test without fooling yourself

Run each as a small, reversible bet, not a renovation. Define the success metric before launching and make it value-delivered (questions resolved, things borrowed, civic functions hosted, records preserved), never the footfall number that already misled everyone. Freeze a baseline so improvement is not misattributed. Run a premortem on each: the most likely failure for all four is that the new function quietly reverts to "another reason to come read," at which point you are back to parameter-tweaking.

## Verdict

The collapse in visits is a signal that the institution's stated goal (be a place people enter to access media) has been eaten by phones, and chasing footfall fights a reinforcing loop you cannot win. The leverage is one level up: change the goal. Of the four, direction 2 (default civic venue) and direction 4 (custodian of local memory) are the strongest, because both make the library something other institutions and residents structurally depend on, which is what actually protects a budget line. Directions 1 and 3 are cheaper, faster pilots and good early experiments. Start with one civic-venue partnership and one library-of-things shelf this quarter, measure value delivered rather than people counted, and let the building's traffic be an output you no longer optimize directly.
