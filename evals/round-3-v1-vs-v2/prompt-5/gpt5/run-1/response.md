Assumptions: the library’s decline is not mainly about awareness, hours, or amenities; it is about the public no longer having a strong enough “job to be done” that requires visiting. The goal should not be generic foot traffic, but politically legible usage: visits that residents, funders, and city officials can see as solving hard local problems.

**Reframing:** a library is not primarily a place for books, computers, or quiet study. It is one of the few trusted, non-commercial, low-friction public institutions left. Its advantage is not content access; the internet won that. Its advantage is neutral space, legitimacy, local trust, and the ability to convene people who would not otherwise coordinate.

Four directions worth testing:

1. **Library As Life-Admin Clinic**

Most people do not lack information; they are stuck in forms, benefits, permits, healthcare portals, school systems, immigration paperwork, tax notices, unemployment claims, housing applications, and job processes. The library could become a recurring “public paperwork clinic” with trained navigators and rotating partners.

Why it is non-obvious: it treats the library less like a knowledge warehouse and more like civic customer support.

Test: run two half-days per week for eight weeks. Track appointments, walk-ins, completed applications, resolved cases, and repeat visits. If 50-100 residents use it meaningfully per month, that is more politically defensible than casual browsing.

Uncertainty: this requires boundaries. Staff cannot become unpaid social workers or legal advisers without training and partnerships.

2. **Library As Local Production Studio**

Instead of offering more consumption tools, offer production capacity residents cannot easily access: podcast booths, product-photo setups for small sellers, resume video recording, digitization, zine printing, oral-history capture, basic legal/financial document templates, and public presentation rooms.

Why it could work: many people now need to produce media, credentials, applications, and small-business assets. A library could become the place where ordinary people make themselves legible to employers, customers, schools, and government.

Test: create a bookable “production hour” model with simple packages: job-seeker kit, small-business kit, family archive kit, student project kit. Measure bookings, completed artifacts, and user outcomes.

Uncertainty: makerspaces often fail when they become equipment museums. The key is packaged workflows, not gadgets.

3. **Library As Civic Matchmaker**

A collapsed-traffic library may still have enormous latent trust. Use that trust to match local surplus with local need: retired professionals tutoring teens, bilingual residents helping newcomers, hobbyists teaching workshops, small employers meeting job seekers, neighbors forming childcare swaps or elder-support circles.

Why it is non-obvious: the asset is not the collection; it is the town’s underused human capital.

Test: pick one narrow matching market first, such as “retired adults tutoring middle-school math” or “local employers meeting entry-level workers monthly.” Measure matches, attendance, retention, and testimonials.

Uncertainty: matchmaking requires active curation. A bulletin board will not do it. Someone must own quality control and follow-up.

4. **Library As Public Memory And Local Intelligence Office**

The library can become the institution that helps the town understand itself: local data dashboards, neighborhood histories, business closures/openings, oral histories, school project archives, “state of the town” briefings, candidate forums, disaster-preparedness maps, and local issue explainers.

Why it could matter: national information is infinite; reliable local information is scarce. A library can own the local layer in a way Facebook groups and city websites do not.

Test: publish one monthly “local intelligence brief” and host one forum around it. Track attendance, newsletter signups, citations by local officials/media, and whether residents return for the next issue.

Uncertainty: neutrality is hard. The library must present evidence, not advocacy.

**Verdict:** do not try to make the library a slightly better cafe, office, or internet terminal. Reposition it as trusted civic infrastructure: a place where residents solve administrative problems, produce useful artifacts, find each other, and understand their town. Test one high-need service first, with outcome metrics strong enough to defend the budget.
