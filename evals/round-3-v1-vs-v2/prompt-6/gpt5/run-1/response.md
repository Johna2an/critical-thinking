Assumption: both camps are looking at real data in good faith, but the policy was not randomly assigned and “worked” means it caused some target outcome to improve relative to what would have happened without it.

The core problem is that the observed decade is not the answer. It is one historical path. The policy question is causal: what was the counterfactual path? If the country adopted a tax cut in 2016 and growth rose afterward, that does not prove the tax cut worked; maybe global demand improved. If growth fell, that does not prove it failed; maybe it prevented an even worse fall. Both camps can use the same data because the dispute is really about the comparison world they are constructing.

With only ten years of aggregate data, the evidence is usually weak. Ten annual observations can be sliced by start date, lag length, inflation adjustment, population group, comparison country, sector, and outcome. A few defensible choices can flip the sign. If each camp has different priors or political commitments, they may gravitate toward specifications that feel “obviously right” to them. That is bias, but not always fraud. Often it is model dependence under uncertainty.

What would make one camp more credible is not whether its story is ideologically attractive, but whether its identification strategy is stronger. The journalist should ask:

1. What exact outcome defines “worked”?
2. Compared with what counterfactual?
3. Why should we believe that counterfactual?
4. Did the result appear after the policy, not before?
5. Does it survive alternative reasonable specifications?
6. Are there placebo tests where no effect should appear?
7. Is there a plausible mechanism and supporting micro evidence?

The best evidence would be randomized or quasi-random variation. For example, if the policy was rolled out to some regions earlier than others for administrative reasons unrelated to local trends, economists could compare treated and not-yet-treated areas, checking whether their pre-policy trends were similar. A clean regression discontinuity could help if eligibility changed sharply at an income threshold or firm-size cutoff. A strong synthetic control could help if one jurisdiction adopted the policy and a weighted mix of similar non-adopters tracked it closely before adoption. Microdata often beats national aggregates because it gives many observations and clearer untreated comparisons.

To settle the dispute, the decisive evidence would show three things: the treated units changed relative to credible controls after the policy; the result is too large and consistent to be explained by normal noise or specification choices; and the proposed mechanism shows up where it should. For example, if a wage subsidy “worked,” we should see eligible firms increasing hiring relative to similar ineligible firms, especially among targeted workers, with timing aligned to the subsidy and no comparable pre-trend.

Sometimes no one can actually know with high confidence. If the policy was adopted everywhere at once, during a crisis, with no clean comparison group, and only ten aggregate observations, the honest answer may be: the data are compatible with several interpretations. In that case, economists can narrow the range of plausible effects, but not settle the matter.

Verdict: the right camp is the one with the more credible counterfactual, not the one with the louder accusation of bias. To ever know, you need ex ante design or credible natural variation, transparent assumptions, robustness checks, and mechanism-level evidence. Without that, the dispute is less about “what the decade showed” and more about which model of the missing counterfactual you are willing to believe.
