# Two camps, one decade, opposite verdicts

## What the journalist actually asked

The question sounds like "who is right," but two different questions hide inside it. The first is descriptive: did the policy change the outcome it targeted? The second is about the meta-fight: why do two competent groups looking at the same data split so cleanly, and can anything resolve it? I will treat the second as the real load-bearing question, because once you see why the split happens, the "who is right" follows.

I am assuming both camps are technically competent and using broadly the same dataset. I am also assuming this is an observational policy, not a randomized experiment, since that is the case that produces durable disputes.

## Why the same decade yields opposite answers

The disagreement almost never lives in the raw numbers. It lives upstream, in choices made before anyone runs a regression. Six forks do most of the work:

1. **The counterfactual.** "Did it work" means "compared to what would have happened otherwise." Nobody observes the otherwise. Each camp builds a comparison world (a control group, a pre-trend extrapolation, a synthetic match) and the verdict is mostly baked into that choice. This is the central denominator question from the statistics kit: compared to what.

2. **The outcome variable.** A policy can lift employment while flattening wages, or cut a deficit while raising borrowing costs. Pick the metric that moved your way and you have your headline. The system delivers exactly the indicator you specify.

3. **The window.** A decade has a start and an end. Move either by two years across a recession boundary and a positive result can flip. Watch for right-censoring and for effects that arrive with a delay.

4. **Controls and specification.** Which confounders to hold fixed is a judgment, and the menu of defensible models is large. The "garden of forking paths" lets an honest analyst reach significance without ever feeling dishonest.

5. **Lag structure and mechanism.** Did the effect need three years to appear? Did something else (a commodity shock, a trading partner's recession) move the same series?

6. **The benchmark for "worked."** Beat zero, beat the prior trend, beat the cost, or beat the next-best policy. These are four different bars.

## The accusation of bias is partly right, and symmetric

Each side accusing the other of bias is usually true of both, which makes it useless as a tiebreaker. The mechanism is motivated reasoning: a researcher who wants the policy to have worked runs the "can I believe it" test on favorable specifications and the "must I believe it" test on hostile ones. The selective-skeptic check exposes this: would each camp accept this same evidence if it pointed the other way. Often neither would. Ad hominem ("they are biased") is also the weakest move available, because a biased analyst can still be correct. The bias charge tells you to distrust the framing, not that the conclusion is false.

There is a deeper layer worth flagging. The two camps may be partly incommensurable: a free-market frame and an interventionist frame disagree about what even counts as the relevant outcome, so "just look at the data" cannot settle it. The data arrive already labeled by one side's categories. That is why the fight feels endless.

## What would actually move the needle

Settling this does not require a new dataset. It requires forcing both camps to expose their hidden premises and run each other's recipe:

- **Pre-register and swap.** Have each camp state, in advance, the outcome, window, controls, and the result that would change its mind. A camp that can name no such result holds an unfalsifiable belief, not a finding. Then run the multiverse: report the result across hundreds of defensible specifications and show where the bulk lands.
- **Hunt a cleaner identification.** A discontinuity at an eligibility cutoff, a staggered rollout across regions, a synthetic-control donor pool. These approximate the missing counterfactual far better than a single time-series fit.
- **Find the disconfirming case.** A region that got the policy late, or a near-identical economy that never adopted it.
- **Demand a mechanism and a placebo.** A real effect should show up where the theory says it must and stay absent where it says it should be.

## Verdict for the journalist

Tell readers three things. First, with one decade of observational data and no clean counterfactual, "who is right" is probably not yet decidable, and reporting it as a coin-flip-resolved fact would be false precision. Second, the honest test is not which camp is louder but which one named in advance what would change its mind and survived its rival's specification. Third, the answer is rarely a clean yes or no. It is conditional: the policy likely helped outcome A under conditions X and did little for outcome B, and the spread of results across reasonable methods is itself the finding. If one camp's verdict collapses the moment you change the window or the comparison group, that fragility is your answer about that camp, regardless of which direction it pointed.
