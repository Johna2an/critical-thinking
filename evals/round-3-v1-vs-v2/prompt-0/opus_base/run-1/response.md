# Should you believe coffee reduces depression? A verdict

**Short answer: No, not on this evidence. The study is consistent with a real protective effect, but it cannot establish one, and the brand's leap from "two cups of coffee" to "buy our coffee-extract supplement" is a further unjustified jump.**

## What the study actually shows

A correlation. Among 8,000 people, two-cup-a-day drinkers had a depression rate 20% lower than non-drinkers, and the result is unlikely to be pure sampling noise (p<0.01). That p-value answers one narrow question: "if there were truly no association, how surprising is this data?" It says nothing about *causation*, effect size in absolute terms, or whether the finding will replicate. A statistically significant correlation in a large observational sample is the *starting* point of an investigation, not its conclusion.

## The core problem: confounding

Coffee drinkers differ from non-drinkers in many ways that also predict depression:

- **Sociability and routine.** Coffee is often consumed at work, with colleagues, in social settings. Employed, socially connected, higher-functioning people both drink more coffee and are at lower depression risk.
- **Health and energy baseline.** People already depressed frequently lose interest in habits, including making coffee, and may avoid stimulants that worsen anxiety or sleep. Coffee consumption can be a *marker* of being well, not a cause of it.
- **Income, age, smoking, exercise, diet.** All correlate with both coffee intake and mood.

Unless the study adjusted carefully for these (and even adjustment is imperfect, because unmeasured confounders remain), the 20% gap could reflect *who chooses to drink coffee* rather than any effect of coffee itself.

## Reverse causation

The arrow may run backwards. Depression reduces energy, appetite, and habitual behavior. Non-drinkers may include a slice of people who *stopped* drinking coffee *because* they became depressed. The study's cross-sectional snapshot cannot distinguish "coffee prevents depression" from "depression suppresses coffee drinking."

## Other red flags

- **A single study.** One n=8,000 observational result is weak evidence on its own. I'd want replication, ideally a dose-response gradient and a prospective cohort that measures coffee *before* depression onset.
- **Effect-size framing.** "20% lower rate" is relative. If baseline depression is, say, 8%, this is a drop to ~6.4%, a 1.6 percentage-point absolute difference. Real but modest, and easily eaten by confounding.
- **Researcher and publication bias.** Was this one of many associations tested? Multiple comparisons inflate false positives even at p<0.01.

## The supplement leap (the weakest link)

Even granting that *brewed coffee* causally protected mood, it would not follow that a *coffee-extract supplement* does. Coffee is a complex mixture: caffeine, chlorogenic acids, the act of a warm ritual, social context. An extract isolates some compounds and discards others, at an unknown dose, in a form never tested in the study. The brand is committing a bait-and-switch: borrowing the credibility of the coffee finding to sell a different product that the data never examined. This is a textbook conflict of interest, and the marketing claim should be treated as advertising, not science.

## What would change my mind

Convergent evidence: prospective cohorts showing coffee intake predicts *later* lower depression after strong confounder control; a plausible biological mechanism; a clear dose-response curve; ideally a randomized trial; and replication across populations. Some real literature does suggest a modest inverse association between coffee/caffeine and depression, but it remains contested and far from "drink this extract to prevent depression."

## Verdict

Do not believe that coffee reduces depression on the strength of this study, and do not buy the supplement on this basis. The finding is a hypothesis worth investigating, contaminated by likely confounding and reverse causation, reported once, and then stretched well past what it supports by a seller with a financial stake. Treat correlation as correlation, and treat the marketing as marketing.
