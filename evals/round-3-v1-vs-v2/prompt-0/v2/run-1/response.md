# Should you believe coffee reduces depression?

## The exact question

Two questions hide inside one. The descriptive question is whether coffee *causes* a reduction in depression. The marketing question is whether you should *buy a coffee-extract supplement* on the strength of this study. The study speaks to neither directly. It establishes a correlation in an observational sample, and the brand then performs two leaps: from correlation to causation, and from coffee-as-consumed to a concentrated extract pill.

## What the study can and cannot show

The design is observational: it measures people who already chose their coffee habit. No one was randomly assigned to drink coffee. That single fact caps how far the result can travel. A correlation between coffee and lower depression is consistent with at least five stories, and only the first is the one the brand wants you to believe:

1. Coffee lowers depression (the claimed direction).
2. **Reverse causation.** Depression lowers coffee intake. Depressed people lose energy, pleasure, and routine; skipping the morning coffee is a symptom, not a cause. This is the strongest rival here, because the mechanism is obvious and well documented.
3. **A common cause.** Employment, social contact, regular sleep, and physical activity all track with coffee drinking (you buy coffee at work, with friends, on a schedule) and all independently protect against depression. The coffee may be a marker of a structured, social life rather than an active agent.
4. Mutual causation, a feedback loop between mood and habit.
5. Coincidence, less likely at n=8,000 but never zero.

Before accepting story 1, the rivals must be ruled out. The headline does not say they were.

## Reading the numbers

**"20% lower rate."** Lower relative to what base rate? A 20% reduction on a 10% baseline is a move from 10% to 8%, two percentage points. On a 4% baseline it is 4% to 3.2%. The relative figure sounds larger than the absolute change almost always does. Ask for the absolute rates and the comparison group before being impressed.

**p<0.01.** With n=8,000 this carries little weight. A large sample manufactures statistical significance from trivial effects. The p-value answers "could this be chance?" and says nothing about "is the effect big enough to matter?" or, more importantly, "is it causal?" Significance and importance are different axes, and significance and causation are different axes again. A confounded effect can be highly significant and still be entirely an artifact of confounding.

**The likely missing adjustments.** Did the analysis control for income, employment, sleep, exercise, smoking, alcohol, and baseline health? Observational nutrition studies live or die on confounder control, and even heavy adjustment cannot remove confounders nobody measured. A wellness-brand press release will quote the rawest, largest-sounding number it can defend.

## The source and the leap to the product

Apply the authority test to who is doing the citing. The brand has a direct stake in the answer, which fails the impartiality filter. Note the semi-attached figure: even if drinking two cups of brewed coffee correlated with lower depression, the product is a *coffee extract supplement*. The study measured a beverage with caffeine, dose, ritual, warmth, and social context bundled together. A pill isolates one fraction of that and discards the rest. The evidence about coffee, whatever its strength, does not transfer to the extract. That is a swap of subject.

There is also prescriptive overreach. The study, at best, is descriptive ("coffee drinkers have lower rates"). The brand converts it into a prescription ("take this to lower yours"). A correlational finding cannot license a "do this" instruction.

## What would change the verdict

Falsifiability check: the claim becomes credible if a randomized controlled trial assigns coffee or caffeine versus placebo and shows a clinically meaningful drop in depression, with the effect surviving for the actual extract product and at the actual dose sold. A meta-analysis of several such trials pointing the same way would move me substantially. Absent that, the observational correlation stays a hypothesis.

## Verdict

No. Do not believe coffee reduces depression on this evidence, and do not buy the supplement because of it. The study shows an association, not a cause, and reverse causation (depressed people drink less coffee) plus shared lifestyle confounders are at least as plausible as the brand's story. The large sample makes the p-value nearly uninformative about effect size or causation. The product leap, from a measured beverage to an unmeasured extract pill sold by an interested party, breaks the chain a second time. Treat this as a reasonable hypothesis worth a randomized trial, and treat the marketing as a motivated reading of a weak design.
