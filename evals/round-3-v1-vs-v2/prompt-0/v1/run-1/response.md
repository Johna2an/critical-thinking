# Does coffee reduce depression? A verdict on the study

## The exact question

Two different questions hide inside the request, and the brand is counting on you to let one stand in for the other.

1. **Descriptive:** Does drinking coffee *cause* a lower rate of depression?
2. **Prescriptive:** Should you buy a coffee-extract supplement to prevent or treat depression?

The study speaks, weakly, to a narrower version of question 1. It says nothing about either the causal claim or the purchase. The brand swapped the easy emotional question ("coffee feels healthy and I like coffee") for the hard one ("does this molecule lower depression risk in people like me, enough to act on it"). Hold both apart.

## What the study can and cannot establish

The design is observational. People chose their own coffee intake; nobody was randomly assigned. That single fact governs everything that follows, because a correlation between coffee and lower depression is consistent with at least five stories, and the headline picks the one that sells supplements.

- **Coffee lowers depression** (the claimed direction).
- **Reverse causation.** Depression blunts energy, motivation, taste, and social routine. Depressed people drink less coffee *because* they are depressed. The arrow runs from mood to cup, not cup to mood. This is the single most damaging rival here, and it predicts the exact 20% gap with no benefit from coffee at all.
- **A common cause (confounding).** Coffee drinking tracks employment, regular morning structure, social contact (coffee is a shared ritual), income, and general health. Each of these independently lowers depression risk. A two-cup-a-day habit is a marker of a structured social life more than a pharmacological dose.
- **Reverse-confounding via health.** People with anxiety, poor sleep, or some chronic illnesses cut caffeine on medical advice, and those same conditions raise depression risk.
- **Coincidence / multiple comparisons.** If the dataset tested many beverages, doses, and outcomes, one significant 20% gap appears by chance.

Before accepting "coffee reduces depression," each rival has to be ruled out. The abstract as described rules out none of them.

## Reading the numbers

- **"20% lower rate"** is a relative reduction, and relative numbers float free of the base. If non-drinkers have a 5% depression rate, a 20% relative cut moves it to 4%: one percentage point. Demand the absolute rates and the comparison group ("compared to what?", "out of what?").
- **p < 0.01 with n = 8,000.** Significance answers "could this be chance?", not "is it big enough to matter?" A sample of 8,000 will stamp "significant" on a trivial or wholly confounded difference. The p-value here is the least interesting number in the sentence. Effect size and adjustment for confounders are what matter, and neither is shown.
- **Self-selected exposure.** Coffee intake was self-reported and self-chosen, so the two groups differ in dozens of ways before coffee is even considered.
- **What is omitted:** absolute rates, how depression was measured (clinical diagnosis vs. a questionnaire), what was adjusted for, whether a dose-response curve exists, and whether this replicates. Omitted information you cannot recover is itself a reason to lower confidence.

## The supplement leap (a separate, larger failure)

Even granting, for argument, that *brewed coffee* causes some benefit, the supplement claim is a semi-attached figure. Coffee is hundreds of compounds (caffeine, chlorogenic acids, trigonelline) plus a morning ritual and social context. A "coffee-extract" capsule isolates an unknown fraction of that and discards the behavior. Proving something about coffee-drinkers proves nothing about a pill. The brand also has a direct stake in the answer, which fails the impartiality test for any source.

## What would change my mind

A large randomized trial that assigned caffeine or a coffee compound vs. placebo and still showed a depression benefit; or strong prospective cohorts that adjusted for baseline mood, sleep, income, and social activity and found a dose-response relationship that held up. Mendelian-randomization evidence would help. None of that is the study on the table.

## Verdict

No, you should not believe that coffee reduces depression on this evidence, and you certainly should not buy the supplement. My confidence that this single observational study demonstrates a real causal effect is low, roughly 10 to 15%. Reverse causation (depression suppresses coffee intake) and lifestyle confounding each explain the entire 20% gap without coffee doing anything. The p-value is inflated by a large sample, the effect is reported in a relative form that hides its true size, and the jump from brewed coffee to an extract capsule is unsupported on its own terms. Treat the citation as marketing built on a correlation, and keep your money.
