Assumptions: the startup sells a reasonably comparable product, customers know both vendors, and the incumbent has enough margin and incentive to respond. Also assume the regression is on observed historical variation in the startup’s own prices, not on randomized price experiments.

I would not “trust the model” as a decision rule. I would treat it as one piece of evidence about baseline demand elasticity under past market conditions.

The first arithmetic check is harsh. A 15% price cut means the startup must increase unit volume by at least `1 / 0.85 - 1 = 17.6%` just to keep revenue flat. To increase gross profit, the hurdle is much higher. If gross margin is 50%, contribution per unit falls from `0.50P` to `0.35P`, so volume must rise about `43%` just to preserve contribution. If gross margin is 30%, volume must double. So a revenue jump may still be a bad business outcome if the model is optimizing revenue instead of contribution, cash payback, retention, or LTV/CAC.

The regression is also likely weakest exactly where the decision matters most. Eighteen months of own sales data can estimate correlations within the startup’s historical operating range. It usually cannot estimate the effect of a conspicuous strategic price cut that changes competitor behavior. If prior price changes were small, temporary, targeted, bundled, or made in different demand environments, the model is extrapolating. It may also suffer from endogeneity: prices may have been cut when demand was already weak, raised when demand was strong, discounted for certain customer types, or paired with promotions and sales effort. Even a statistically clean model may only answer: “When we previously observed lower effective prices, what happened to our revenue?” It does not answer: “What happens after we start a price war with a better-capitalized incumbent?”

The advisor warning about incumbent matching deserves substantial weight. If the incumbent can match quickly, the startup loses the relative-price advantage while resetting the market’s reference price lower. The startup may get a short bump, but the incumbent can neutralize the differentiation and force the startup into lower margins. The larger player may not need to match list price universally; they can selectively discount in contested accounts, bundle, extend contract terms, or pressure channels. That is often worse for the startup because the incumbent can respond surgically while the startup’s public cut affects all revenue.

But “incumbent will simply match” is not certain. They may avoid matching if it cannibalizes a large installed base, violates channel expectations, weakens premium positioning, or creates internal approval friction. The key question is not whether they can match, but whether they will match in the specific segments where the startup competes, how fast, and at what cost to them.

Recommendation: do not make a broad 15% price cut based on this regression. Run controlled, reversible tests instead. Use segmented experiments: limited geographies, customer cohorts, sales-led discount bands, or time-boxed offers. Measure not just revenue, but win rate, volume lift, gross contribution, sales cycle, retention, expansion, and competitor response. Predefine the required lift: for example, if margin is 50%, require roughly 43% more units just to protect gross profit. Watch whether the incumbent matches in contested deals within days or weeks.

Strategically, the startup should prefer targeted pricing over a public price war: discount where elasticity is high, protect price where willingness to pay is strong, and pair price with differentiation, onboarding, service, contract flexibility, or niche positioning. If testing shows the incumbent does not respond and contribution improves, expand carefully. If the incumbent matches fast, stop and compete on wedge, product, service, or segment focus instead.

Verdict: trust the regression as a demand hypothesis, not as a pricing strategy. A broad 15% cut is too risky unless validated by controlled tests that include margin impact and competitor reaction.
