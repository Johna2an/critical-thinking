Assuming **Chicago means city proper**, and counting people who earn meaningful income tuning acoustic pianos, including self-employed technicians who serve Chicago clients.

**(a) Piano tuners in Chicago: estimate 70, with a 90% range of 35-130.**

Chicago has about **2.73 million people** and **1.16 million households** per Census QuickFacts. I’d assume **5-8%** of city households have an acoustic piano, lower than suburban/Midwest averages because Chicago is dense and renter-heavy. That gives roughly **60k-90k household pianos**, plus perhaps **5k-15k institutional pianos** in schools, churches, studios, hotels, universities, and performance spaces.

Not every piano is tuned yearly. Many home pianos are neglected; institutional pianos are tuned more often. A blended average of **0.3-0.5 tunings per piano per year** gives roughly **20k-50k tuning jobs/year**. A full-time tuner might do **500-700 tunings/year** after travel, repairs, admin, and cancellations, implying **35-80 full-time equivalents**. Since this trade has part-timers and self-employed people, headcount should be above FTE.

Supply-side check: BLS-derived May 2025 data lists **150 musical instrument repairers and tuners** in the broader Chicago-Naperville-Elgin metro, not just piano tuners, and BLS OEWS excludes self-employed workers. If 35-45% of metro demand is city proper, 50-70% of the occupation is piano-related, and self-employed workers roughly double the visible wage/salary count, the answer lands around **50-100**. So my best point estimate is **70**.

Sources: Census population/households; BLS-derived occupation data and BLS scope limits. **(b) Probability a current Fortune 500 CEO has an undergraduate degree in engineering or hard science: estimate 30%, with a 90% range of 22-40%.**

I’m interpreting this as an **absolute probability**, not conditional on the CEO having either a STEM or business/economics degree. I count engineering, computer science, math, physical sciences, life sciences, and health sciences as “engineering or hard science”; I exclude economics.

Evidence points to roughly one-third. A 2021 Fortune 500 CEO education study found **engineering alone at 19.2%**, with economics at **11.8%**. Older S&P 500 data put **science and engineering at 28.1%**, and math adds another **3.4%** if classified as hard science rather than liberal arts. A 2019 Fortune 50 study found **18 STEM majors among Fortune 50 CEOs**, or about **36%**, though the top 50 is a small and possibly more technical/sector-skewed sample. ([academicinfluence.com](https://academicinfluence.com/rankings/schools/which-colleges-most-alumni-ceos-fortune-500-companies)) ([economics.appstate.edu](https://economics.appstate.edu/sites/economics.appstate.edu/files/econmajor_and_ceos_study.pdf)) ([sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/org/science/article/pii/S2042389620000700))

The 2026 Fortune 500 is current as of June 2026, and tech/AI/healthcare weight likely nudges STEM upward versus older cohorts, but CEO backgrounds change slowly and retail, finance, insurance, and consumer sectors still supply many business/econ/accounting leaders. ([fortune.com](https://fortune.com/ranking/fortune500/2026/?linkId=954998603))

**Verdict:** about **70 piano tuners in Chicago city proper**; about **30%** of current Fortune 500 CEOs have engineering or hard-science undergraduate degrees.
