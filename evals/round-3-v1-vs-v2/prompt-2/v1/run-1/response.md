# Critique: "Remote work is destroying our productivity"

## The argument, mapped

- **P1.** Since going fully remote in 2020, revenue per employee fell 12%.
- **P2.** Competitors who stayed in-office grew.
- **Hidden premise.** Remote work is the cause of the decline (and the only or dominant cause).
- **Conclusion.** Therefore mandate a full five-day return to office to restore productivity.

This is a prescriptive conclusion ("we must mandate") resting on a descriptive causal claim ("remote work caused the fall"). The whole case stands or falls on that hidden causal premise, so that is where to aim the scrutiny.

## Flaw 1: post hoc, ergo propter hoc

The core inference is "the decline happened after we went remote, so remote caused it." Sequence in time is not causation. 2020 is one of the most confounded years imaginable: a pandemic, a demand shock, supply-chain disruption, hiring freezes or surges, inflation, interest-rate swings, and any company-specific events all hit at once. The argument silently attributes the entire 12% to the one variable it wants to blame.

## Flaw 2: rival causes never ruled out

A correlation between "went remote" and "revenue per employee fell" is consistent with several stories, and none are eliminated:

- A third factor caused both (a macro shock pushed the company remote AND depressed revenue).
- Reverse-tinged timing: the company may have gone remote because it was already under stress.
- Composition change: rapid headcount growth dilutes revenue per employee even when total revenue rises (the metric is a ratio, see Flaw 4).
- Sector mix: the company may sit in a sector that contracted 2020-2024 regardless of work model.
- Coincidence across a single, noisy six-year window.

Until these are listed and tested with discriminating evidence, the causal claim is unearned.

## Flaw 3: the competitor comparison is not a controlled experiment

"Competitors who stayed in-office grew" looks like a control group, but it is selection by outcome. Which competitors? All of them, or the ones that happened to grow? We are not told how many stayed in-office and shrank, or how many went remote and grew. Counting only the in-office winners is cherry-picking the denominator. Firms also differ in product, market, capital, leadership, and timing. Attributing the entire performance gap to office policy is a single-variable explanation for a multi-variable difference.

## Flaw 4: the metric is a black box, and the denominator is unexamined

"Revenue per employee" = revenue / headcount. It falls if revenue drops OR if headcount rises faster than revenue. A company that doubled its workforce to invest for growth would show a falling ratio during its strongest expansion. We never see the numerator and denominator separately, the absolute revenue trend, or whether headcount changed. A 12% move with no base, no time-path, and no decomposition tells us little. There is also no benchmark: 12% over five years compared to what? Inflation alone over 2020-2025 was substantial, so "revenue per employee" in nominal terms may understate real performance.

## Flaw 5: equivocation between revenue and productivity

The argument swaps "revenue per employee" for "productivity" and then for the emotionally loaded "destroying." These are three different things. Productivity is output per input; revenue per employee is a financial ratio driven by pricing, demand, and headcount strategy, not only by how hard or effectively people work. The leap from a financial ratio to "productivity is destroyed" is a semi-attached figure: one thing is measured, a different and scarier thing is asserted.

## Flaw 6: the conclusion does not follow, and ignores the reverse risk

Even granting that remote work hurt revenue per employee, a *full five-day* mandate is one option presented as the only one. The space includes hybrid schedules, role-specific policies, better management of remote teams, or fixing whatever non-remote cause actually drove the decline. This is a false dilemma (remote-vs-five-days-in) layered on a search for a single lever. The mandate also carries unpriced downside risk: attrition of staff hired under remote terms, a smaller hiring pool, real-estate cost, and morale effects. A premortem ("we mandated five days and revenue fell further because our best people left") is never run.

## What would actually settle it

To support the causal claim: decompose the ratio (revenue path vs. headcount path), deflate for inflation, benchmark against the full set of peers (not just survivors), isolate sector and macro effects, and look for within-company variation (did remote teams underperform in-office teams on comparable work?). To support the policy: estimate the expected effect of each option, including the attrition cost of a mandate, and pilot before mandating.

## Verdict

The argument is unsound. It rests on a post-hoc causal leap, ignores every rival cause, uses a cherry-picked and uncontrolled competitor comparison, treats an undecomposed financial ratio as if it were "productivity," and jumps to the most extreme of many available policies. The 12% figure may be real, but as presented it supports no conclusion about cause and certainly none about a five-day mandate. Recommendation: do not mandate on this basis. Decompose the metric, build a fair peer comparison, and pilot any return-to-office change against measured outcomes before committing to an irreversible, costly policy.
