# Critique: "Remote work is destroying our productivity"

## The argument, mapped

- **Premise 1:** Since going fully remote in 2020, revenue per employee fell 12%.
- **Premise 2:** Competitors who stayed in-office grew.
- **Conclusion (descriptive):** Remote work is destroying our productivity.
- **Conclusion (prescriptive):** Therefore mandate a full five-day return to office.

The argument runs backward. It picks the verdict ("remote is destroying us") and the remedy ("five days back") first, then recruits two statistics to dress them. The reasoning needs to survive four separate tests: is the metric measuring what it claims, is the causal story the only one, is the comparison fair, and does the remedy follow even if the cause were real. It fails all four.

## Flaw 1: The metric does not measure productivity

The claim is about productivity; the evidence is revenue per employee. These are different quantities (a semi-attached figure). Revenue per employee moves with pricing, market demand, product mix, competitive dynamics, headcount growth, and macro conditions, none of which is worker output. A company can raise productivity (more output per hour, lower cost per unit) while revenue per head falls because the market softened or because it hired ahead of revenue. Naming the measure is the first discipline: this is a revenue-per-head figure being passed off as a productivity verdict.

## Flaw 2: Post hoc, with the rival causes ignored

"Ever since 2020" is the tell. The period that began in 2020 also contains a global pandemic, supply shocks, an inflation spike, rate hikes, sector-specific demand swings, and possible rapid hiring that dilutes the per-employee denominator. A correlation between "went remote" and "revenue per head fell" is consistent with at least five stories: remote caused the fall; the same macro shock caused both the remote shift and the fall (a confound, the strongest candidate here); the firm hired aggressively and grew the denominator faster than revenue; product or pricing decisions unrelated to location; or coincidence. The argument lists none of these and tests none. A cause asserted without ruling out rivals is not established.

## Flaw 3: The competitor comparison is not controlled, and likely cherry-picked

"Competitors who stayed in-office grew" compares two groups that differ in many ways beyond office policy: which segments they serve, their size, their funding, their products, their starting point. Office policy is one variable among dozens, and the groups were not randomized; they self-selected into remote or in-office for reasons that themselves predict performance. There is also a selection problem: the sentence names the in-office firms that grew. What about in-office firms that shrank, and remote firms that grew? Counting only the favorable cases on each side manufactures the pattern. Without the full 2x2 (remote-grew, remote-shrank, office-grew, office-shrank), the comparison carries no weight.

## Flaw 4: Denominators and base rates are missing

Twelve percent of what, over how long? A 12% fall over six years is roughly 2% a year, which sector-wide may be flat or even above peers. There is no industry base rate ("did revenue per employee fall across the whole sector?"), no absolute numbers, and no read on whether headcount grew (which mechanically lowers the ratio regardless of any worker's output). A single internal number with no comparison year, no denominator, and no peer baseline cannot anchor a causal claim.

## Flaw 5: The remedy does not follow, and ignores its own costs

Even granting, for argument, that remote hurt output, "five days, mandatory, for everyone" is a false-dilemma jump. The realistic option set includes hybrid schedules, team-level choice, targeted in-person time for collaboration-heavy roles, better async tooling, or fixing whatever specific process broke. A full mandate also carries omitted costs the argument never weighs: attrition of staff hired on a remote promise, a shrunken hiring pool, real-estate expense, and morale. The prescriptive leap treats one extreme intervention as the only lever.

## Flaw 6: Hard-to-vary failure and an unfalsifiable frame

The "remote is destroying us" story is too flexible. Had revenue per head risen, the same speaker could credit "lean remote operations." A story that fits either outcome explains neither. The argument also names no observation that would change its mind, which marks it as a conclusion defended rather than a hypothesis tested.

## What would actually settle it

Decompose revenue per employee into revenue and headcount and check which moved. Pull the sector base rate for the same metric over the same window. Build the full competitor 2x2 rather than the two flattering cells. If possible, compare teams or offices that varied in remote intensity for reasons unrelated to performance (a natural experiment). Then weigh hybrid against full-return on expected output, attrition, and cost.

## Verdict

The argument is unsound. It substitutes a revenue ratio for productivity, infers causation from coincident timing while ignoring the obvious macro confound and headcount dilution, leans on a cherry-picked and uncontrolled competitor comparison, and jumps to the most extreme remedy without an option set or a cost ledger. On the evidence given, the case for a full five-day mandate is not made. Treat it as a hypothesis to test with decomposed, base-rate-anchored data, and scope any office policy to the specific problem the data actually reveals.
