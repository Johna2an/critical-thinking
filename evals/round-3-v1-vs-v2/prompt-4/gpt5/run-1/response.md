I would not approve tripling the allocation based on the 19-of-20 track record alone. I would treat that record as a warning sign as much as evidence of skill.

Assumptions: the strategy is systematically short deep out-of-the-money options, earning small recurring premiums and carrying rare but large jump, volatility, and liquidity risk. The desk’s profits likely come from harvesting volatility risk premium, but the payoff profile is negatively skewed: many small wins, occasional severe losses.

The headline evidence is weak for the decision being proposed. Nineteen profitable quarters out of 20 is a 95% hit rate, but 20 quarters is only five years. If the true catastrophic-loss frequency is once every 10 to 25 years, the sample could easily miss the relevant event. Statistically, even with 19 wins, the confidence interval around the true quarterly win probability is wide. More importantly, win probability is the wrong metric. A strategy can win 95% of the time and still be unacceptable if the 5% loss is 20 to 50 quarters of premium.

The central question is not “has it made money?” but “what happens when it loses?” Deep OTM option selling is exposed to gap moves, volatility spikes, correlation breakdowns, and liquidity disappearance. Losses can accelerate nonlinearly as options move toward the money. Tripling the book may also worsen execution, margin needs, and forced-liquidation risk. The risk is not just mark-to-market loss; it is whether the firm can survive the path of losses during stress.

Before approving any increase, I would require:

1. Full tail-loss analysis: historical and hypothetical stress tests including 1987-style equity crash, 2008, Covid March 2020, volatility spike scenarios, overnight gaps, rate shocks, commodity jumps, and multi-asset correlation-to-one cases.

2. Expected shortfall, not just VaR: show 97.5%, 99%, and 99.5% expected shortfall under stressed volatility and liquidity assumptions. VaR alone is especially dangerous for short-option books.

3. Loss-to-premium math: quantify how many quarters or years of average premium would be lost in the 1-in-20, 1-in-50, and 1-in-100 quarter scenarios. If one stress event wipes out most accumulated gains, the allocation should not be tripled.

4. Liquidity and margin analysis: estimate margin calls, collateral usage, bid-ask widening, slippage, and ability to hedge or close positions under stress. Include intraday liquidity, not only end-of-day marks.

5. Independent model validation: confirm pricing, Greeks, scenario generation, volatility surface behavior, and jump assumptions. Short-vol strategies often look safest when models understate jumps.

6. Hard risk limits: maximum loss, maximum vega/gamma exposure, concentration limits by underlying/expiry/strike, stop-loss rules, escalation triggers, and authority to cut risk independent of the desk.

7. Incentive review: ensure compensation does not reward steady carry while leaving the firm with tail losses.

My recommendation: do not approve a threefold increase now. At most, approve a staged increase only if the desk passes independent stress testing, the firm can absorb severe but plausible losses without forced liquidation, and limits are set on tail exposure rather than premium income. The track record supports further investigation, not a blank check.
