# Judgment in Managerial Decision Making, Bazerman & Moore

## Thesis
Smart people make predictable, systematic errors because the fast intuitive mind relies on mental shortcuts that misfire in identifiable situations; once you can name the bias and the trigger, you can deliberately switch to slower analytic reasoning and correct course.

## Thinking tools

1. **The two-system audit (System 1 vs System 2).** System 1 is fast, automatic, emotional, and runs most decisions. System 2 is slow, effortful, and logical. The biases in this book live in System 1. The mechanism: System 1 always answers first, and System 2 often endorses that answer without checking it. To apply: treat your first impression as a draft rather than a verdict. For decisions that are important, reversible at cost, or unfamiliar, deliberately trigger System 2 by writing out the reasoning, computing numbers, or asking a colleague to check. Reserve System 1 for low-stakes, high-frequency choices where the cost of analysis exceeds the value of accuracy.

2. **The six-step rational scaffold.** A normative checklist for any consequential choice: define the problem, identify the criteria, weight the criteria, generate alternatives, rate each alternative on each criterion, then compute the weighted total and pick the highest. The value here is diagnostic: most real errors trace to a skipped or sloppy step (solving the wrong problem, missing criteria, or generating too few alternatives). To apply: when stuck or when a choice feels off, walk the six steps explicitly and find which one you short-circuited. Watch especially for defining the problem in terms of a pet solution or treating symptoms as the problem.

3. **Catch the four heuristics behind most biases.** The book traces nearly every common error to four shortcuts. **Availability**: you judge how likely or frequent something is by how easily examples come to mind, so vivid, recent, and emotionally charged events feel more common than they are. **Representativeness**: you judge category membership by resemblance to a stereotype, ignoring how the math actually works. **Confirmation**: you test a hypothesis by looking for cases that fit it rather than cases that would break it. **Affect**: a gut emotional reaction sets the judgment before reasoning starts. To apply: when you make a probability or frequency claim, ask which of these four shortcuts produced it, then check the claim against actual data or the full set of cases.

4. **Pull the base rate before the story.** Specific, vivid case detail crowds out background frequency. A test that is 86 percent accurate for a condition that occurs once in a thousand still produces mostly false alarms, because the rare true case is swamped by false positives from the huge healthy population. The mechanism is representativeness: the case description "looks like" the target, so you anchor on the story and forget the population. To apply: before trusting a diagnostic signal, ask "out of every thousand cases, how many are real, and how many false alarms does this signal generate?" Build the natural-frequency picture, then update.

5. **Respect sample size (beware the law of small numbers).** Small samples swing wildly; large samples are stable. People expect a small sample to mirror the population as faithfully as a large one, which is statistically false. The small hospital records far more extreme boy-heavy days than the large one. Marketing exploits this with "four out of five dentists" claims that hide the sample size. To apply: always ask how many observations a result rests on, distrust extreme outcomes drawn from tiny samples, and remember that early data (your first few customers, your pilot results) is noisier than it feels.

6. **Predict toward the mean rather than the last data point.** When an outcome mixes skill and luck, extreme results tend to be followed by less extreme ones, because the luck component does not repeat. A career-best season is usually followed by a worse one; a disastrous one by a better one. The trap: people read the rebound as caused by something (praise hurt, punishment helped) when it is statistical regression. To apply: when forecasting a repeat performance, shrink your estimate toward the group average in proportion to how much luck is involved; weight last year's number against the population mean rather than projecting it forward unchanged. This also corrects the false lesson that punishment works better than reward.

7. **See chance for what it is.** Random processes have no memory and do not self-correct. Four girls in a row does not make a boy "due"; a coin owes you nothing. The mind both over-detects order (the basketball "hot hand" is mostly a chance streak in skill-plus-luck data) and expects randomness to look more even than it does. To apply: for any independent event, ignore the run that preceded it. Before claiming a streak, a pattern, or momentum, ask whether the same data could be produced by pure chance, and demand evidence beyond your pattern-detecting intuition.

8. **Run the confirmation counter-search.** To genuinely test a belief, look hard for the evidence that would disconfirm it. Associations between two events require checking all four cells (present-present, present-absent, absent-present, absent-absent), yet people sample only the confirming cell. Once a hypothesis is in mind, people treat it as true until forced otherwise, which feeds anchoring (an irrelevant starting number drags the final estimate toward it) and hindsight bias (once you know the outcome, you believe it was foreseeable). To apply: ask "what evidence would prove me wrong, and have I looked for it?" Before negotiating or estimating, set your own anchor first so someone else's number does not capture you. Record predictions in advance to immunize against hindsight.

9. **Decompose overconfidence into three failures.** Overconfidence is called the mother of all biases because it blocks correction of every other one. It comes in three forms. **Overprecision**: your confidence intervals are too narrow, so you are surprised too often. **Overestimation**: you think you will perform better or finish sooner than you will. **Overplacement**: you rate yourself above others more than the math allows (most drivers cannot be above average). To apply: widen your ranges until you would honestly be surprised to be wrong; build calibration by tracking how often your "90 percent sure" claims come true; and when comparing yourself to others, remember that everyone runs the same self-flattering reasoning.

10. **Reframe before you decide (preference reversals).** Logically equivalent descriptions of the same choice produce different decisions. A gain framing makes people risk-averse and a loss framing makes them risk-seeking, even when the outcomes are identical. The same payment stimulates spending when called a "bonus" and saving when called a "rebate." Certainty gets overvalued: people pay disproportionately to remove the last unit of risk and fall for pseudocertainty (full protection against one named hazard feels better than a larger reduction across hazards). To apply: restate every important option in at least two frames, in gains and in losses, in absolute and in percentage terms, and check whether your preference survives. If it flips, you have found a framing artifact rather than a real preference.

11. **Use joint evaluation to expose what separate evaluation hides.** Evaluating options one at a time lets emotionally salient, easy-to-judge attributes dominate; evaluating them side by side surfaces the attributes that actually matter. People pay more for the intact dictionary alone but for the bigger dictionary when comparing, because word count is hard to value in isolation and easy to value in comparison. This is the "want versus should" split: the affectively arousing "want" option wins in isolation, the reasoned "should" option wins in comparison. To apply: force important decisions into joint, side-by-side comparison with explicit criteria so the hard-to-evaluate but decisive attributes get their weight, and so the louder emotional attribute does not run away with the choice.

12. **Break the escalation of commitment.** People throw good money after bad to justify a prior decision, both unilaterally (sticking with a failing project because of sunk costs) and competitively (the dollar-auction dynamic where two bidders trap each other into overpaying). Sunk costs are irrelevant to the forward-looking choice; only future costs and benefits should count. To apply: judge every continuation decision as if you were arriving fresh today with no prior investment. Ask "knowing what I know now, would I start this from zero?" Separate the person who can stop a project from the person who launched it, and pre-commit to kill criteria before you are emotionally invested.

13. **Adopt the outside view.** Insiders forecast from the specifics of their own case and ignore the base rate of similar cases, which breeds optimism and planning failures. Entrepreneurs dwell on their personal path to success and skip the failure rate for ventures like theirs. The outside view asks what happened to the reference class of comparable projects. To apply: before estimating cost, time, or success odds, find the distribution of outcomes for a class of similar past efforts and start your estimate from that distribution, then adjust only modestly for what is genuinely special about your case.

14. **Aggregate small positive-expected-value bets.** Risk aversion makes people reject individually attractive gambles that would be clearly worth taking in bulk. Samuelson's colleague refused one coin flip with a positive expected value but happily took a hundred, because aggregation shrinks the risk while keeping the edge. The mechanism: loss aversion overweights the single downside in isolation. To apply: for the many small, repeated, favorable risks in life and business, decide by expected value rather than by the fear of one bad outcome; treat them as a portfolio. Reserve special risk-aversion for the rare large, one-shot decisions, and analyze those from multiple frames.

## Named concepts, biases, fallacies, and techniques

- **Bounded rationality and satisficing (Simon).** We lack the information, memory, and computing power to optimize, so we search only until we find an option that is good enough and stop. Knowing this tells you where to spend more search effort: on high-stakes, infrequent decisions.
- **The five bounds.** The book organizes human limits as bounded rationality, bounded willpower (present bias, undersaving), bounded self-interest (we genuinely care about others' outcomes and fairness), bounded awareness (we miss obvious, available information outside our focus), and bounded ethicality (our ethics fail in ways we cannot see).
- **Bounded awareness: inattentional blindness, change blindness, focalism.** Sharp focus on one thing makes you miss information that is right in front of you, including obvious changes and the very information a competitor's behavior is signaling. In strategic settings (auctions, negotiations), failing to think about what the other side knows produces the winner's curse.
- **The conjunction fallacy (Linda problem).** People rank "bank teller and feminist" as more probable than "bank teller," which is impossible, because the detailed story is more representative. A conjunction can never be more likely than either of its parts.
- **Anchoring.** An arbitrary starting number (even your phone digits) pulls numerical estimates toward it. First offers in negotiation anchor the entire range.
- **Hindsight bias and the curse of knowledge.** After an outcome, you overestimate how predictable it was and how much you knew in advance, which corrupts learning and unfairly blames decision-makers for bad luck.
- **Endowment effect and loss aversion.** Owning something raises its value to you; losses loom larger than equivalent gains (roughly two to one). Sellers demand far more than buyers will pay for the identical item, and unsold goods pile up.
- **Mental accounting.** Money is tracked in separate non-fungible mental buckets, so identical sums get treated differently depending on their label and source. Two separate small losses hurt more than one combined larger loss; windfalls labeled "bonus" get spent.
- **Transaction utility.** Purchases carry value beyond the item: the pleasure or pain of the deal relative to what you think the item "should" cost. This explains paying more for the same beer from a fancy hotel than a grocery store.
- **Certainty and pseudocertainty effects.** Moving a risk to exactly zero is overvalued relative to an equal-sized reduction that leaves residual risk; framing partial coverage as "full protection" against one hazard exploits this. It drives the overselling of insurance and extended warranties.
- **Self-serving fairness bias.** People interpret what is "fair" in whatever way favors them, then sincerely believe it, which fuels impasse in negotiation and disputes.
- **The mythical fixed pie.** Negotiators assume their interests are purely opposed and miss trades that create value by exploiting differences in priorities across issues.
- **Lewin's unfreeze-change-refreeze.** Lasting debiasing requires unfreezing old habits (seeing your errors), supplying new methods, then refreezing the new process so it sticks. Awareness alone does not change behavior.

## What this book uniquely contributes
It translates the academic heuristics-and-biases tradition (Simon, Tversky, Kahneman, Thaler) into a manager's working toolkit, organized around a clean normative benchmark (the rational model) so every bias is defined as a specific, measurable deviation you can spot and correct. Three contributions stand out. First, it is built from self-administered problems: you commit to an answer, then see exactly how your own judgment broke, which makes the lessons hard to dismiss. Second, it widens the frame beyond cognition to bounded willpower, self-interest, awareness, and ethicality, treating ethical failure as a predictable judgment error rather than a character flaw. Third, it closes with concrete debiasing strategies (acquire expertise, reason by analogy to a reference class, take the outsider's view, understand others' biases, and design nudges) and is honest that awareness alone fades, so it builds in the unfreeze-change-refreeze discipline for making improvement durable.

## Memorable principles and examples (paraphrased)
- A test that is highly accurate can still be wrong most of the times it fires a positive, when the condition is rare. The rarity of the condition rather than the accuracy of the test, governs what a positive result means. Always populate the natural frequencies before you trust a signal.
- Flight instructors concluded that scolding works and praise backfires, because pilots tended to do worse after praise for a great landing and better after criticism for a bad one. The real driver was regression to the mean. Their feedback may have done nothing at all, and the false lesson favored punishment.
- A colleague turned down a single coin flip with a positive expected value yet wanted to take a hundred of them. The book's point: for the steady stream of small favorable risks in a career, decide by expected value and let the portfolio absorb the variance, rather than flinching at each isolated downside.
- The U.S. government's 2001 payment did less to stimulate spending because it was labeled a "rebate," which people mentally filed as money returning them to baseline and therefore saved. The identical sum labeled a "bonus" reads as surplus cash and gets spent. The label rather than the amount, drove the behavior.
