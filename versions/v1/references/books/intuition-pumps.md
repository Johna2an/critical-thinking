# Intuition Pumps and Other Tools for Thinking, Daniel C. Dennett

## Thesis

Good thinking about hard problems depends on a kit of reusable mental tools (vivid thought experiments, warning labels, and reverse-engineering habits) that extend a limited brain the way a workshop extends a pair of bare hands, and the same craft that builds persuasive tools lets you detect the rigged ones.

## Thinking tools

**1. The intuition pump (and "turn all the knobs")**
A short, vivid story engineered to provoke a confident gut verdict about a hard thesis. The mechanism: concrete imagery bypasses the fatigue of abstract argument and makes an intuition feel inevitable. To use one well, treat it as a device with adjustable settings. List every feature of the story (who, when, how often, how extreme), then change each setting one at a time. If your verdict survives every variation, the intuition tracks something real. If flipping one detail (asleep versus drugged, once versus every night, a cell door versus the borders of Australia) collapses the verdict, that detail was carrying the persuasion, and the pump may be smuggling in a conclusion. Apply this to any thought experiment you read before you trust the feeling it gives you.

**2. Reductio ad absurdum ("by parody of reasoning")**
Grant your opponent's premises in full, then derive a contradiction or an absurd consequence from them. The mechanism: a single impossible conclusion proves at least one premise is false, without needing to identify which one first. To apply it, take the claim seriously, push it to its logical edge, and look for the point where it collides with itself. Watch the failure mode too: a parody only works if you reasoned validly, so check that you did not slip in a step the original argument never required.

**3. Rapoport's Rules (steelmanning before criticizing)**
Before you attack a position, re-express it so clearly and fairly that your opponent says "I wish I had put it that way," list every point you agree with, name what you learned from it, and only then state your disagreement. The mechanism: this disarms defensiveness, forces you to actually understand the view, and earns you a hearing. It also protects you from beating a strawman and mistaking the win for a real one. Apply it whenever the stakes or your irritation are high.

**4. Sturgeon's Law (judge a field by its best work)**
Roughly ninety percent of everything is mediocre, so critiquing the weak examples of a discipline proves nothing about the discipline. The mechanism: cherry-picking the dregs is easy and dishonest. Apply it by directing your criticism at the strongest available version of a position, and by forgiving yourself for the inevitable mediocrity in your own field while still aiming above it.

**5. Occam's Razor and Occam's Broom**
The Razor: prefer the explanation that needs the fewest assumptions, because needless complexity is usually a sign of error rather than depth. The Broom is its dishonest cousin: sweeping inconvenient facts under the rug, quietly omitting the data that would sink your thesis. Apply the Razor when choosing between live hypotheses. Apply suspicion of the Broom when reading anyone's case: ask what facts a motivated author would have left out, then go looking for them.

**6. Jootsing (jumping out of the system)**
Creativity and breakthrough often come from stepping outside the framework whose rules you have been obeying, so you can see the rules themselves as choices rather than walls. The mechanism: many "impossible" problems are only impossible inside an assumed system. Apply it by asking which unstated rules you are taking for granted, and what becomes thinkable if you suspend one. Making the familiar strange is a deliberate version of this move.

**7. The "surely" alarm**
The word "surely" (and its cousins, the italicized emphasis and the triumphant repetition) usually marks the exact spot where an author asserts instead of argues. The mechanism: "surely" sits at the boundary of what the writer is confident about and hopes you will wave through, so it flags the weakest joint in the argument. Apply it by searching any persuasive text for "surely" and treating each hit as a place to stop and ask "is this actually true, or is it being slipped past me?"

**8. The rhetorical-question test**
A rhetorical question is a compressed argument that assumes its answer is too obvious to state. The mechanism: by refusing to voice the answer, the author dodges the burden of defending it. Apply it by silently supplying a good, non-obvious answer to every rhetorical question you meet. When you find one, the question's persuasive force evaporates.

**9. The deepity detector**
A deepity is a statement that sounds profound because it is ambiguous between two readings: one reading is true but trivial, the other would be earth-shaking but is false. The listener absorbs the importance of the false reading and the truth of the trivial one and mistakes the blend for wisdom ("love is just a word"). The mechanism: ambiguity launders triviality into apparent depth. Apply it by separating the two readings explicitly. Once you ask "true-but-trivial, or thrilling-but-false?", the spell breaks.

**10. The intentional stance**
To predict the behavior of any sufficiently complex system (a person, an animal, a thermostat, a chess program), treat it as a rational agent with beliefs, desires, and goals, and ask what it ought to do given them. The mechanism: this stance buys enormous predictive power cheaply, without knowing the system's physical guts. It complements two other stances: the physical stance (predict from physics and chemistry) and the design stance (predict from what something was built or evolved to do). Apply it by choosing the stance that pays off best for your purpose, and by remembering that calling something an agent is a useful interpretive posture rather than a claim about a soul inside.

**11. The "sorta" operator and competence without comprehension**
Real understanding is built out of components that only "sorta" understand, stacked into layers, none of which grasps the whole. The mechanism: a neuron sorta detects, a circuit sorta recognizes, and full comprehension emerges from millions of these partial competences with no magic ingredient added. This dissolves the demand for a single inner "understander." Apply it whenever you are tempted to posit a homunculus (a little intelligent agent inside) to explain intelligence: replace it with a cascade of dumber sub-agents, each doing a sliver of the job, until the residual cleverness needed at the bottom is nearly zero.

**12. Cranes versus skyhooks (lifting in design space)**
A crane is an explanation that lifts you to greater complexity using mechanisms already grounded in what came before (natural selection, learning, culture). A skyhook is a miracle hung from nothing, an appeal to design that floats free of any prior process. The mechanism: evolution and other cranes do real lifting without borrowing from the future or from magic. Apply it by demanding, of any account of how complexity or competence arose, that it bottom out in cranes. Treat every skyhook as an IOU that has not been paid.

**13. The "surely"-class bad tools: rathering, piling on, and the boom crutch**
A boom crutch is a thinking tool that backfires, spreading confusion while looking like illumination. "Rathering" is a specific one: forcing a false either/or by saying "it is not X, rather it is Y" when both could be true, or when a third option is the better answer. "Piling on" buries a weak central claim under a heap of loosely related true points so the reader credits the whole. Apply these as detectors: when you meet a forced dichotomy, ask whether both horns could hold; when you meet a pile of supporting facts, find the load-bearing claim and test it alone.

**14. Free-floating rationales**
A behavior can have a good reason without any creature representing that reason. The mechanism: natural selection embeds purposes into organisms (a cuckoo chick's eviction of rivals has a clear adaptive rationale) even though the chick comprehends nothing. The reason floats free of any mind that holds it. Apply it to avoid two opposite errors: do not deny that there is a reason just because no one is thinking it, and do not assume a sophisticated mind just because the behavior is well-aimed.

## Named concepts, biases, fallacies, and techniques

- **Going meta (semantic ascent):** Climb from arguing about a thing to arguing about how we talk and reason about the thing. Useful exactly when people are talking past each other or fooled by their own words; useless or evasive when deployed to dodge a concrete question.
- **Manifest image versus scientific image (Sellars):** The everyday world of solid tables, colors, dollars, and free will, set beside the world of atoms, fields, and quarks. Most deep confusion comes from failing to line these two pictures up. Neither is an illusion; the task is registration between them.
- **Original versus derived intentionality (and Dennett's dissolution of it):** Searle's distinction holds that artifacts mean things only because we interpret them (derived), while our own thoughts mean things intrinsically (original). Dennett's two-bitser, Twin Earth, and giant-robot pumps argue that on close inspection our own "original" meaning works the same way derived meaning does, with our genes as the ultimate "unmeant meaners."
- **Folk psychology:** The universal human knack for reading others as agents with beliefs and desires. It is a practiced skill more than an explicit theory, which is why "theory of mind" can mislead.
- **The Chinese Room (Searle) reconsidered:** A famous pump arguing that symbol manipulation cannot produce understanding. Dennett treats it as a defective pump: it works by shrinking the system in your imagination so you overlook the staggering complexity that real understanding would require, then invites you to conclude no understanding is present.
- **Beware the Prime Mammal:** The error of demanding a sharp first instance of a gradually emerging category (the first mammal, the first living thing, the first conscious being). Continuous processes have no clean first member, and insisting on one manufactures a pseudo-problem.
- **Sphexishness:** Behavior that looks intelligent and flexible but is actually a rigid loop, named for the digger wasp Sphex that re-checks its burrow in an endless cycle when you keep nudging its prey. A reminder to test apparent understanding by perturbing the situation and watching whether the agent adapts or just repeats.
- **The self as a center of narrative gravity:** The self is a useful abstraction, real in the way a center of gravity is real (a calculable, predictive fiction with no physical location), generated by the stories a brain tells.
- **Heterophenomenology:** A neutral method for studying consciousness scientifically by taking subjects' reports as data about what they believe they experience, without prejudging whether those beliefs are accurate.

## What this book uniquely contributes

The book treats persuasion devices as engineering objects you can take apart. Its signature contribution is the "turn the knobs" discipline: a repeatable procedure for stress-testing any thought experiment by varying its parameters one at a time, which converts a vague feeling of "that seems right" into a checkable claim about which detail is doing the work. Alongside this it supplies a working taxonomy of bad tools (boom crutches, rathering, the surely operator, deepities, Occam's Broom) so a reader can name a fishy move the moment it appears. It also models intellectual generosity as a method rather than a courtesy, through Rapoport's Rules. The result is a kit that is simultaneously offensive (how to build a convincing case) and defensive (how to detect when one is being run on you).

## Memorable principles and examples (paraphrased)

- **The bird and the trains.** Asked how far a bird flies zigzagging between two trains closing on each other, von Neumann instantly summed the infinite series in his head, missing the one-line shortcut: figure out how long until the trains meet, then multiply by the bird's speed. Genius can blind you to the easy path, so reach for the simple route before the laborious one.

- **The whimsical jailer.** A jailer unlocks every cell each night while the prisoners sleep, then relocks before dawn. They never leave. Are they free? Barely, because an opportunity you cannot detect in time to act on is no real opportunity. Freedom needs timely information about genuine options, which means free choice requires being embedded in a rich web of causes rather than cut off from it.

- **Making mistakes is the method.** Treat errors as the raw material of progress rather than as shame to be hidden. The history of life is a history of mistakes (mutations) that occasionally pay off, and the same holds for thinking: a well-examined blunder teaches more than a guarded near-miss, so cultivate the habit of being wrong out loud and studying the wreckage.

- **The two-bitser in Panama.** A coin detector built to spot US quarters works perfectly on Panamanian coins of identical metal once shipped abroad, and now the US quarters count as the "errors." What the device means depends entirely on the purpose it was selected to serve rather than on anything intrinsic inside it. Meaning, even arguably our own, is fixed by function and history rather than by some inner essence.
