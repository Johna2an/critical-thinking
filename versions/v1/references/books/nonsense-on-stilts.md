# Nonsense on Stilts, Distilled

**Author:** Massimo Pigliucci (2nd ed., 2010/2018)

## Thesis

Telling sound science from bunk is a learnable skill that rests on probabilistic reasoning, an honest grasp of what each kind of science can and cannot do, and a disciplined approach to trusting expertise, because no single litmus test cleanly separates the two and the cost of getting it wrong runs from wasted money to lost lives.

## Thinking Tools

1. **The demarcation gradient (reject the bright line).** Science and pseudoscience sit on a fuzzy continuum, with "almost science" in between. **Why:** Popper's hope that one rule (falsifiability) would draw a clean border failed, because good science survives apparent counterexamples and bad science can mimic any single criterion. **Apply:** Stop asking "is this science, yes or no." Score a claim across several dimensions at once: does it generate testable predictions, does it respond to disconfirming evidence, does it make progress, do its practitioners accept failure. A field that scores low on most is bunk; one that scores high on most is science; mixed scores mark the borderlands.

2. **Falsifiability, used as a flashlight not a guillotine.** A genuine scientific claim specifies what observation would count against it. **Why:** A theory that fits every conceivable outcome (classic examples: a history that is always class struggle, a psychology that is always repressed drives) explains nothing because it forbids nothing. **Apply:** Ask any claimant, "What result would make you abandon this?" A confident "nothing could" is the tell. Use the answer as diagnostic evidence rather than as an automatic verdict, since real theories also get a fair chance to absorb a few anomalies before being dropped.

3. **Probability over possibility.** Decisions should track how likely something is rather than whether it is merely conceivable. **Why:** Almost any non-mathematical statement is "possible" because its negation involves no logical contradiction, so possibility-talk lets a skeptic stall forever ("but can you rule it out?"). **Apply:** When someone retreats to "isn't it possible that...," concede the bare possibility, then redirect to the live question: how probable is it given the evidence, and which way would you bet your money or your child's health? Treat a flat "everything is equally possible" distribution as the error it is; real probability distributions are sharply uneven.

4. **Proportion belief to evidence (Hume) and demand extraordinary evidence for extraordinary claims (Sagan).** Confidence should rise and fall with the weight and quality of evidence. **Why:** A claim that overturns well-established knowledge needs proportionally stronger support to move a rational person's prior. **Apply:** Before accepting a striking result, ask how much it would have to overturn and whether the evidence is strong enough to carry that load. Weak evidence for a mundane claim can be enough; weak evidence for a revolutionary claim is not.

5. **Bayesian updating as a habit of mind.** Treat a belief as a bet whose odds you revise as evidence arrives. **Why:** The posterior probability of a hypothesis depends on how well it predicts the data times your starting estimate (the prior); iterated over enough evidence, honest updating converges toward the truth regardless of where you began. **Apply:** State your prior out loud. When new evidence appears, ask whether it is more expected under the hypothesis or under its rivals, and shift accordingly. Resist both the refusal to update (dogmatism) and the wild swing on thin data.

6. **The historical-versus-experimental science distinction.** Sciences vary along two axes: historical to ahistorical, and simple-tractable to complex-statistical. **Why:** Experimental sciences (physics, chemistry) excel at precise prediction under controlled conditions but degrade fast as variables multiply and you leave the lab; historical sciences (paleontology, cosmology, much of evolutionary biology) cannot rerun the past, so they reason like detectives from present traces toward a "smoking gun." Neither is "harder" or better. **Apply:** Calibrate your expectations to the field. Demanding lab-grade prediction from a historical science, or treating its inability to forecast as a defect, is a category error. Judge each by what its subject matter actually permits.

7. **The baloney detector (systematic skepticism toward all authority).** Run a standing checklist on every strong claim, including from credentialed sources and self-described skeptics. **Why:** A PhD and a university affiliation are neither necessary nor sufficient for being right; legitimate academics endorse fringe ideas, and even good debunkers carry biases (a libertarian think-tank fellow downplaying climate findings, for example). **Apply:** For any claim, independently check the source's track record and funding, look for converging evidence rather than a lone study, ask whether the claim has been replicated, and verify before repeating. Keep an open mind without letting your brain fall out.

8. **The p-value / large-sample trap.** Statistical "significance" measures incompatibility with chance rather than the size or reality of an effect. **Why:** With a huge number of trials, even a microscopic bias in the apparatus (a coin a hair heavier on one side) produces a tiny deviation with a vanishingly small p-value, manufacturing "significance" where nothing interesting is happening (the paranormal-lab results are the canonical case). **Apply:** Always ask for effect size and sample size alongside the p-value. A minuscule effect across millions of trials points to instrument bias rather than discovery. Distrust a result that requires gigantic samples to surface.

9. **Replication and the simplest-explanation test.** A real effect survives independent labs reproducing it; the simplest hypothesis is usually that an unreplicable effect was never there. **Why:** Failed paranormal research repeatedly produced positive results in one lab that vanished when outsiders, magicians, or pre-registered protocols entered, which is what you expect from artifacts rather than phenomena. **Apply:** Weight independently replicated results far above one-off findings. Favor pre-registration. When a claimed effect cannot be reproduced and its defenders pivot to excuses and martyrdom, treat that pivot itself as the signature of pseudoscience.

10. **Spot informal fallacies, and avoid weaponizing the labels.** Learn the common moves: red herring (irrelevant diversion), ad hominem (attacking the person), appeal to authority (the lone credentialed dissenter), Gish gallop (burying an opponent in many weak claims). **Why:** These structures let bad arguments feel persuasive, and naming them clarifies a tangled exchange. **Apply:** When a conversation lurches between unconnected topics, name the red herring and steer back. But note the trap: pointing out a person's ideological motive is sometimes legitimately relevant, so "ad hominem!" can itself be misused as a thought-terminating shortcut. Distinguish a relevant fact about a source from a genuine fallacy.

11. **The manufacture-of-doubt tactic.** Recognize the deliberate strategy of seeding confusion so the public concludes "nobody really knows." **Why:** The tobacco industry pioneered it on smoking and cancer; the same playbook recurs on climate, vaccines, and intelligent design (documented in *Merchants of Doubt*). Doubt is the product. **Apply:** When you encounter relentless emphasis on uncertainty around a well-settled question, ask who benefits from the confusion and whether genuine expert disagreement exists or only manufactured noise. Existence of a controversy among the public is not evidence of controversy among experts.

12. **False balance.** Equal airtime is not equal validity. **Why:** Presenting two positions as evenly matched ("you decide") misleads audiences when one side reflects overwhelming expert consensus and the other a tiny fringe; there is no scientific evenly-split debate over evolution or basic climate physics. **Apply:** Before accepting a "both sides" framing, check where the weight of qualified expertise actually sits. Resist the reporter's reflex to balance every story; some questions have a settled answer and a vocal minority, which is a different thing from a live scientific dispute.

13. **The expertise / trust triage.** Since you cannot personally verify most claims, you must decide whom to trust on rational grounds. **Why:** Credentials alone fail, yet wholesale distrust of all expertise is worse; the practical question is how to allocate trust well. **Apply:** Prefer the relevant specialty over a credentialed outsider (a physicist on consciousness, or a celebrity on vaccines, is off-domain). Weight consensus among working specialists over lone mavericks. Check whether the expert's conclusion is driven by ideology or funding. Treat the "I'll just make up my own mind" stance as dangerous when a little knowledge masquerades as enough.

14. **Logos, ethos, pathos (honest persuasion).** Changing minds needs sound argument (logos), established credibility (ethos), and emotional connection (pathos), used ethically. **Why:** Facts and logic alone rarely move people whose beliefs are psychologically comforting; rationality "goes out the window" under threat to cherished identity. **Apply:** When you want to persuade rather than win a debate, get the facts right, establish why you can be trusted, and make the audience care, without manipulating emotion. Recognize that public debates are scored on rhetoric and likability more than on evidence, and prepare accordingly.

15. **Scientism check and virtue epistemology.** Resist the overreach that science answers every meaningful question, and cultivate intellectual virtues. **Why:** Science cannot settle questions of value (ethics, aesthetics) and has real limits; treating it as sovereign over all inquiry is itself a distortion. The durable cure for both credulity and cynicism is character: open-mindedness, intellectual honesty, humility, courage to follow evidence. **Apply:** Hold science as the best tool for empirical questions while granting that some questions are not empirical. Practice the virtues deliberately, because critical thinking is a habit you build rather than a fact you memorize.

## Named Concepts, Biases, Fallacies, and Techniques

- **Demarcation problem:** the challenge of separating science from non-science and pseudoscience; the book's organizing question, answered as a gradient rather than a line.
- **Falsifiability (Popper):** a claim is scientific if some observation could in principle refute it; useful but insufficient on its own.
- **Possibility versus probability:** the rhetorical move of treating any conceivable scenario as decision-relevant; counter by shifting to likelihood.
- **Bayes's theorem / priors / posterior:** the mathematics of updating belief with evidence; convergence to truth over iterations regardless of starting point.
- **Regress trilemma (Agrippa / Sextus Empiricus):** every justification ends in circularity, infinite regress, or unproven axioms, so absolute certainty is unreachable, which licenses fallibilism rather than relativism.
- **Smoking-gun reasoning:** in historical sciences, a single decisive trace can confirm a hypothesis about the unrepeatable past.
- **Statistical significance / p-value pitfall:** significance with enormous samples can reflect tiny apparatus bias rather than a real effect.
- **Conditional versus unconditional probability:** drawing cards without replacement changes the odds as you go; misapplying fixed odds inflates apparent results (the Zener-card error).
- **Red herring, ad hominem, appeal to authority, Gish gallop:** common informal fallacies, with the caveat that fallacy labels can themselves be misused as shortcuts to avoid engaging an argument.
- **Manufacture of doubt:** funded campaigns that sell uncertainty to paralyze public judgment.
- **False balance / "fair and balanced":** misleading equal treatment of unequally supported positions.
- **Anthropic principle, multiverse, string theory:** mathematically rich but currently untestable ideas that illustrate the live edge between physics and philosophy.
- **Fallibilism, coherentism, foundationalism, infinitism:** epistemological stances on living without certainty.
- **Scientism:** the overextension of scientific authority into domains it cannot settle.
- **Virtue epistemology:** grounding good reasoning in intellectual character rather than in a mechanical rulebook.

## What This Book Uniquely Contributes

It refuses the comforting fantasy of a one-line test for science and replaces it with a working map. Three contributions stand out. First, it reframes the question as a graded landscape, giving "almost science" (string theory, SETI, evolutionary psychology) its own honest category so that borderline fields are neither canonized nor dismissed. Second, it treats the variety of sciences seriously, showing that historical and experimental disciplines have different powers and limits, which dissolves the lazy hard-versus-soft hierarchy. Third, it puts the problem of expertise and trust at the center: in a world too complex to verify firsthand, knowing whom to believe and why becomes the practical core of critical thinking, with intellectual virtue as the lasting remedy.

## Memorable Principles and Examples (paraphrased)

- **Keep your mind open, but not so open your brain falls out.** Genuine inquiry weighs fringe claims fairly; it also stops giving infinite hearings to claims that have failed every honest test.
- **A theory that explains everything explains nothing.** When a framework can absorb any possible observation, it has lost the power to inform you about the world.
- **A weighted coin, flipped a million times, will look "significant."** A microscopic, real bias plus a gigantic sample manufactures statistical importance, which is why effect size and replication matter more than a small p-value.
- **A diploma is neither necessary nor sufficient for being right.** Credentialed people endorse nonsense and outsiders sometimes see clearly, so trust the convergence of relevant specialists over any single authority, and verify before you repeat.
