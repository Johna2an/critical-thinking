# Thinking, Fast and Slow, Daniel Kahneman

## Thesis

Most judgment runs on a fast, automatic, intuition-generating mental system that is brilliant at pattern recognition and terrible at statistics, so thinking well means knowing when to slow down and override the answer that came to mind first.

## Thinking tools

### 1. The two-system model (System 1 and System 2)
**Definition.** Treat the mind as two characters. System 1 is fast, automatic, effortless, always on, and emotional; it produces impressions, intuitions, and snap answers. System 2 is slow, deliberate, effortful, and lazy; it allocates attention, does arithmetic, checks logic, and can override System 1 when it bothers to engage.
**Why it works.** Effortful thinking is metabolically expensive, so System 2 conserves energy by endorsing System 1's suggestions by default. Most of the time the intuitions are good enough. The failures cluster in predictable places where intuition has no machinery for the right answer (probability, sample size, regression).
**How to apply.** When a question involves numbers, chance, sample size, or a high-stakes commitment, name it as a System 2 task and deliberately slow down. Treat the first answer that arrives as a hypothesis to test rather than a conclusion. The felt ease of an answer tells you nothing about its correctness.

### 2. Attribute substitution (answering an easier question)
**Definition.** When facing a hard question ("Is this stock underpriced?"), System 1 quietly swaps in an easier one it can answer ("Do I like this company?") and reports the answer to the easy question as if it answered the hard one. You rarely notice the substitution.
**Why it works.** System 1 always has an answer ready for an easy, emotionally loaded question, and that answer arrives faster than the recognition that it addressed the wrong question.
**How to apply.** Ask yourself: what is the exact question I was asked, and what question did I actually answer? If a judgment about probability, value, or risk feels suspiciously easy and emotionally clear, suspect a substitution and force yourself back to the original question.

### 3. WYSIATI (what you see is all there is)
**Definition.** System 1 builds the most coherent story it can from whatever information is currently available, and it does not register the information it lacks. Confidence comes from the coherence of the story rather than from the quantity or quality of evidence behind it.
**Why it works.** The mind suppresses ambiguity and runs ahead of the facts to construct meaning. Missing data simply does not enter the story, so the story feels complete.
**How to apply.** Before committing to a judgment, ask: what would I need to know to be confident, and do I actually know it? Treat a vivid, coherent narrative built on thin evidence as a warning sign. Deliberately list the information you are missing.

### 4. Anchoring
**Definition.** Any number you consider before estimating an unknown quantity pulls your estimate toward itself, even when the number is random, absurd, or obviously uninformative (a rolled die, a spun wheel, a Social Security digit).
**Why it works.** Two mechanisms run together. System 2 adjusts away from a starting number and stops too early, at the near edge of the range it considers plausible. System 1 primes a set of memories compatible with the anchor, biasing the evidence you retrieve.
**How to apply.** Assume any number on the table has already moved you, including asking prices, opening offers, and a target's own revenue projections. To resist, deliberately generate arguments against the anchor and think about reasons it is wrong before you estimate. In negotiations, refuse to counter an outrageous opening number; reset rather than anchor on it.

### 5. The availability heuristic
**Definition.** You judge how frequent or probable something is by how easily examples come to mind. Events that are recent, vivid, dramatic, or heavily covered feel more common than they are.
**Why it works.** Ease of retrieval is a fast proxy for frequency, and it is usually adequate. It breaks when retrieval is distorted by media coverage, personal salience, or emotional intensity rather than actual frequency.
**How to apply.** When estimating risk or frequency, ask whether your sense comes from real base rates or from memorable instances. Distrust impressions shaped by news cycles and dramatic anecdotes. Reach for actual numbers when stakes are high.

### 6. Representativeness and base-rate neglect
**Definition.** You judge probability by resemblance to a stereotype while ignoring how common the categories actually are. The shy, tidy, detail-loving man feels like a librarian, so people call him a librarian even though male farmers vastly outnumber male librarians.
**Why it works.** Resemblance is computed instantly by System 1; base rates require holding a statistical fact in mind and combining it with the description, which System 1 cannot do and System 2 usually skips.
**How to apply.** Start from the base rate (how common is this category in the population?) and let the specific evidence adjust it, rather than letting a vivid description erase the prior. Ask "how many of these exist in total?" before asking "how much does this resemble the type?"

### 7. The conjunction fallacy (the Linda problem)
**Definition.** Adding plausible, specific detail to a scenario makes it feel more probable even though every added condition can only make it less probable. People rate "bank teller and active feminist" as more likely than "bank teller," which is logically impossible.
**Why it works.** A richer, more coherent story is more representative and easier to picture, and System 1 reads ease and coherence as likelihood.
**How to apply.** Be suspicious when a detailed, specific forecast feels more credible than a vague one. More conditions joined together always mean lower probability. Strip a scenario to its components and ask whether each added detail is buying credibility it has not earned.

### 8. Regression to the mean
**Definition.** Extreme outcomes tend to be followed by more average ones for purely statistical reasons, with no cause involved. A pilot who flew brilliantly will usually fly closer to average next time; a stock-picker on a streak will usually cool off.
**Why it works.** Any performance combines skill and luck. When luck runs extreme in one direction, the next observation usually has less extreme luck, so it moves toward the average. The mind refuses to accept "luck" as an explanation and invents a causal story instead (praise hurt the pilot, the bonus spoiled the trader).
**How to apply.** Before explaining why something improved or worsened after an extreme, ask whether plain regression already accounts for it. Distrust causal stories built on top of a single extreme observation. Expect star performers and disasters to drift toward average without any intervention.

### 9. The law of small numbers
**Definition.** Small samples produce extreme results far more often than large ones, yet people treat small-sample findings as reliable signals about the world. Both the best and the worst schools tend to be small, because small samples are simply more variable.
**Why it works.** System 1 expects small samples to resemble the population (it believes in coherence), and it seeks causes for patterns that are actually sampling noise.
**How to apply.** Ask how many observations a claim rests on before you believe it. Treat streaks, hot hands, and "best of" lists as candidates for chance until a large sample says otherwise. When the sample is small, the right move is to withhold conclusions rather than to explain them.

### 10. The planning fallacy and the outside view
**Definition.** Forecasts of cost and time are built from an optimistic, detail-rich picture of the specific project (the inside view) and systematically run over. The outside view sets the project aside and asks how similar projects actually turned out (reference-class forecasting).
**Why it works.** The inside view generates a smooth best-case scenario and neglects the unknown obstacles that derail almost every comparable effort. The base rate of similar projects captures those obstacles even when you cannot name them in advance.
**How to apply.** For any plan, find the reference class of comparable past efforts and anchor your forecast on their real outcomes, then adjust for specifics. Treat the project's own plan as a best-case scenario rather than a prediction. Run a premortem: imagine the project has failed and write the story of how.

### 11. Prospect theory (loss aversion and reference points)
**Definition.** People value gains and losses relative to a reference point rather than valuing final wealth, and losses hurt roughly twice as much as equivalent gains feel good. Outcomes are weighted by change from the status quo, with diminishing sensitivity.
**Why it works.** The mind is built to track changes and threats, so a drop below the current state registers more sharply than an equal rise above it. This explains risk-seeking to avoid a sure loss and risk-aversion to lock in a sure gain.
**How to apply.** Notice that how a choice is referenced (as a gain or a loss) changes your preference even when the money is identical. Set reference points deliberately. Recognize that the fear of losing what you have will tempt you into bad gambles to avoid booking a loss.

### 12. Framing effects
**Definition.** Logically identical descriptions of the same choice produce different decisions depending on wording. "90 percent survival" is accepted where "10 percent mortality" is refused; keeping versus losing the same outcome flips the choice.
**Why it works.** System 1 responds to the surface form of a description and the emotional valence of its words rather than to the underlying facts, which it does not translate into a common representation.
**How to apply.** Reframe every important decision in at least two ways, including its opposite, and check whether your preference survives the reframing. A preference that flips with wording is a preference you do not actually hold. Watch for survival-versus-mortality, gain-versus-loss, and discount-versus-surcharge framings.

### 13. Overconfidence, the illusion of validity, and hindsight
**Definition.** People hold subjective confidence that far outstrips their actual accuracy. The illusion of validity is the unshaken confidence experts feel even after evidence shows their judgments fail. Hindsight bias rewrites memory so past events feel as if they were predictable, fueling more overconfidence.
**Why it works.** Confidence is produced by the coherence of the story you can tell (WYSIATI), and a coherent story is easy to build regardless of predictive accuracy. After the fact, the mind updates its model and forgets it ever believed otherwise.
**How to apply.** Calibrate confidence against track record rather than against how convincing your story feels. Keep a decision journal recording what you predicted and how sure you were, so hindsight cannot rewrite it. In domains that are low-validity and noisy (long-range forecasting, stock picking), distrust expert intuition and lean on simple statistical rules.

### 14. When to trust expert intuition (the two conditions)
**Definition.** Intuitive expertise is real but conditional. Valid intuition requires an environment regular enough to contain learnable patterns, and prolonged practice with rapid, clear feedback. Chess masters, firefighters, and anesthesiologists meet these conditions; long-range political and financial forecasters do not.
**Why it works.** Intuition is recognition. Skilled intuition forms only when the world has stable cues to recognize and the learner gets feedback fast enough to associate cues with outcomes. Without regularity and feedback, "expertise" is confident pattern-matching on noise.
**How to apply.** Before trusting a gut call (yours or an expert's), ask whether the domain is regular and whether the person got real feedback while learning it. If either condition fails, substitute algorithms, checklists, or base rates for intuition. In low-validity environments, simple formulas reliably beat expert judgment.

## Named concepts, biases, and techniques (quick reference)

- **Halo effect**, one salient good trait (confidence, attractiveness) colors judgment of unrelated traits, manufacturing a coherent but unfounded overall impression.
- **Affect heuristic**, liking or disliking drives judgments of benefit and risk; if you like a thing you judge its risks low and benefits high.
- **Cognitive ease vs. strain**, fluent, familiar, easy-to-process information feels true and pleasant; strain (hard fonts, unfamiliar claims) triggers System 2 scrutiny. Repetition breeds belief.
- **Priming and the ideomotor effect**, exposure to a word or idea unconsciously shifts later thoughts and even behavior; we are nudged by context far more than introspection reveals.
- **Ego depletion**, self-control and effortful thought draw on a limited, fatigable resource; depleted or hungry people fall back on easy defaults (tired judges deny more parole).
- **Cognitive Reflection Test**, the bat-and-ball puzzle and its kin separate people who check intuitions from those who endorse the first answer; failing predicts impulsivity and bias.
- **Denominator neglect**, vivid numerators (a few winning tickets) overpower the denominator, so "1 in 1,000" feels riskier when stated as "10 in 10,000."
- **Possibility and certainty effects**, moving from impossible to merely possible, or from very likely to certain, is overweighted; this distorts how we price rare events and insurance.
- **The fourfold pattern**, risk attitudes flip across the four cells of high/low probability crossed with gains/losses, explaining why people buy both lottery tickets and insurance.
- **Endowment effect**, once you own something, you demand far more to give it up than you would have paid for it, because losing it is a loss and loss looms large.
- **Narrow framing and mental accounting**, treating decisions one at a time instead of as a portfolio, and putting money in mental buckets, produces inconsistent and costly choices (the sunk-cost trap, the disposition effect).
- **The experiencing self vs. the remembering self**, well-being judged in the moment differs from well-being remembered; memory weights peaks and endings (peak-end rule) and ignores duration (duration neglect), so we choose to repeat experiences our remembering self misjudges.
- **The illusion of understanding (narrative fallacy)**, coherent stories about the past create false confidence that the past was predictable and the future is knowable; luck is written out of success stories.

## What this book uniquely contributes

It gives biases a precise vocabulary and a mechanism. Instead of dismissing error as carelessness or emotion, it shows that systematic mistakes are built into the architecture of normal, healthy cognition, which makes them predictable, nameable, and partly correctable. It supplies a working model (two systems) that ties dozens of separate biases to a single cause: a fast pattern-matcher that substitutes easy questions for hard ones and a lazy supervisor that rarely audits it. It also draws the boundary of intuition, specifying the exact conditions under which a gut feeling deserves trust and the conditions under which a simple formula should replace it. And it turns the abstract into the experiential through self-demonstrations, so a reader catches their own mind in the act.

## Memorable principles worth keeping (paraphrased)

- A confident professional invested tens of millions in a carmaker because he admired its cars at an auto show. He answered "do I like this company?" while believing he had answered "is this stock underpriced?" Liking is not a valuation, and the substitution went unnoticed.
- Roll a loaded die in front of experienced judges, then ask for a prison sentence, and the random number moves the sentence by months. The people most certain that an obviously useless number did not sway them are exactly the ones it swayed.
- The schools with the best results tend to be small, which inspired large investments in small schools. The schools with the worst results also tend to be small. Small samples swing to both extremes; the size was variance rather than a cause, and the causal story was built on sampling noise.
- A coherent, vivid story feels true in proportion to how easily it hangs together rather than in proportion to the evidence behind it. The mind builds the best story it can from what is in front of it and stays blind to everything it does not know.
