# Biases and Judgment, A Tool Library

This reference catalogs the predictable errors of human judgment and the self-checks that counter each one. It is the everyday core of the critical-thinking skill. The premise across every source: systematic mistakes are built into normal, healthy cognition, which makes them nameable, predictable, and partly correctable (Kahneman). The binding constraint on good judgment is attitude rather than information, so a catalog of biases only helps if you also adopt the stance that wants the truth (Galef).

Each tool is named in bold, defined in plain words, given its mechanism (the why), and given a way to apply it.

---

## 1. The Two-System Model and When to Trust Intuition

**The two-system model.** Treat the mind as two characters (Kahneman). System 1 is fast, automatic, effortless, always on, and emotional; it produces impressions, intuitions, and snap answers. System 2 is slow, deliberate, effortful, and lazy; it allocates attention, does arithmetic, checks logic, and can override System 1 when it bothers to engage. Hastie and Dawes draw the same line as automatic thinking (associational, below awareness) versus controlled thinking (deliberate "what if" reasoning that treats an observation as one of several possibilities).

**Why it works.** Effortful thinking is metabolically expensive, so System 2 conserves energy by endorsing System 1's suggestions by default. Most of the time the intuitions are good enough. The failures cluster in predictable places where intuition has no machinery for the right answer: probability, sample size, and regression (Kahneman).

**How to apply.** Treat your first impression as a draft rather than a verdict (Bazerman and Moore). When a question involves numbers, chance, sample size, or a high-stakes commitment, name it a System 2 task and deliberately slow down. The felt ease of an answer tells you nothing about its correctness. When a conclusion arrives instantly and feels obvious, treat that fluency as a flag to pause and run the controlled check (Hastie and Dawes). Reserve full System 2 analysis for choices that are important, hard to reverse, unfamiliar, or numeric; let fast intuition handle low-stakes, high-frequency, reversible choices where the cost of analysis exceeds the value of accuracy.

**The two conditions for trusting expert intuition.** Intuitive expertise is real but conditional (Kahneman). A gut call deserves trust only when two conditions both hold:
1. **The environment is regular enough to contain learnable patterns** (stable cues that repeat).
2. **The person got prolonged practice with rapid, clear feedback** while learning the domain.

Chess masters, firefighters, and anesthesiologists meet both conditions. Long-range political and financial forecasters meet neither. The mechanism: intuition is recognition, and skilled recognition forms only when the world offers stable cues and feedback fast enough to bind cues to outcomes. Without regularity and feedback, expertise is confident pattern-matching on noise. To apply: before trusting any gut call (yours or an expert's), diagnose the domain on both conditions. If either fails, substitute base rates, checklists, or simple linear models for intuition.

## 2. Attribute Substitution and WYSIATI

**Attribute substitution.** When facing a hard question ("Is this stock underpriced?"), System 1 quietly swaps in an easier one it can answer ("Do I like this company?") and reports the answer to the easy question as if it answered the hard one (Kahneman). You rarely notice the swap. The investor who put tens of millions into a carmaker because he admired its cars answered "Do I like this company?" while believing he had answered "Is this stock underpriced?"

**Why it works.** System 1 always has a ready answer for an easy, emotionally loaded question, and that answer arrives faster than the recognition that it addressed the wrong question.

**How to apply.** Ask: what is the exact question I was asked, and what question did I actually answer? If a judgment about probability, value, or risk feels suspiciously easy and emotionally clear, suspect a substitution and force yourself back to the original question.

**WYSIATI (what you see is all there is).** System 1 builds the most coherent story it can from whatever information is currently available, and it does not register the information it lacks (Kahneman). Confidence comes from the coherence of the story rather than from the quantity or quality of the evidence behind it.

**Why it works.** The mind suppresses ambiguity and runs ahead of the facts to construct meaning. Missing data never enters the story, so the story feels complete.

**How to apply.** Before committing to a judgment, ask: what would I need to know to be confident, and do I actually know it? Treat a vivid, coherent narrative built on thin evidence as a warning sign. Deliberately list the information you are missing.

## 3. Anchoring

**Anchoring.** Any number you consider before estimating an unknown quantity pulls your estimate toward itself, even when the number is random, absurd, or obviously uninformative (Kahneman). A loaded die rolled in front of experienced judges moved the prison sentences they then imposed by months, and the judges most certain the number had not swayed them were exactly the ones it swayed.

**Why it works.** Two mechanisms run together. System 2 adjusts away from a starting number and stops too early, at the near edge of the plausible range. System 1 primes memories compatible with the anchor, biasing the evidence you retrieve. You also anchor on yourself, on the present, and on the status quo (Hastie and Dawes).

**How to apply.** Assume any number on the table has already moved you: asking prices, opening offers, a target's own revenue projections. To resist, generate arguments against the anchor before you estimate, or generate an independent estimate from scratch, or deliberately anchor from the opposite extreme and average (Hastie and Dawes). In negotiation, set your own anchor first and refuse to counter an outrageous opening number; reset rather than anchor on it (Bazerman and Moore).

## 4. Availability

**The availability heuristic.** You judge how frequent or probable something is by how easily examples come to mind (Kahneman). Events that are recent, vivid, dramatic, or heavily covered feel more common than they are. Availability also runs through imagination: outcomes you can picture easily feel more likely (Hastie and Dawes).

**Why it works.** Ease of retrieval is a fast proxy for frequency and is usually adequate. It breaks when retrieval is distorted by media coverage, personal salience, or emotional intensity rather than actual frequency.

**How to apply.** When an estimate rests on "I can think of several cases," ask whether those cases come to mind because they are common or merely because they are memorable. Distrust impressions shaped by news cycles and dramatic anecdotes. Reach for an actual base rate when stakes are high.

## 5. Representativeness and Base-Rate Neglect

**Representativeness and base-rate neglect.** You judge probability by resemblance to a stereotype while ignoring how common the categories actually are (Kahneman). A shy, tidy, detail-loving man feels like a librarian, so people call him a librarian even though male farmers vastly outnumber male librarians. A person who "sounds like" an art history major gets judged a likely one even when such majors are far rarer than the alternative (Hastie and Dawes).

**Why it works.** Resemblance is computed instantly by System 1. Base rates require holding a statistical fact in mind and combining it with the description, which System 1 cannot do and System 2 usually skips.

**How to apply.** Run two separate questions. First the similarity judgment fires automatically; then ask the second, "How common is this category in the first place?" Start from the base rate and let the specific evidence adjust it (Bazerman and Moore). For a diagnostic signal, build the natural-frequency picture: a test that is 86 percent accurate for a condition that occurs once in a thousand still produces mostly false alarms, because the rare true case is swamped by false positives from the huge healthy population. Ask "out of every thousand cases, how many are real, and how many false alarms does this signal generate?"

**Confusion of the inverse.** The probability of A given B is not the probability of B given A (Hastie and Dawes). "Most hard-drug users smoked marijuana" does not mean "most marijuana users use hard drugs." The ratio rule fixes the relationship: p(A given B) and p(B given A) differ by the ratio of the base rates p(A) and p(B). To apply: name which direction a conditional points, then ask what the reverse conditional is and how the base rates differ.

## 6. The Conjunction Fallacy

**The conjunction fallacy (the Linda problem).** Adding plausible, specific detail to a scenario makes it feel more probable even though every added condition can only make it less probable (Kahneman). People rate "bank teller and active feminist" as more likely than "bank teller," which is logically impossible, because the richer story is more representative.

**Why it works.** A more coherent, easier-to-picture story is more representative, and System 1 reads ease and coherence as likelihood. The effect persists even in people who understand the logic: a little voice should shout "read the description," yet the detailed option still feels more probable, the way a visual illusion survives knowing the trick (Hastie and Dawes).

**How to apply.** Be suspicious when a detailed, specific forecast feels more credible than a vague one. More conditions joined together always mean lower probability. Strip a scenario to its components, picture the nested sets (the detailed case lives inside the general one), and ask whether each added detail is buying credibility it has not earned. A related trap is subadditivity: unpacking a whole into vivid named parts inflates the total above 1, so check that complementary probabilities sum to 1 (Hastie and Dawes).

## 7. Regression to the Mean and the Law of Small Numbers

**Regression to the mean.** Extreme outcomes tend to be followed by more average ones for purely statistical reasons, with no cause involved (Kahneman). A pilot who flew brilliantly will usually fly closer to average next time; a stock-picker on a streak will usually cool off.

**Why it works.** Any performance combines skill and luck. When luck runs extreme in one direction, the next observation usually carries less extreme luck and drifts toward the average. The mind refuses to accept "luck" as an explanation and invents a causal story instead. Flight instructors concluded that scolding works and praise backfires, because pilots tended to do worse after praise for a great landing and better after criticism for a bad one; the real driver was regression, and the false lesson favored punishment (Bazerman and Moore).

**How to apply.** Before explaining why something improved or worsened after an extreme, ask whether plain regression already accounts for it. When the starting point was extreme, some of the "improvement" or "decline" is just regression (Hastie and Dawes). When forecasting a repeat performance, shrink your estimate toward the group average in proportion to how much luck is involved.

**The law of small numbers.** Small samples produce extreme results far more often than large ones, yet people treat small-sample findings as reliable signals (Kahneman). Both the best and the worst schools tend to be small, because small samples are simply more variable. Investment in small schools followed the observation that the best schools were small, while the worst schools were equally small; the size was variance rather than a cause.

**Why it works.** System 1 expects a small sample to mirror the population as faithfully as a large one, and it seeks causes for patterns that are actually sampling noise.

**How to apply.** Ask how many observations a claim rests on before you believe it. Treat streaks, hot hands, and "best of" lists as candidates for chance until a large sample says otherwise. Marketing exploits this with "four out of five dentists" claims that hide the sample size (Bazerman and Moore). When the sample is small, withhold conclusions rather than explaining them.

**Misreading chance.** Random processes have no memory and do not self-correct (Bazerman and Moore). Four girls in a row does not make a boy "due"; a coin owes you nothing (the gambler's fallacy). The mind both over-detects order (the basketball "hot hand" is mostly a chance streak in skill-plus-luck data) and expects randomness to look more even than it does. For any independent event, ignore the run that preceded it.

## 8. The Planning Fallacy and the Outside View

**The planning fallacy.** Forecasts of cost and time are built from an optimistic, detail-rich picture of the specific project (the inside view) and systematically run over (Kahneman).

**The outside view (reference-class forecasting).** Set the project aside and ask how similar projects actually turned out (Kahneman, Bazerman and Moore). The inside view generates a smooth best-case scenario and neglects the unknown obstacles that derail almost every comparable effort; the base rate of similar projects captures those obstacles even when you cannot name them in advance. Insiders forecast from the specifics of their own case and skip the failure rate for ventures like theirs (Bazerman and Moore).

**How to apply.** For any plan, find the reference class of comparable past efforts, anchor your forecast on their real distribution of outcomes, then adjust only modestly for what is genuinely special about your case. Treat the project's own plan as a best-case scenario rather than a prediction. Run a premortem: imagine the project has failed and write the story of how.

## 9. Prospect Theory, Loss Aversion, Framing, the Endowment Effect, Mental Accounting

**Prospect theory and loss aversion.** People value gains and losses relative to a reference point rather than valuing final wealth, and losses hurt roughly twice as much as equivalent gains feel good (the canonical **loss aversion ratio is about 2:1**) (Kahneman). Outcomes are weighted by change from the status quo, with diminishing sensitivity. This explains risk-seeking to avoid a sure loss and risk-aversion to lock in a sure gain.

**Why it works.** The mind is built to track changes and threats, so a drop below the current state registers more sharply than an equal rise above it.

**How to apply.** Notice that how a choice is referenced (as a gain or a loss) changes your preference even when the money is identical. Recognize that fear of losing what you have will tempt you into bad gambles to avoid booking a loss.

**Framing effects.** Logically identical descriptions of the same choice produce different decisions (Kahneman). "90 percent survival" is accepted where "10 percent mortality" is refused. A gain framing makes people risk-averse and a loss framing makes them risk-seeking, even with identical outcomes (Bazerman and Moore). To apply: restate every important option in at least two frames (gains and losses, absolute and percentage) and check whether your preference survives. A preference that flips with wording is a preference you do not actually hold (Hastie and Dawes). Watch for the certainty and pseudocertainty effects: moving a risk to exactly zero is overvalued relative to an equal-sized reduction that leaves residual risk, which drives the overselling of insurance and extended warranties.

**The endowment effect.** Once you own something, you demand far more to give it up than you would have paid for it, because losing it is a loss and loss looms large (Kahneman, Bazerman and Moore). Sellers demand far more than buyers will pay for the identical item, and unsold goods pile up.

**Mental accounting.** Money is tracked in separate non-fungible mental buckets, so identical sums get treated differently depending on their label and source (Kahneman, Bazerman and Moore). A government payment labeled a "rebate" got saved as money returning people to baseline; the identical sum labeled a "bonus" reads as surplus and gets spent. Two separate small losses hurt more than one combined larger loss. A related idea is transaction utility: a purchase carries the pleasure or pain of the deal relative to what you think the item should cost, so the same beer costs more from a fancy hotel than a grocery store.

**Joint vs. separate evaluation.** Evaluating options one at a time lets emotionally salient, easy-to-judge attributes dominate; evaluating them side by side surfaces the attributes that actually matter (Bazerman and Moore). The affectively arousing "want" option wins in isolation; the reasoned "should" option wins in comparison. To apply: force important decisions into joint, side-by-side comparison with explicit criteria.

## 10. Overconfidence (Overprecision, Overestimation, Overplacement)

**Overconfidence.** People hold subjective confidence that far outstrips their actual accuracy (Kahneman). It is called the mother of all biases because it blocks correction of every other one (Bazerman and Moore). It decomposes into three distinct failures:

1. **Overprecision.** Your confidence intervals are too narrow, so you are surprised too often.
2. **Overestimation.** You think you will perform better or finish sooner than you will.
3. **Overplacement.** You rate yourself above others more than the math allows (most drivers cannot be above average).

**Why it works.** Confidence is produced by the coherence of the story you can tell (WYSIATI), and a coherent story is easy to build regardless of predictive accuracy. The **illusion of validity** is the unshaken confidence experts feel even after evidence shows their judgments fail.

**How to apply.** Calibrate confidence against track record rather than against how convincing your story feels. Widen your ranges until you would honestly be surprised to be wrong (counters overprecision). Build calibration by tracking how often your "90 percent sure" claims come true (counters overestimation). When comparing yourself to others, remember that everyone runs the same self-flattering reasoning (counters overplacement). In low-validity, noisy domains, distrust expert intuition and lean on simple statistical rules.

## 11. Hindsight Bias and the Bias Blind Spot

**Hindsight bias.** Once you know an outcome, it feels as if you knew it all along, which corrupts your memory of what you predicted and inflates confidence in future predictions (Kahneman, Hastie and Dawes). It unfairly blames decision-makers for bad luck and corrupts learning (Bazerman and Moore). Its companion is **resulting**: judging a decision by how it turned out rather than by the information available at the time (Duke). The companion treats a probabilistic result as if it had been certain.

**How to apply.** Keep a decision journal recording what you predicted and how sure you were, so hindsight cannot rewrite it. Record predictions and confidence levels before outcomes arrive, then score yourself for calibration over time (Hastie and Dawes).

**The bias blind spot.** We detect bias in others while staying blind to our own, and this gap grows larger rather than smaller, with intelligence and numeracy (Duke). Higher numeracy did not reduce polarization on a charged topic; it increased it, because skilled reasoners spin the same data to fit what they already believe. Intelligence serves whatever goal you point it at, including self-deception.

**How to apply.** Accept that you cannot reliably de-bias yourself alone. Recruit external scaffolding: a group that holds you accountable to accuracy catches what you miss (Duke).

## 12. The Scout Stance and Its Tests

**Scout vs. soldier mindset.** Two stances toward evidence (Galef). The **soldier** treats reasoning as defensive combat: beliefs are positions to fortify, criticism is an attack, changing your mind is surrender. The engine is **directionally motivated reasoning**, where unconscious wants steer conclusions. The **scout** treats reasoning as mapmaking: you want an accurate map including the regions you are unsure about, and discovering you were wrong improves the map. The engine is **accuracy-motivated reasoning**. Nobody is a pure scout; the skill is noticing which mode you are in and shifting deliberately toward the scout.

**The three questions.** Your mind silently asks one of three questions of any claim (Galef):
- For conclusions you want: "Can I believe it?" (hunting for any excuse to accept).
- For conclusions you resist: "Must I believe it?" (hunting for any excuse to reject).
- The scout asks: **"Is it true?"**

Motivated reasoning rarely fabricates evidence; it quietly lowers the bar of proof for convenient claims and raises it for inconvenient ones. To apply: when you reach a conclusion, ask which of the three questions you were actually running, and if you accepted something on thin evidence or dismissed something on a technicality, flip the standard and re-check.

### The five thought-experiment tests (Galef)

1. **The selective skeptic test.** If this same evidence supported the other side, would I find it equally credible? Hold the evidence fixed and swap which side it favors to expose a double standard in your skepticism.
2. **The double standard test.** Am I judging this person or group by a standard I would not apply to myself or my own side? Hold the action constant and change the actor. It also catches the reverse: judging yourself far more harshly than you would judge anyone else.
3. **The outsider test.** If I were dropped into this situation fresh, with no ego attached to past choices, what would I do? Intel's leaders asked what a new CEO would do, answered "get out of memory chips," then walked out the door and back in to do it themselves.
4. **The conformity test.** If the people around me no longer held this view, would I still hold it? Imagine the speaker announcing they were only playing devil's advocate, and check whether your agreement survives.
5. **The status quo bias test.** If my current situation were not already the status quo, would I actively choose it now? If no, your attachment is to the status quo rather than to the thing itself.

**The equivalent-bet test.** To elicit your true confidence, ask: would I rather bet on my claim being true, or draw a winning ball from an urn with a known proportion of winners? Adjust the urn's odds until you are indifferent; that proportion is your real confidence (Galef). Calibration means that when you say 70 percent, you are right about 70 percent of the time.

**Hold your identity lightly.** Treat your beliefs as things you happen to hold rather than as pieces of who you are (Galef). Once a belief becomes part of your identity, changing it feels like a betrayal of self and tribe, and every challenge becomes a personal threat. Watch for the warning signs of an identity-fused belief: a flash of defensiveness, using the belief as a banner, describing yourself with it. Let a belief be something you think rather than something you are.

**Supporting scout habits.**
- **Lean into confusion.** When reality violates your expectations, get curious instead of explaining it away. Surprise is information about where your map is incomplete (Galef). Darwin's golden rule: record every observation that contradicts your view immediately, because the mind drops inconvenient facts faster than convenient ones.
- **Watch the de minimis error.** Explaining away conflicting evidence one piece at a time so the contradiction never accumulates. Use the spring metaphor: each time you stretch your theory to fit an awkward fact, note the strain; when the spring has been stretched too often, declare yourself uncertain and consider a new paradigm (Galef).
- **Look for the hidden option C.** When you frame a puzzle as "I'm right" versus "the other guy is right," remember a third explanation neither side has named often holds. The London homeopathic hospital's lower cholera death rate was neither quack luck nor proof of homeopathy; option C was accidental good hygiene and rehydration (Galef).
- **Separate the decision from the estimate.** Separate the decision to pursue a goal from the estimate of its odds. Founders who privately rated their ventures likely to fail still pursued them, which let them prepare honestly while keeping credibility (Galef).

## 13. Decisions as Bets and Fielding Outcomes

**Treat every decision as a bet.** A bet is a choice about an uncertain future where you commit a limited resource (money, time, attention, reputation) against the alternatives you forgo (Duke). Framing a choice as a bet forces the implicit risk into the open and prices the opportunity cost.

**The "Wanna bet?" gut check.** Imagine someone you respect just offered to bet real money against the belief you stated with confidence. We form beliefs lazily: we hear something, accept it as true by default, and only sometimes go back to vet it. "Wanna bet?" forces that neglected third step (Duke). To apply: run the vetting checklist. How do I know this? What is the source quality? How current is it? What are the plausible alternatives? What might the challenger know that I do not?

**Calibrate on a scale rather than a switch.** Express beliefs as a probability or a range rather than as "right" or "wrong" (Duke). All-or-nothing belief makes contrary evidence an identity threat; degrees of confidence let you make small honest adjustments ("I was 58 percent, now I'm 46 percent") that cost nothing to your self-image. A tighter range signals better information; a wider range signals more luck or ignorance. Stating your uncertainty to others makes you more credible rather than less.

**Field outcomes: sort luck from skill.** "Fielding" an outcome means deciding, for each result, how much was caused by your decision and how much by luck (Duke). Self-serving bias fields our wins as skill and our losses as bad luck, and does the reverse for other people. To apply: deliberately consider the opposite attribution from your gut. When you win, ask what luck helped; when you lose, ask what you controlled.

**Outcome-blind analysis.** Present a decision for review up to the choice point and withhold how it turned out, so reviewers cannot reason backward from the result (Duke). Knowing the outcome is itself a conflict of interest that triggers resulting.

**The truthseeking pod and CUDOS norms.** A small group that prizes accuracy over comfort catches what you miss (Duke). The charter borrows Merton's **CUDOS** norms: **Communism** of data (share all relevant detail), **Universalism** (judge a claim on its merits rather than its source), **Disinterestedness** (guard against conflicts of interest), and **Organized Skepticism** (welcome dissent, civilly). Outside the pod, lead with assent and say "and" rather than "but," since people are open to dissent that supplements and defensive toward dissent that negates.

**Mental time travel and Ulysses contracts.** Pull past similar situations and likely future consequences into the present so you are not trapped in in-the-moment thinking (Duke). Ask how this will look in ten minutes, ten months, and ten years. A **Ulysses contract** is a binding decision made in a cool state that constrains your hot future self (predefined exit prices, stop-losses, removed temptations). Run **backcasting** (work backward from a vivid future success) and a **premortem** (work backward from a vivid future failure) to surface obstacles optimism would suppress.

## 14. The Rational Scaffold, the Five Bounds, and Satisficing

**The six-step rational scaffold.** A normative checklist for any consequential choice (Bazerman and Moore): define the problem, identify the criteria, weight the criteria, generate alternatives, rate each alternative on each criterion, then compute the weighted total and pick the highest. Its value is diagnostic: most real errors trace to a skipped or sloppy step (solving the wrong problem, missing criteria, generating too few alternatives). When a choice feels off, walk the six steps and find which one you short-circuited. Watch especially for defining the problem in terms of a pet solution.

**Decision trees and expected utility.** Lay a decision out as a tree: choices, then chance events with their probabilities, then consequences with values (Hastie and Dawes). The expected utility of each option is the sum of probability times value across its consequences; the rational choice maximizes that sum. The discipline of building the tree is most of the benefit, because it forces you to decide what to include, exclude, and quantify, where unaided thinking elaborates one favored branch and ignores the rest.

**Ignore sunk costs.** A sunk cost is money, time, or effort already spent and unrecoverable (Hastie and Dawes). Because it attaches equally to every remaining option, it cannot discriminate between them, so it is irrelevant to what you should do now. Draw the tree starting from today; the sunk amount will not appear on any future branch. "Today is the first day of the rest of your life": probabilities and values attach only to the future.

**Break escalation of commitment.** People throw good money after bad to justify a prior decision (Bazerman and Moore). Judge every continuation decision as if arriving fresh today: "Knowing what I know now, would I start this from zero?" Separate the person who can stop a project from the person who launched it, and pre-commit to kill criteria before you are emotionally invested.

**Aggregate small positive-expected-value bets.** Risk aversion makes people reject individually attractive gambles that would clearly be worth taking in bulk (Bazerman and Moore). A colleague refused one favorable coin flip but happily took a hundred, because aggregation shrinks the risk while keeping the edge. Decide the many small, repeated, favorable risks by expected value and treat them as a portfolio; reserve special caution for rare, large, one-shot decisions.

**Simple linear models over intuition.** For repeated predictive judgments (admissions, diagnosis, risk), a simple linear formula that weights a few valid cues consistently usually beats expert intuition (Hastie and Dawes). The reason is reliability: a formula applies the same weights every time, while a human applies different weights depending on mood, order, and fatigue, adding noise without signal. Even unit-weight models often outperform experts, because most accuracy lives in picking the right variables and combining them consistently.

**The five bounds (Bazerman and Moore).** Human limits organized as five bounds:
1. **Bounded rationality.** We lack the information, memory, and computing power to optimize.
2. **Bounded willpower.** Present bias and undersaving: we favor the immediate over our own long-term interest.
3. **Bounded self-interest.** We genuinely care about others' outcomes and fairness.
4. **Bounded awareness.** We miss obvious, available information outside our focus (inattentional blindness, change blindness, focalism); failing to think about what the other side knows produces the winner's curse.
5. **Bounded ethicality.** Our ethics fail in ways we cannot see, a predictable judgment error rather than a character flaw.

**Satisficing (Simon).** Because we cannot optimize, we search only until we find an option that is good enough and then stop (Bazerman and Moore). Knowing this tells you where to spend more search effort: on high-stakes, infrequent decisions, where good enough is not good enough.

## 15. Lewin's Unfreeze-Change-Refreeze

**Unfreeze-change-refreeze (Lewin).** Lasting debiasing requires three stages, because awareness alone fades and does not change behavior (Bazerman and Moore):
1. **Unfreeze.** See your own errors, usually by committing to an answer and then watching exactly how your judgment broke.
2. **Change.** Supply and practice the new method (base rates, joint evaluation, decision trees, reference classes).
3. **Refreeze.** Lock the new process in so it becomes the default and sticks.

To apply: do not treat reading a bias catalog as the end. Build the correction into a repeated procedure and rehearse it until it replaces the old habit.

## 16. The Factfulness Ten Instincts and Their Counters

Ten dramatic instincts systematically distort our picture of the world toward fear and pessimism; each pairs with one counter-question you can deploy under emotional pressure (Rosling). Educated audiences scored worse than random chance (near 16 percent against a chance baseline of 33 percent), and the errors all leaned the same way, which marks active misconception rather than missing knowledge.

A foundational reframe to install first: replace the rich-vs-poor binary with **four income levels** (Level 1 roughly $1-2/day, Level 2 around $2-8/day, Level 3 around $8-32/day, Level 4 above $32/day). Most of humanity lives on Levels 2 and 3, in the middle.

1. **Gap Instinct** (counter: look for the majority in the middle). The mind splits everything into two opposed camps with a chasm between. Ask where the bulk of cases actually sit; they usually cluster in a middle the framing hides.
2. **Negativity Instinct** (counter: expect bad news, and separate level from trend). We notice negatives more and romanticize the past. Hold that a situation can be bad and improving at once; distinguish the level (still bad) from the direction (getting better).
3. **Straight Line Instinct** (counter: assume the line bends). The mind extends a rising trend as a straight line. Ask what shape the curve actually has (S-bend, slide, hump, doubling) before extrapolating.
4. **Fear Instinct** (counter: calculate risk as exposure times severity). Frightening and dangerous are different properties. Compute **risk = exposure x severity** rather than by how scary something feels. Get calm before you carry the risk assessment.
5. **Size Instinct** (counter: get a comparison and a rate). A lonely number looks big by default. **Compare** it against a relevant total, then **divide** to get a rate (deaths per 1,000, share of a whole). Use the 80/20 rule: find the few items that dominate the sum and start there.
6. **Generalization Instinct** (counter: question your categories). Look for differences within a group and similarities across groups, beware "the majority" (anything above 50 percent), and beware vivid examples standing in for a class. Income predicts daily life better than nationality or culture (Dollar Street).
7. **Destiny Instinct** (counter: slow change is still change). Slow change is invisible on a human attention span. Track gradual change over decades (1 percent a year doubles in 70 years), keep your knowledge fresh, and collect counterexamples of cultural change.
8. **Single Perspective Instinct** (counter: get a toolbox rather than a hammer). One beautiful idea tempts you to explain and solve everything with it. Test your favorite ideas for weaknesses, welcome people who disagree as a resource, and combine perspectives.
9. **Blame Instinct** (counter: look for causes and systems rather than villains). A clear culprit gives a satisfying story and blocks the systemic fix. Resist pointing fingers and crediting single saviors; look for the system that produced the outcome.
10. **Urgency Instinct** (counter: insist on data and take small steps). "Act now, before it is too late" shuts down careful thinking. Take a breath, demand the relevant data, be wary of forecasts dressed as certainties (ask for a range and the assumptions), and favor incremental, testable steps.

**Factfulness as practice and the possibilist stance.** Treat the ten counters as a routine habit rather than a one-time fix; when information triggers a strong emotional reaction, that reaction is the signal to ask which instinct is firing (Rosling). Adopt the **possibilist** stance: neither optimist nor pessimist, committed only to the data and updating as it changes. Prefer few opinions that are right to many that are wrong.

## Quick-Recall Layer (numbers and lists to keep exact)

- **Two conditions for valid intuition:** a regular environment, plus prolonged practice with rapid, clear feedback (Kahneman).
- **Loss aversion ratio:** about 2:1 (losses hurt about twice as much as equal gains feel good) (Kahneman).
- **Overconfidence triad:** overprecision (intervals too narrow), overestimation (you will do better/sooner), overplacement (you rank yourself too high) (Bazerman and Moore).
- **The three questions:** "Is it true?" vs. "Can I believe it?" vs. "Must I believe it?" (Galef).
- **The five thought-experiment tests:** selective skeptic, double standard, outsider, conformity, status quo, plus the equivalent-bet test for true confidence (Galef).
- **CUDOS norms:** Communism of data, Universalism, Disinterestedness, Organized Skepticism (Duke, after Merton).
- **The five bounds:** rationality, willpower, self-interest, awareness, ethicality (Bazerman and Moore).
- **Four income levels:** Level 1 ~$1-2/day, Level 2 ~$2-8, Level 3 ~$8-32, Level 4 >$32 (Rosling).
- **The ten instincts:** Gap, Negativity, Straight Line, Fear, Size, Generalization, Destiny, Single Perspective, Blame, Urgency (Rosling).
- **Lewin's sequence:** unfreeze, change, refreeze (Bazerman and Moore).
- **Risk arithmetic:** risk = exposure x severity (Rosling); calibration = your 70% calls happen ~70% of the time (Galef, Duke).
